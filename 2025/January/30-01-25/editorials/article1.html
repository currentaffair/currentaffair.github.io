India's Act East Policy

Introduction

India’s engagement with Southeast Asia and the broader Indo-Pacific region has undergone a significant transformation since the post-Cold War era. The introduction of the Look East Policy and later the Act East Policy under PM Modi marked a pivotal shift in India's foreign policy priorities, signaling a new era of cooperation and strategic alignment with the Southeast Asian and Indo-Pacific regions.

Historical Context and Evolution

Look East Policy (Post-Cold War Era)

The Look East Policy was introduced by Prime Minister PV Narasimha Rao in the early 1990s, following the end of the Cold War. The policy aimed to rectify India’s neglect of Southeast Asia despite deep cultural and historical ties. Key objectives included enhancing trade, economic development, and forging stronger strategic and cultural connections with ASEAN nations.

Primary objectives of the Look East Policy:

  • Enhance trade and economic development.
  • Forge stronger strategic and cultural connections with ASEAN nations.
  • Draw inspiration from the economic success stories of East Asia.

Initial outcomes were promising, including the reduction of trade barriers and an increase in inbound tourism from Southeast Asia.

Act East Policy (Post-2014)

Under Prime Minister Narendra Modi, the Look East Policy was elevated to the Act East Policy in 2014. The shift from "Look East" to "Act East" signified a more proactive approach to engaging with Southeast Asia and the Indo-Pacific region. The policy now emphasizes action-oriented outcomes, greater integration of Northeast India, and recognition of the Indo-Pacific as a strategic and economic priority.

Key advancements of the Act East Policy include:

  • Greater emphasis on action-oriented outcomes.
  • Integration of Northeast India as a critical fulcrum in policy implementation.
  • Recognition of the Indo-Pacific as a strategic and economic priority.

PM Modi's 3 Cs approach at the 2014 East Asia Summit—Commerce, Culture, and Connectivity—became the cornerstone of this policy, aimed at enhancing economic ties, cultural exchanges, and infrastructural connectivity.

Objectives and Achievements of Act East Policy

Strategic Expansion

The Act East Policy broadened India's focus from ASEAN to include the entire Indo-Pacific region. This expansion strengthened India's ties with regional groupings such as BIMSTEC, the Asia Cooperation Dialogue, and the Indian Ocean Rim Association (IORA). Additionally, defense diplomacy was enhanced with strategic deals like the sale of BrahMos missiles to the Philippines and a military logistics pact with Vietnam.

Economic and Trade Ties

The policy facilitated the reduction of trade barriers, greater economic integration with Southeast Asia through Free Trade Agreements (FTAs), and an increase in foreign investment flows into India.

Cultural and Soft Power Diplomacy

India has utilized its rich cultural heritage to strengthen ties with Southeast Asia. Shared epical traditions like the Ramayana and Mahabharata, as well as Buddhist linkages, have been central to cultural diplomacy. The Ramayan Festival, which involves Southeast Asian nations, is an example of this cultural engagement.

Connectivity

Improved infrastructure development, particularly in Northeast India, has been crucial for facilitating connectivity between India and Southeast Asia. Notable projects like the India-Myanmar-Thailand Trilateral Highway have played a significant role in boosting regional connectivity.

Challenges and Areas of Improvement

Strategic and Economic Challenges

Urbanization and industrialization, particularly in Northeast India, threaten both ecological sustainability and cultural heritage. Additionally, competition from China’s Belt and Road Initiative (BRI) poses a challenge as it offers significant financial incentives to Southeast Asian countries.

Soft Power and Cultural Challenges

China’s active claims over Buddhist heritage challenge India’s cultural narrative in the region. Furthermore, India’s lack of linguistic linkages with Southeast Asia hinders deeper engagement. Few Indian universities offer language courses in key Southeast Asian languages, limiting cultural and educational exchanges.

Connectivity Bottlenecks

Several critical infrastructure projects, such as the Kaladan Multi-Modal Transit Transport Project, have faced delays. Moreover, the underdeveloped transport and trade facilities in Northeast India limit its ability to serve as a gateway to Southeast Asia.

Key Pillars for Strengthening Act East Policy

Cultural Diplomacy

India should organize global conferences focused on the Ramayana and Mahabharata, bringing together countries that share cultural ties to these epics. Developing cultural organizations centered on shared epical heritage, akin to BIMSTEC, can further solidify cultural diplomacy.

Buddhism as a Soft Power Tool

Promoting Buddhist tourism in India, such as at Bodh Gaya and Sarnath, and organizing global conferences on Buddhist studies can enhance India's soft power. Digital platforms that disseminate India’s Buddhist legacy should be developed to broaden the reach of India’s cultural heritage.

Linguistic Integration

Offering undergraduate and postgraduate courses in Southeast Asian languages at central universities, and producing multilingual tourist guides, will be critical for deepening India’s cultural and educational engagement with Southeast Asia.

Economic and Connectivity Initiatives

India must expedite key infrastructure projects such as the India-Myanmar-Thailand Trilateral Highway and the Kaladan Multi-Modal Transit Transport Project to facilitate better economic ties with ASEAN nations. Moreover, improving trade facilitation measures for Northeast India will be vital in engaging with ASEAN markets.

Institutional and Defense Cooperation

Deepening ties with ASEAN countries through security dialogues, military exercises, and intelligence-sharing mechanisms will further enhance India’s role in the Indo-Pacific. Expanding defense exports, particularly leveraging indigenous weapon systems like BrahMos, will help strengthen defense cooperation.

Future Prospects: “Attract East”

India must evolve its Act East policy into an "Attract East" initiative. This will involve making India a hub for Southeast Asian investments and tourism, fostering people-to-people connections through student exchange programs, and promoting India’s image as a global leader in sustainability and green energy.

Conclusion

To fully realize the potential of Act East, India must address the challenges it faces by leveraging its cultural heritage, enhancing connectivity, and fostering economic and linguistic ties. By doing so, India can deepen its engagement with Southeast Asia and emerge as a pivotal player in the Indo-Pacific region.
