India-China Disengagement Agreement: A Temporary Reprieve

Introduction

The October 2024 India-China disengagement agreement offers a temporary reprieve, but it does not address the deeper issues underlying their complex relationship. While the agreement may provide a momentary pause in tensions, it fails to resolve the growing influence and territorial ambitions of China that continue to challenge India’s strategic position. This article analyzes key areas of convergence and friction between India and China, examining the economic, geopolitical, and military dynamics that shape their relationship.

Key Areas of Convergence Between India and China

Trade and Economic Ties

India and China have established strong trade and economic ties, which play a central role in their bilateral relationship. India imports machinery, electronics, and chemicals from China, while exporting raw materials and software services. In FY24, bilateral trade reached US$ 118.40 billion, with India exporting goods worth US$ 16.65 billion and importing goods worth US$ 101.74 billion. These mutual dependencies offer potential for continued cooperation, but also highlight the imbalances that need addressing.

Infrastructure Financing and Connectivity

Both countries collaborate through multilateral platforms like the Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank (AIIB) and the New Development Bank (NDB). These platforms aim to improve regional infrastructure, with initiatives such as the China-Calcutta Service (CCS) launched in July 2024, which enhances trade connectivity and reduces transit times. Continued investments in infrastructure projects will be crucial for regional economic development.

Climate Change and Renewable Energy

Both India and China have shown a commitment to tackling climate change. China, a global leader in solar energy, and India, with a rapidly expanding renewable energy capacity, share common goals in the transition to sustainable energy. Both countries have worked together under international frameworks like the Paris Agreement and within the BASIC group (Brazil, South Africa, India, China) to promote clean energy initiatives and secure financial and technological support for climate action.

Health and Pharmaceutical Cooperation

The COVID-19 pandemic underscored the importance of cooperation between India and China, particularly in the pharmaceutical sector. India’s pharmaceutical industry, which relies heavily on Chinese raw materials, collaborates with China to secure the supply of Active Pharmaceutical Ingredients (APIs) and vaccines. This partnership is critical for ensuring regional and global health security.

Tourism and People-to-People Exchanges

Tourism and cultural exchanges have gained momentum in strengthening India-China relations. Shared Buddhist heritage and educational collaborations have increased mutual understanding. In December 2024, both nations agreed to resume the Kailash Mansarovar Yatra and other exchanges that foster goodwill and people-to-people ties.

Key Areas of Friction Between India and China

Border Disputes and Military Standoffs

The unresolved boundary disputes along the Line of Actual Control (LAC) remain a central point of tension between India and China. Military standoffs, such as the Galwan Valley clash in 2020, have escalated the situation, eroding trust and complicating bilateral relations. Despite ongoing diplomatic efforts, significant friction persists over disengagement and military activities in disputed areas.

Trade Imbalance and Economic Concerns

India's trade imbalance with China remains a major issue, with India heavily reliant on Chinese imports, particularly in machinery and electronics. In FY24, the trade deficit reached US$ 85 billion. Additionally, India's concerns include the dumping of low-cost Chinese products, which adversely affect domestic industries and hinder economic growth. Restrictions on Chinese investments in sectors like telecom and fintech further contribute to economic tensions.

China’s Support for Pakistan

China’s close relationship with Pakistan, particularly regarding the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC), is a major source of friction for India. The CPEC passes through Pakistan-occupied Kashmir (PoK), which India considers a violation of its sovereignty. Furthermore, China's opposition to India’s efforts to designate Pakistan-based terrorists as global threats has strained relations.

Conclusion

In conclusion, India’s path forward in its relationship with China requires a delicate balance between cooperation and strategic caution. The India-China disengagement agreement is a temporary solution, but deeper issues such as territorial disputes, economic imbalances, and geopolitical rivalry continue to pose challenges. India must continue to strengthen its internal capabilities, diversify its supply chains, and explore new partnerships while avoiding unnecessary conflict with China.
