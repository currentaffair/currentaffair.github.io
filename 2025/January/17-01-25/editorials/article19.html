Evolution of Ambulance Services in India

Introduction

An increase in chronic diseases and road accidents has spurred the demand for emergency services and hospitalisation. Ambulance services are an important link for transporting patients who require emergency care.

Evolution of Ambulance Services in India

Early History

For a long time, ambulances were available only at a hospital. However, in the 1980s, non-governmental organizations began stepping in, particularly in southern India, to provide voluntary and free ambulance services.

Government Initiatives

State governments introduced services targeting women of reproductive age and children under five years. These initiatives were primarily aimed at improving healthcare access and reducing infant mortality rates.

National Rural Health Mission (NRHM)

The NRHM played a key role in the introduction of public-private partnership (PPP) ambulance models. The mission focused on increasing institutional deliveries, particularly in urban areas, thereby improving maternal and child healthcare.

Private Sector Involvement

In recent years, private ambulance services have seen significant growth, primarily driven by corporate hospitals and standalone companies. For instance, Blinkit launched a “10-minute” ambulance service in Gurugram, providing rapid access to emergency services.

Current Landscape of Ambulance Services

Market Overview

The Indian ambulance market was valued at $1.5 billion in 2022, with expectations of over 5% compound annual growth rate (CAGR) from 2024-2028. However, of the 17,495 ambulances in operation, only 3,441 have Advanced Life Support (ALS) units.

Regional Disparities

Western and southern states, such as Maharashtra, Gujarat, Rajasthan, and Goa, dominate the market. In contrast, northeastern states face significant shortages, leading to challenges in accessibility and service quality.

Types of Ambulances

Different types of ambulances cater to specific needs:

Type Description
Patient Transport Basic transport with no medical support
Basic Life Support (BLS) CPR, oxygen therapy, and basic medical assistance
Advanced Life Support (ALS) Equipped with cardiac monitors, defibrillators, and ventilators
Specialised Services Air ambulances and ICU ambulances for long-distance transport and critical care during transit

Infrastructure and Workforce Challenges

According to a NITI Aayog study, 88% of hospitals have in-house ambulances, but only 3% employ Emergency Medical Technicians (EMTs). Furthermore, only 12% of hospitals offer specialized care during transportation, highlighting a gap in the healthcare workforce.

Challenges

Operational Barriers

Traffic congestion and poor road planning significantly hinder emergency response times. Moreover, the regulatory landscape remains fragmented, with central and state-level inconsistencies and bureaucratic hurdles causing delays and inefficiencies.

Quality and Regulation

A lack of standardised protocols for ambulance services has resulted in deficiencies in trained staff, equipment availability, and accountability mechanisms. The absence of a unified pan-India toll-free number for emergency services exacerbates the issue.

Private Sector Concerns

Unregulated growth in the private sector has led to disparities in service quality. High costs limit accessibility for low-income populations, making emergency healthcare less equitable.

Way Forward: Recommendations

Policy Interventions

It is crucial to develop standard protocols for general and specialised ambulance services and establish a unified toll-free emergency number across the country. This will streamline emergency response systems and improve accessibility.

Skilling and Certification

Proper training and certification of paramedics and EMTs are essential for efficient emergency care delivery. Ensuring a skilled workforce will enhance the quality of services provided.

Infrastructure Development

Improving road connectivity and traffic management is necessary for faster ambulance transit. Addressing infrastructure bottlenecks will ensure timely access to emergency care.

Regulatory Measures

Streamlining central and state-level regulations will reduce inconsistencies. Introducing stringent licensing requirements will ensure better service quality and accountability.

Strengthening Public-Private Partnerships

Encouraging collaborations between government, private entities, and NGOs will help create affordable and accessible ambulance services, particularly for underserved regions.

Conclusion

Given the increased longevity and growing health emergencies, the provision of efficient and modern ambulance services will remain a critical area for private investment. Publicly-funded services, public-private partnerships, and civil society-sponsored services play a vital role in offsetting the high costs of private services and ensuring equitable access.
