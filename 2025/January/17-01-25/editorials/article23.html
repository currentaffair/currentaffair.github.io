India’s Engagement with the Taliban: Geopolitical and Humanitarian Dimensions

Introduction

Indian Foreign Secretary Vikram Misri’s meeting with the Taliban’s acting foreign minister Amir Khan Muttaqi in Dubai on January 8 marked a significant elevation in the level of dialogue. Despite this, India, like the rest of the international community, has not accorded formal recognition to the Taliban regime. This article explores India’s evolving engagement with the Taliban, the geopolitical context, the opportunities and challenges that lie ahead, and how India can navigate this complex situation.

India’s Engagement with the Taliban

Historical Context

India has not formally recognized the Taliban regime since their return to power in August 2021. Despite this, India has maintained functional communication channels with the Taliban regime. India’s primary focus has been to address humanitarian concerns and support its strategic interests in Afghanistan.

Recent Developments

The meeting in Dubai is India’s highest-level engagement with the Taliban since their return to power. India has committed to extending material support in the health sector and humanitarian assistance to refugees. The Indian government has emphasized its willingness to assist Afghanistan in rebuilding critical infrastructure, while the Taliban remains unrecognized by the international community.

Practical Measures

India reinstated a technical team to its embassy in Kabul in 2022. Humanitarian aid sent by India includes medicines, vaccines, food supplies, and other essential items. The Union Budget for 2024-25 has allocated Rs 200 crore for assistance to Afghanistan, highlighting India’s commitment to providing aid despite the lack of formal recognition.

Geopolitical Context

Pakistan’s Role and Challenges

Pakistan has long sought “strategic depth” in Afghanistan, but now faces a resurgent Taliban that threatens its western flank. The Taliban’s refusal to recognize the Durand Line and its support for Tehrik-e-Taliban Pakistan (TTP) has escalated tensions between Pakistan and the Taliban. Pakistan’s airstrikes against TTP camps have been met with international condemnation, including from India, further complicating the situation.

The Taliban’s Geopolitical Position

The Taliban remains unrecognized by the international community, including the UN Credentials Committee. They face accusations of human rights violations and links with terrorist organizations like al-Qaeda. Despite this, the Taliban’s control over Afghanistan is secure, and they continue to seek international recognition.

Broader Implications

The rise of regional terrorist networks, including the Islamic State (Khorasan Province), further complicates the Afghan security scenario. Comparisons are drawn with the rehabilitation of Syria’s Hayat Tahrir al-Sham (HTS), raising questions about the Taliban’s prospects for international legitimacy.

Opportunities for India

Strengthening Humanitarian Support

Enhanced humanitarian aid, particularly for refugees expelled by Pakistan, could improve India’s standing in Afghanistan. Leveraging the Chabahar Port for trade and assistance delivery in collaboration with Iran could help India expand its regional influence.

Promoting Soft Power

India’s involvement in promoting cricket in Afghanistan, including through the IPL, serves as a tool for youth engagement and fostering goodwill. This cultural diplomacy helps India build bridges in Afghanistan despite political complexities.

Resuming Development Projects

Reviving infrastructure and development initiatives could strengthen traditional ties between India and Afghanistan, enhancing India’s influence in the region.

Act West Policy Integration

Afghanistan’s strategic location could play a crucial role in India’s broader “Act West” policy, complementing its goals to enhance regional connectivity and counterbalance China’s growing influence in South Asia.

Challenges for India

Security Concerns

The Taliban’s ties with terrorist groups like al-Qaeda and the TTP pose a direct threat to regional stability. Afghanistan’s volatile political and security environment remains a significant challenge for India’s engagement efforts.

Lack of International Recognition

The absence of a global consensus on recognizing the Taliban limits India’s engagement options. Balancing relations with the Taliban while not alienating other global powers, such as the US and EU, is a diplomatic tightrope that India must navigate.

Conclusion and Way Forward

The Taliban is here to stay, and India should enhance humanitarian assistance and resume development projects in Afghanistan. By capitalizing on its traditional friendship with Afghanistan, there is scope for the country to figure even more prominently in India’s Act West policy. Navigating the complexities of Afghanistan’s political and security environment will require careful diplomacy and a balance between humanitarian assistance, security concerns, and regional ambitions.
