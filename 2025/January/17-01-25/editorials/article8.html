Reducing Human-Elephant Conflict in Namibia’s Northern Highlands

Introduction

Human-elephant conflict in Namibia’s Northern Highlands has been a significant issue, exacerbated by competition for limited water resources. A recent study, published in the Journal of Environment & Development on January 7, 2025, investigates the role of upgrading water points and creating separate water sources for elephants as a potential solution to this problem.

Insights and Findings

Upgrading water points with solar-powered pumps, protective walls, and concrete dams has proven to be an effective strategy in reducing human-elephant conflicts. By ensuring that both humans and elephants have reliable access to water, the risk of elephants damaging infrastructure or crops is minimized.

Water Point Upgrades to Reduce Conflict

The introduction of solar-powered systems and protective measures like concrete dams has drastically reduced the likelihood of elephants coming into close contact with human settlements. This approach ensures that elephants can access water without the need to encroach on village lands, thus reducing conflict.

Human-Elephant Conflict Data

From 2012 to 2021, the study found no significant long-term increase in human-elephant conflicts at water points. However, a spike in incidents occurred during 2020-2021, coinciding with the establishment of vegetable gardens after a severe drought (2018-2019). These new gardens attracted elephants, increasing crop damage and incidents of conflict.

Despite a rise in crop damage, the study highlights that infrastructure damage, such as to pipes and tanks, remains a more pressing issue, leading to disruptions in water supply for both communities and wildlife.

Challenges of Repair and Maintenance

The destruction of water infrastructure often leaves villages without a reliable water supply, which is a significant concern in rural, remote areas. Repairing these systems is both costly and difficult, exacerbating the logistical challenges faced by these communities.

Role of Solar-powered Water Systems

The transition from diesel-powered pumps to solar-powered systems has been a game-changer. Supported by the Namibian government, these systems have ensured consistent water availability, significantly reducing the likelihood of elephants damaging water infrastructure in search of water.

Separate Water Dams for Elephants

One proposed solution to further reduce human-elephant conflict is the construction of separate water dams for elephants. These dams would be located away from human settlements, creating designated spaces for elephants to drink without coming into contact with people. This strategy is in line with Namibia’s National Elephant Conservation and Management Plan.

Impact on Local Communities

The study reveals that the impact of human-elephant incidents is felt most severely by local communities. While crop damage from elephants is a growing concern, it is the destruction of water infrastructure that has long-term consequences, disrupting daily life for villagers.

Challenges from Climate Change

Climate change is expected to further intensify competition for water resources in Namibia’s Northern Highlands. The region’s reliance on seasonal rainfall and the potential reduction in water availability make it essential to upgrade and expand water infrastructure to prevent escalating human-wildlife conflicts.

Community Conservation Efforts

The Namibia Community Conservation Programme, established in the 1990s, has been a success in promoting local involvement in wildlife conservation. However, increasing crop damage is threatening local support for elephant conservation. It is crucial to address the economic challenges farmers face in order to maintain this support.

Recommendations for Future Conservation

The study recommends pilot projects to test protective measures, such as fencing or deterrents, to prevent further crop damage. This approach would balance the needs of local farmers with the preservation of elephant populations.

Expansion of Water Point Upgrades

Further investment in the upgrading of water systems across the region is essential to ensure the long-term sustainability of both human and elephant water sources. This would reduce conflicts and safeguard vital resources for both communities and wildlife.

Community Support and Education

Ongoing education and community engagement efforts are key to maintaining support for conservation programs. Addressing the challenges faced by farmers and assisting them in managing human-wildlife conflict will be crucial for the success of Namibia’s conservation initiatives.

Conclusion

In conclusion, the study demonstrates that upgrading water points has been an effective strategy in reducing human-elephant conflict. However, continued investment in water infrastructure and the creation of separate water sources for elephants are essential for sustainable conservation efforts in Namibia’s Northern Highlands.
