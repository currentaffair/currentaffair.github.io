Impact of Interlinking Rivers in India

Introduction

On December 25, 2024, Prime Minister Narendra Modi laid the foundation stone for the Ken-Betwa River Link Project, which aims to address water scarcity in the Bundelkhand region of Uttar Pradesh and Madhya Pradesh. The project involves the construction of a dam located within the Panna Tiger Reserve, raising concerns about the potential submergence of the region. This river linking project seeks to transfer water from the surplus Ken River to the drought-prone Betwa River, addressing the water deficit in Bundelkhand. This is despite the region having 58% forest cover in 1938.

A Misplaced Model

The river linking project, costing approximately ₹45,000 crore, has been launched despite objections from experts, including an empowered committee appointed by the Supreme Court of India. These concerns highlight the circumvention of laws meant to regulate hydroelectric power projects and the increasing reliance on technological fixes for water scarcity. The political class has been touting the idea of interlinking rivers for inter-basin water transfer as a geoengineering solution, which raises fundamental concerns about sustainability.

Concept of Inter-Basin Transfer

Inter-basin water transfer was first proposed 130 years ago by Sir Arthur Cotton, known for his work in the Godavari and Krishna river valleys. Visionary engineers like M. Visvesvaraya, K.L. Rao, and Captain Dinshaw J. Dastur expanded this concept over the years. Initially termed the 'National Water Grid,' it eventually became known as the 'River-Interlinking Project.' The project aimed to transfer surplus water from the Ganga and Brahmaputra rivers to water-deficient regions of India. While this project has garnered attention, it overlooks critical ecological and environmental concerns.

National Water Development Agency (NWDA)

Established in 1982, the NWDA has been tasked with conducting surveys and preparing feasibility reports for proposed river links. The agency identified 30 potential links under the National Perspective Plan, estimating a cost of ₹5.5 lakh crore. However, this estimate does not account for the environmental, social, and operational costs associated with such large-scale projects. The burden of these costs will ultimately fall on taxpayers, further compounding the economic challenges facing India.

Environmental Concerns

The idea of interlinking rivers to connect surplus rivers with water-deficient ones overlooks the significant environmental consequences. The uncontrolled disruption of natural hydrographic systems will result in the destruction of ecological niches, with long-term repercussions for both the environment and society. These changes could lead to the eventual degradation of deltaic regions and the demise of vital ecosystems that depend on free-flowing rivers.

Climate Change Considerations

As India faces the challenge of climate change, the nation must consider its impact on rainfall patterns and river flow. These changes could further exacerbate water scarcity, making the proposed river interlinking projects less effective. It is unclear how these large-scale geoengineering solutions align with India’s commitment to mitigating human-induced climate change.

The Eco-Services of Rivers

Rivers play a vital role in providing eco-services, such as maintaining land fertility through floodwater, promoting groundwater recharge, and sustaining biodiversity. However, the current discourse surrounding water diversion disregards these vital ecological functions. By viewing rivers solely as surplus water carriers, policymakers overlook the long-term environmental consequences of disrupting the natural flow of rivers.

Impact of Water Diversion on Deltaic Regions

By diverting river water, deltaic regions could suffer from a loss of freshwater, which helps maintain the balance between freshwater and saltwater in these ecosystems. The resulting damage to the deltaic ecosystem is evident in regions such as the Indus Delta, where human interventions have caused irreparable damage. Similarly, the Narmada River's downstream areas, after the completion of the Sardar Sarovar dam, provide a modern example of this environmental degradation.

Global Examples of River Channelization

The environmental disasters resulting from river channelization in the U.S. and Central Asia serve as critical lessons for India. In Florida, the Kissimmee River's channelization, intended to mitigate flooding, led to the loss of wetlands. The Aral Sea, once one of the largest lakes in the world, became a desert due to Soviet-era irrigation projects that diverted the rivers that fed it. These examples highlight the risks associated with altering natural water systems on a large scale.
