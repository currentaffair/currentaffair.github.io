Analysis of Section 152 of the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS)

Introduction

The Rajasthan High Court, in Tejender Pal Singh v. State of Rajasthan (2024), cautioned against the misuse of Section 152 of the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS), a legal provision that criminalizes actions endangering India's sovereignty, unity, and integrity. Section 152, modeled after the sedition laws of the past, has raised concerns about its potential to stifle free speech and its vagueness. This article explores the problems associated with this law, judicial precedents, and suggests the way forward for its effective and responsible enforcement.

Issues with Section 152

1. Vagueness in Definition: The terms “sovereignty, unity, and integrity of India” in Section 152 BNS are not clearly defined. This lack of clarity gives authorities wide discretion to interpret the law expansively, which could lead to the suppression of legitimate dissent. This vagueness makes it easier to misuse the law against individuals expressing opposition to government policies.

2. Lower Threshold for Offence: The provision states that any individual who "knowingly" engages in actions that undermine India’s sovereignty can be prosecuted. This broad wording means that even minor acts, such as sharing a social media post, could lead to criminal prosecution, regardless of intent. Section 152 is cognizable and non-bailable, posing a risk for wrongful arrests and detention.

It is important to recognize that the law applies without the need to establish a direct causal link between speech and its consequences. This could create a chilling effect on free speech, especially when it is difficult to prove intent.

Judicial Precedents on Free Speech

The courts have historically drawn lines between speech that undermines national security and speech that simply criticizes the government. Some key cases include:

  • Balwant Singh v. State of Punjab (1995): The Supreme Court ruled that casual sloganeering that does not incite any further action is not punishable under sedition laws.
  • Javed Ahmad Hazam v. State of Maharashtra (2024): The Court emphasized that the impact of words should be judged from the perspective of reasonable individuals, rather than those who are overly sensitive.
  • Kedar Nath Singh v. State of Bihar (1962): The Supreme Court ruled that criticism of government policies, no matter how strong, cannot be equated with disloyalty to the nation.

Way Forward

1. Judicial Guidelines: The Supreme Court should issue clear guidelines on the interpretation of Section 152, similar to the D.K. Basu case, which provided guidelines for arrests. These guidelines should ensure that only speech that has a direct causal link to actions threatening national security is criminalized.

2. Safeguards Against Misuse: There should be checks in place to prevent the misuse of the law, ensuring that arrests and prosecutions are made only after establishing a genuine threat to India’s sovereignty or unity. Additionally, the government should introduce awareness programs to educate citizens about their rights under the law.

The importance of free speech cannot be overstated. In today’s age of social media, where opinions are shared rapidly, safeguarding the freedom of expression is vital for the growth of a democratic society.

Conclusion

Section 152 of the BNS poses significant challenges to the protection of free speech. While aimed at protecting India’s sovereignty and integrity, its vague provisions and low threshold for prosecution could lead to the stifling of legitimate political discourse. Clear guidelines and stronger safeguards are necessary to ensure that the law is not misused to suppress free speech. By promoting greater awareness and accountability, the government can strike a balance between national security and individual freedoms.
