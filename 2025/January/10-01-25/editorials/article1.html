Eklavya Model Residential Schools (EMRS): Challenges and Solutions

Introduction

The Eklavya Model Residential Schools (EMRS) scheme was introduced with the aim of providing quality education to Scheduled Tribe (ST) students in remote areas, particularly from Class VI to XII. Despite significant budgetary allocations and the establishment of these schools, a third of the 716 approved EMRS remain non-functional. The challenges faced by these schools, especially regarding infrastructure, teacher shortages, and dropout rates, point to persistent gaps in ensuring universal access to education for tribal communities, particularly those from the Particularly Vulnerable Tribal Groups (PVTGs).

Key Challenges Facing EMRS

1. Infrastructure Gaps

One of the significant issues faced by the EMRS is the inadequate infrastructure in many schools. Around 240 schools are non-functional primarily due to land acquisition problems, lack of hostels, and the absence of basic facilities like drinking water, electricity, and functional toilets. Furthermore, only about 53.9% of schools have internet access, which limits the use of modern learning tools that are essential for todayâ€™s education system.

2. Teacher Shortages and Retention Issues

Another critical challenge for the EMRS is the shortage of qualified teachers, especially subject specialists. Many teachers are appointed on deputation or on a contractual basis, leading to an inconsistent quality of education. The lack of local recruitment adds another layer of complexity, as teachers often struggle to communicate with students in their native tribal languages, creating a significant language barrier.

3. High Dropout Rates Among PVTGs

High dropout rates among students, particularly from PVTGs, have been observed. From Class VI to Class XII, enrolment numbers drop significantly, with only half of the students enrolled in Class VI continuing to Class XII. The enrolment rates of PVTGs in states like Madhya Pradesh, Gujarat, and Chhattisgarh fall well below the 5% quota allocated for these groups, with many tribal children forced into daily wage work to support their families instead of pursuing education.

Statistical Insight: Only 53.9% of EMRS have internet access.

4. Socio-Economic Barriers

PVTGs often face extreme poverty, landlessness, and the need to migrate for work, making regular school attendance a challenge. Illiterate parents may not fully understand the importance of education, which leads to lower enrolment rates and greater dropout rates. A lack of awareness about educational opportunities exacerbates the problem.

Reasons Behind Dropout Rates

1. Economic Pressures

Poverty and seasonal migration are key factors that disrupt the education of tribal children. These economic pressures force children to take up daily wage work, which interrupts their academic continuity. The seasonal nature of migration means that many students miss out on crucial parts of the academic year.

2. Language Barriers

Another reason for the high dropout rates is the language barrier. The medium of instruction in many schools is bilingual, using both regional languages and English, making it difficult for tribal students to fully comprehend lessons. Many teachers also struggle to speak tribal dialects, further contributing to the communication gap.

3. Entrance Exam Challenges

Admission to EMRS is based on competitive entrance exams, which many PVTG students find difficult to clear due to a lack of preparatory resources. This gives students from more privileged backgrounds an advantage, exacerbating educational inequalities.

Policy and Governance Gaps

1. Ineffective Implementation of PVTG Quota

The PVTG quota for EMRS, which is meant to ensure that tribal groups have access to education, is underutilized in many states. Despite the policy, states like Madhya Pradesh, Gujarat, and Chhattisgarh have not fully implemented the 5% quota, leading to inequitable access to education for these vulnerable groups.

2. Lack of Monitoring and Accountability

Although the National Educational Society for Tribal Students (NESTS) was set up in 2019 to oversee operations of the EMRS, its capacity is limited. Inconsistent monitoring and reporting by states contribute to the failure to track school performance and enrolment data effectively.

3. Budget Utilization and Allocation Issues

Despite significant increases in budget allocations for tribal welfare, funds remain underutilized due to poor execution. Programs like the Tribal Sub-Plan (TSP) have not effectively addressed the gaps in secondary and higher secondary education, which further exacerbates the problem.

Potential Solutions

1. Improving Infrastructure

Before establishing new schools, it is essential to focus on improving the infrastructure of existing schools. Ensuring the availability of clean drinking water, electricity, and internet access will significantly enhance the learning environment in EMRS.

2. Local Teacher Recruitment

Recruiting teachers from local communities can help overcome language barriers. Offering permanent positions and incentives for teachers to work in remote areas will improve teacher retention and contribute to a more stable education system.

3. Revising the Admission Process

The entrance exam process should be revised to be more inclusive, particularly for PVTG students and first-generation learners. Providing preparatory support can also help these students perform better in competitive exams.

4. Strengthening Awareness and Community Support

Community outreach programs can help raise awareness about the importance of education among tribal families. Parent-teacher engagement is also crucial to reduce dropout rates and ensure that students remain committed to their studies.

Conclusion

The Eklavya Model Schools have great potential to address the educational disparities faced by tribal communities. However, the issues of infrastructure, teacher shortages, and high dropout rates highlight the need for systemic reforms in policy implementation and governance. Addressing these challenges, including language barriers, economic pressures, and social challenges, will make these schools more accessible and effective for tribal children, especially those from PVTGs. By focusing on localized solutions, community involvement, and better governance, India can move closer to its NEP 2030 goals and SDG targets for inclusive education.
