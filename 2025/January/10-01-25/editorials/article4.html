Section 152 BNS and Free Speech: Legal Concerns and Way Forward

Introduction

The recent case of Tejender Pal Singh v. State of Rajasthan (2024) highlights the growing concerns around the misuse of laws like Section 152 of the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS), a statute criminalizing activities that endanger India’s sovereignty, unity, and integrity. With the judiciary’s increasing scrutiny over laws that affect free speech and civil liberties, the case underscores the need for clear and balanced legal frameworks. Section 152, while designed to protect national security, raises questions about the potential for abuse and overreach, especially when vagueness in the law allows authorities to act with greater discretion.

Background of Section 152 BNS

Section 152 BNS criminalizes acts that threaten India’s sovereignty and integrity, including promoting armed rebellion or engaging in subversive activities. This law, however, has been criticized for its broad and vague definitions, leading to its potential misuse against legitimate expressions of dissent. The Supreme Court has addressed similar concerns in the past with other laws like Section 124A of the Indian Penal Code, which criminalizes sedition. Despite the suspension of sedition cases by the Supreme Court in 2022, Section 152 BNS presents a broader challenge to free speech, especially in today’s social media age.

Key Issues with Section 152 BNS

One of the central concerns surrounding Section 152 is its vagueness. The terms "sovereignty, unity, and integrity" are not clearly defined, which allows for subjective interpretation by authorities. This ambiguity may lead to the criminalization of actions and speech that do not directly incite violence or rebellion but are nonetheless considered threatening based on the discretion of law enforcement. Moreover, the law’s low threshold for prosecution—where even sharing a social media post without the intent to incite violence can lead to arrest—further exacerbates the risk of wrongful prosecutions.

Important Point: Between 2015 and 2020, there were 548 arrests under Section 124A (sedition) but only 12 convictions, highlighting the potential for misuse in sedition-related cases, a concern amplified by Section 152 BNS's broader scope.

Judicial Precedents and Free Speech

The Indian judiciary has repeatedly emphasized the importance of free speech as a fundamental right, but also recognizes the need for limits when speech threatens national security. Past judgments, like Balwant Singh v. State of Punjab (1995) and Javed Ahmad Hazam v. State of Maharashtra (2024), have distinguished between casual expressions that do not pose a direct threat and speech that leads to actual harm or violence. However, Section 152 BNS’s ambiguity challenges these distinctions, as the law allows for prosecution even in the absence of a direct causal link between speech and any consequential harm.

Key Case Law: In Kedar Nath Singh v. State of Bihar (1962), the Supreme Court clarified that strong criticism of government policies should not be treated as disloyalty or sedition, further supporting the need for clearer definitions in Section 152 BNS.

Way Forward and Recommendations

In light of the concerns raised by the Rajasthan High Court and legal experts, it is imperative for the Supreme Court to issue clear guidelines on the interpretation of Section 152 BNS, similar to the D.K. Basu case regarding arrests. Enforcement agencies must be mandated to establish a direct causal nexus between speech and its harmful consequences before proceeding with criminal action. Furthermore, safeguards against misuse—such as ensuring that arrests are made only when there is clear evidence of a threat—should be implemented.

Key Recommendation: A balanced approach should be taken, where national security is protected while safeguarding individual freedoms, especially in the digital space where ideas can spread rapidly.

Conclusion

The concerns over Section 152 BNS echo the broader debate on the limits of free speech in a democracy. While the protection of India’s sovereignty is essential, laws like Section 152 must be carefully crafted to prevent overreach. It is crucial for the judiciary to continue its role in ensuring that laws are not misused, and that the right to free speech is upheld as a cornerstone of democracy. The judiciary and policymakers should collaborate to balance security concerns with civil liberties, thereby ensuring that citizens’ voices are not silenced under the guise of national interest.
