Marine Fungi

About Marine Fungi

Marine fungi constitute 5% of ocean biomass and are vital for ocean ecosystems, found from rocky shores to deep waters. They perform key roles like decomposition, symbiosis, and producing bioactive compounds.

Types of Marine Fungi

  • Obligate Marine Fungi: Exclusively live in marine environments.
  • Facultative Marine Fungi: Evolved from terrestrial habitats but can survive in marine settings.

Survival Strategies

Marine fungi, such as Paradendryphiella salina, adapt to feast-famine conditions by changing their cell forms to better handle resources, like producing enzymes from bacteria to digest their host.

Ecological Importance

Marine fungi are crucial for nutrient cycling and maintaining ecosystem stability. Lichens, a symbiotic relationship between fungi and algae, also contribute significantly to marine ecosystems.

Fungi Characteristics

  • Fungi are eukaryotic and heterotrophic, feeding on other organisms, often functioning as saprophytes or parasites.
  • Reproduce sexually or asexually via spores.
  • Classified as a distinct multicellular eukaryotic kingdom by R.H. Whittaker.
  • While beneficial for medicine and industry, they can also produce toxic mycotoxins and cause diseases.
