Freshwater Species Threat

Over 4,000 species out of 23,496 assessed species are threatened, including decapod crustaceans, fishes, and odonates. These species are at high risk of extinction, with 89 confirmed extinctions since 1500. Key geographic hotspots include Lake Victoria, Lake Titicaca, Sri Lanka’s wet zone, and the Western Ghats, India.

Key Findings

Several causes contribute to freshwater species decline, such as agriculture, invasive species, dams, and climate change. Pollution, overharvesting, and water extraction are major threats, while climate change exacerbates the effects of these threats. Regional extinctions have been reported, particularly in the United States, Mexico, and the Philippines.

Impact on Ecosystem Services

Freshwater ecosystems, though covering less than 1% of Earth’s surface, support 10% of all species globally, including one-third of vertebrates and half of all fish species. They also provide essential ecosystem services like nutrient cycling, flood control, and climate change mitigation.

Conservation Urgency

The report stresses urgent action and the need for integrated water management practices and species-specific conservation efforts to prevent further biodiversity loss. Upcoming events, like the 2025 IUCN World Conservation Congress, aim to set conservation targets aligned with global biodiversity frameworks.
