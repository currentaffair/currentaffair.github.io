Accelerated and Extended Degree Programmes in Indian Education

Introduction

The latest guidelines of the University Grants Commission (UGC) herald a transformative shift in higher education in India. Among other reforms, the UGC has introduced an accelerated degree programme and an extended degree programme for undergraduate students, which will allow them to complete their degrees either earlier or later than the standard duration.

Advantages and Challenges

The introduction of these degree formats aims to create a globally competitive educational framework, offering students greater autonomy and flexibility in shaping their academic trajectory. This shift is revolutionary in India's traditionally rigid education system.

Advantages of Accelerated and Extended Degree Programmes

Accelerated Degree Programmes are advantageous for students eager to join the workforce quickly, gain professional experience early, or reduce tuition costs. On the other hand, Extended Degree Programmes provide students with the freedom to explore diverse subjects, engage in research, and balance academic pursuits with personal life.

These programmes align with the National Education Policy (NEP), 2020, which advocates for a multidisciplinary approach to learning, producing graduates equipped with a range of soft skills, creative abilities, and innovative thinking.

Enhancing Student Mobility and Autonomy

The structural alignment of undergraduate education with global standards aims to foster greater student mobility, both domestically and internationally. Furthermore, the flexible credit system provides students the opportunity to progress at their own pace, tailoring their academic experience to individual needs, interests, and career aspirations.

Challenges and Concerns

Despite the advantages, questions remain regarding the depth and rigor of accelerated programmes. There is concern that compressing the curriculum could lead to a superficial understanding of core concepts, potentially undermining educational outcomes. Conversely, the extended programme may reduce academic urgency, causing delays in degree completion and diminishing its value.

Adapting to the New Structure

Transitioning to accelerated and extended formats presents challenges for disciplines like engineering, which demand both theoretical and practical mastery. The risk lies in oversimplifying technical learning, compromising essential hands-on experiences.

Practical Issues

These reforms require substantial restructuring of curricula, teaching methods, and university systems, which may be challenging for institutions already facing resource constraints. Additionally, the shift towards digitalisation in education could exacerbate the digital divide, limiting access for certain student populations.

Need for Robust Administrative Systems

The introduction of these new degree formats requires efficient tracking of student progress, managing credit transfers, and ensuring proper academic recognition. The complexities of these administrative systems could be daunting for universities, and the equity implications may affect underprivileged students who struggle to navigate the system.

Faculty Adaptation Challenges

Faculty members must undergo professional development to adapt to the demands of flexible, interdisciplinary curricula. The success of these reforms depends not only on students' ability to adjust but also on educators' capacity to provide the necessary support.

Conclusion

If these challenges are addressed with strategic planning, adequate investment, and timely recruitment of faculty and staff, these reforms could lay the foundation for a dynamic higher education system better aligned with market needs. This transformation could help India realise its vision of a Viksit Bharat by 2047.
