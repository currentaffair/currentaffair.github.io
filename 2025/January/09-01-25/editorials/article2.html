Ken-Betwa River Link Project: Impacts, Challenges, and Lessons

Introduction

On December 25, 2024, Prime Minister Narendra Modi laid the foundation for the ambitious Ken-Betwa River Link Project. This project is poised to address the water scarcity issues that have long plagued the Bundelkhand region, located across the states of Uttar Pradesh (U.P.) and Madhya Pradesh (M.P.). However, the project has raised concerns, particularly regarding its ecological impacts, including the construction of a dam inside the Panna Tiger Reserve.

With a budget of ₹45,000 crore sanctioned despite objections from a Supreme Court-appointed committee, the Ken-Betwa River Link Project is an essential milestone in India's ongoing efforts to manage water resources, especially in drought-prone areas. Yet, its potential environmental consequences cannot be overlooked.

Historical Evolution of River Interlinking

The concept of interlinking rivers in India dates back to the 19th century, first proposed by Sir Arthur Cotton. Cotton's vision was further refined by eminent engineer M. Visvesvaraya, who emphasized the role of interlinking in addressing water shortages. In the 1970s and 1980s, the idea evolved into the notion of a National Water Grid, advocated by K.L. Rao and Dinshaw Dastur.

In 1982, the National Water Development Agency (NWDA) was established to study the feasibility of interlinking 30 rivers across India. However, despite years of deliberation, the realization of a comprehensive water grid remains a challenge.

Objectives of River Interlinking Projects

The key objectives behind river interlinking projects like the Ken-Betwa River Link include:

  • Transfer of water from surplus rivers like the Ganga and Brahmaputra to water-scarce regions in central and southern India.
  • Mitigation of floods and droughts through better water management and distribution.
  • Improvement of rural incomes by reducing water distress in agriculture.

Environmental Concerns

Ecological Costs

Despite the benefits, river interlinking projects carry significant ecological costs. The construction of dams and reservoirs leads to the destruction of wetlands, forests, and biodiversity. Additionally, deltaic ecosystems, which rely on silt deposition and regular flooding, are at risk. The diversion of rivers could impact land fertility and the livelihoods of those who depend on these ecosystems for survival.

Climate Change Impact

Climate change further complicates the issue by introducing unpredictable rainfall patterns, making it difficult to accurately plan and manage water resources. Additionally, reducing the silt load in rivers could lead to the degradation of coastal and river ecosystems, harming biodiversity.

Case Studies of Failures

Several previous river diversion projects have experienced adverse ecological consequences:

  • Indus Delta (Pakistan): The construction of barrages led to the impoverishment of the delta, once rich in biodiversity.
  • Narmada River (India): The Sardar Sarovar Dam caused severe downstream drying, affecting the surrounding ecosystem.
  • Kissimmee River (Florida): Channelization of the river destroyed wetlands, leading to costly restoration efforts.
  • Aral Sea (Soviet Union): Irrigation projects diverted rivers, leading to the desiccation of the Aral Sea.

The Flawed Assumption of ‘Surplus’ Water

The notion that rivers have "surplus" water is misleading. In reality, floodwaters are vital for several reasons:

  • Silt deposition in deltas, essential for soil fertility.
  • Groundwater recharge, ensuring sustainable water supply.
  • Support for biodiversity in wetland and river ecosystems.

India’s Real Water Crisis

While projects like the Ken-Betwa River Link aim to address water shortages, India's water crisis is rooted in systemic issues such as inadequate water management, corruption, and inefficient policies. Solutions must go beyond large-scale infrastructure projects and focus on:

  • Watershed management that involves local communities in conservation efforts.
  • Adoption of water-efficient technologies like drip irrigation (successfully implemented in Israel).
  • Innovative wastewater recycling programs to maximize resource use.

Impact on Rivers and Religious Significance

Rivers hold profound religious and cultural significance in India, revered in the Hindutva ideology. However, excessive dam construction, pollution, and commercialization of rivers have placed them at risk. It is essential for India's political leadership to recognize the sacred value of rivers and act to protect them from environmental degradation.

Conclusion

The Ken-Betwa River Link Project represents both an opportunity and a challenge. While it promises relief to water-scarce regions, its ecological impacts demand careful consideration. As India moves forward with river interlinking projects, it is crucial to balance infrastructural development with environmental preservation, ensuring that future generations inherit a sustainable and thriving ecosystem.
