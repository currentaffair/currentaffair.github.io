One Nation, One Election: A Critical Analysis

Introduction

The ruling government, led by the Bharatiya Janata Party/National Democratic Alliance, has been pursuing the One Nation, One Election framework (ONOE) with all seriousness. This proposal seeks to synchronise the Lok Sabha and State Assembly elections under one single electoral cycle. While the advocates of the ONOE have cited several administrative and fiscal efficiencies, its opponents point to the far-reaching consequences of this plan on the democratic and federalist character of India, as laid out in the Constitution of India.

The Historical Context

Simultaneous elections are not something very new in India. During the initial years after Independence, the Election Commission of India (ECI) used to conduct simultaneous elections for both Parliament and State Assemblies.

However, the cycle of cooperative federalism was disrupted with the appearance and imposition of Article 356, which is popularly known as President’s Rule. The first application of this rule in Kerala in 1959 began a trend of federal overreach by the Union government.

Article 356 and Its Misuse

Article 356 was originally intended to restore normalcy in states where governance had become impossible. However, its misuse by successive governments, notably from 1950 to 1994, has had a profound impact on the federal structure of India. The frequent invocation of Article 356 — over 130 times since Independence — has distorted its original purpose.

Defections also pose a serious threat to the stability of State governments, as elected representatives frequently change parties for various enticements, undermining democratic mandates.

The ONOE Proposal and Its Potential Impact

The ONOE proposal suggests aligning State elections with the Lok Sabha elections, which involves constitutional amendments, particularly in Articles 83 and 172. While advocates of ONOE claim it would bring efficiencies, it risks reducing state autonomy and undermining the federalist structure that is central to India's democracy.

Concerns and Challenges

The synchronization of state and national elections could blur the distinctions between the evaluation of state and national governments. Voters might find it difficult to assess the performance of their State government in the context of national elections.

Moreover, the proposal risks creating truncated terms for state governments. A midterm fall of a state government would lead to an election only for the remainder of the synchronized cycle, undermining the democratic principle of "one person, one vote, one value".

Federalism Under Siege

The federal system of India is a fundamental part of its democratic structure. The ONOE proposal threatens to erode the autonomy of state governments, undermining the diversity and plurality that form the basis of India’s democracy.

Implementing ONOE without addressing the systemic flaws such as the misuse of Article 356 and loopholes in anti-defection laws could lead to further centralization of power at the expense of states.

Conclusion

As long as these foundational issues remain unresolved, the One Nation One Election proposal may exacerbate existing vulnerabilities rather than addressing them. True democratic governance requires more than just synchronized elections; it demands a commitment to the principles of federalism and strengthening state governments as equal partners in the governance of India.
