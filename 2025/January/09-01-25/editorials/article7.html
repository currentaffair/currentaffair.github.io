Public Distribution System (PDS) Crisis: The Struggles of the Musahar Community

Introduction

The Right to Food is a fundamental right enshrined under Article 21 of the Indian Constitution, and the Public Distribution System (PDS) is a key mechanism that aims to ensure food security for marginalized sections of society. However, systemic flaws within the PDS, particularly in states like Jharkhand, Odisha, and Bihar, have exacerbated the vulnerability of already marginalized communities. This article delves into the current PDS crisis, focusing on the Musahar community’s struggles and the broader governance issues hindering food security.

Ration Shortage and Ongoing Crisis

In Bihar, the COVID-19 pandemic led to a significant ration supply crisis, affecting marginalized communities like the Musahars, one of the poorest communities in the state. Even after the pandemic, these communities continue to face challenges in accessing regular food supplies through the PDS. The social-political environment, particularly caste-based marginalization, plays a crucial role in exacerbating these issues. Musahars are often denied access to adequate food, reflecting systemic flaws within the distribution system.

Issues with Ration Cards

Many Musahar households, particularly in districts like Patna, either do not have an active ration card or possess incomplete ration cards, which fail to include all family members. Biometric verification, which became mandatory in Fair Price Shops (FPS), has led to widespread exclusion due to technology failures, further straining access to essential food supplies.

Corruption in the PDS

Corruption within the PDS is a significant issue that hampers its effectiveness. Many beneficiaries have reported that FPS dealers release less grain than entitled, with some even distributing poor-quality rice like ‘Usna’ rice. Moreover, wheat, which is also part of the entitlement, is often unavailable. This corruption not only affects the quantity and quality of food but also undermines the entire welfare system meant to aid the most vulnerable.

Documentation Issues and Legal Gaps

States like Bihar, Jharkhand, Uttar Pradesh, and Madhya Pradesh impose additional documentation requirements for PDS enrollment that have no legal basis. The National Food Security Act (NFSA) of 2013 and the PDS Control Order of 2015 do not mandate such documents, yet citizens are often asked to provide income certificates, caste certificates, and residence proof, causing delays and denials. The e-governance systems that were meant to streamline the application process have instead created barriers for the most vulnerable populations.

The Exploitation Market

Exploitation of the poor, particularly the Musahar community, has created a market where middlemen charge exorbitant fees (over ₹3,000) for processing ration cards. These middlemen often disappear after taking payments, leaving people stranded without any guarantee of getting their ration cards. This system feeds off the ignorance and desperation of marginalized individuals, creating a vicious cycle of exploitation.

Application Delays and Bureaucratic Inefficiency

The application process for obtaining a ration card is riddled with delays. Despite the 2015 PDS Control Order stating that ration cards should be issued within 30 days, many applications remain pending for months, sometimes up to 18 months. These delays are not just an inconvenience; they threaten the very survival of families who depend on the PDS for basic sustenance.

Conclusion

Since the landmark People’s Union for Civil Liberties vs Union of India case, which recognized the Right to Food as part of the Right to Life under Article 21, India has made significant strides in recognizing food security as a fundamental right. However, the bureaucratic hurdles, systemic corruption, and the bureaucratic inefficiency that plague the PDS undermine the very purpose of the law. Strengthening the PDS, eliminating unnecessary documentation requirements, and curbing corruption within the system are critical steps needed to ensure that the Right to Food becomes a reality for all, especially marginalized communities like the Musahars.
