Interlinking Rivers: Environmental Cost and Impact

Introduction

On December 25, 2024, Prime Minister Narendra Modi laid the foundation stone for the Ken-Betwa River Link Project, which aims to address the water scarcity in the Bundelkhand region. This region, covering parts of Uttar Pradesh and Madhya Pradesh, has been severely affected by drought. The project plans to connect the Ken river, which is considered surplus, to the Betwa river, aimed at alleviating the water shortage in this drought-prone area. However, the project also raises concerns regarding its environmental impact, including the submergence of the Panna Tiger Reserve due to the construction of a dam.

A Misplaced Model

The Ken-Betwa River Link Project has an estimated cost of ₹45,000 crore. It was launched despite objections from experts, including members of an empowered committee appointed by the Supreme Court of India. Furthermore, it circumvents strict laws regulating hydroelectric power projects. The idea of using technological fixes, such as river interlinking, to solve water scarcity has gained momentum in recent years, with politicians promoting it as a solution to India's growing water crisis.

Concept of Inter-basin Transfer

The concept of inter-basin water transfer was first proposed by Sir Arthur Cotton in the late 19th century. His work in the Godavari and Krishna river valleys laid the foundation for this idea. Later, M. Visvesvaraya refined the concept, and engineers like K.L. Rao and Captain Dinshaw J. Dastur further developed the idea. Initially referred to as the 'National Water Grid,' the concept later re-emerged as the 'River-Interlinking Project' by the Ministry of Water Resources. This project seeks to transfer surplus water from rivers like the Ganga and Brahmaputra to water-deficient regions in central and southern India.

National Water Development Agency (NWDA)

Established in 1982, the NWDA was tasked with conducting surveys and preparing feasibility reports for links under the National Perspective Plan. The agency identified 30 links for feasibility studies, which are expected to alleviate the impacts of floods and droughts, boost rural income, and address water distress in agriculture. However, the estimated cost of these projects is ₹5.5 lakh crore, and this figure does not account for the social, environmental, and operational costs.

Environmental Concerns

The interlinking of rivers may seem like a viable solution to India's water crisis, but it overlooks the enormous environmental costs. The uncontrolled manipulation of natural hydrographic systems could lead to the destruction of ecosystems, and the eventual death of rivers and deltaic regions. These ecological changes could have incalculable repercussions for society in the long term.

Climate Change Considerations

The impacts of climate change could exacerbate the problem, as altered rainfall patterns and fluctuating river flows may aggravate water scarcity. The question arises: How do these river manipulation projects align with India's declared commitment to mitigating climate change?

The Eco-Services of Rivers

Rivers provide essential ecosystem services, such as the flushing of silt from riverbeds and the support of biodiversity in deltaic regions. Diverting water from rivers not only impacts the fertility of the land but also disrupts the delicate balance between freshwater and saltwater in deltaic regions. The idea that water "lost to the sea" is surplus is misleading, as it fails to account for the ecological importance of river flow.

Impact of Water Diversion on Deltaic Regions

Diverting river water for interlinking projects could starve deltaic regions of the freshwater they need to balance the intrusion of saltwater. This could have disastrous effects on the ecosystem, leading to long-term environmental damage.

Lessons from the Indus Delta

The Indus Delta offers a valuable lesson in the consequences of water diversion. The construction of barrages on the Indus River, beginning with the British and continuing with Pakistan, led to the impoverishment of the delta ecosystem. Similarly, the Narmada River has suffered since the completion of the Sardar Sarovar dam, demonstrating the long-term effects of altering river systems.

Global Examples of River Channelization

The channelization of the Kissimmee River in Florida in the 1950s, authorized by the U.S. Congress to mitigate flooding, resulted in the loss of wetlands and severe ecological damage. Similarly, the diversion of rivers that fed the Aral Sea in Central Asia has led to one of the largest ecological disasters in history, with the lake now transformed into a desert.

The Real Reasons for India’s Water Crisis

India’s water crisis is not just due to water scarcity, but also poor water management, ineffective environmental practices, legal ambiguities, and widespread corruption. These issues need to be addressed comprehensively in order to solve the water crisis in India.

National Water Policy Recommendations

The national water policy should adopt a holistic approach to watershed management. Local citizen involvement in monitoring the hydrological cycle, aquifer management, and effective irrigation practices should be prioritized. Additionally, wastewater management programs and comprehensive studies of river basins should be initiated before embarking on large-scale water transfer projects.

International Example: Israel’s Success

Israel has achieved remarkable success in water resource management. The country has implemented drip irrigation techniques that save up to 75% of pumped water. Along with modern technology, Israel also incorporates vernacular practices in watershed management to ensure sustainable water use.

Conclusion

Despite the ruling regime's emphasis on the spiritual significance of rivers, the River Interlinking Project poses a grave threat to India's rivers. Manipulated by dams, polluted by waste, and commodified for political gain, India’s rivers face existential challenges. The question remains: who will save them?
