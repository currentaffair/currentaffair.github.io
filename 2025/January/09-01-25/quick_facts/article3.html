Supreme Court Criticism on RTI Appointments

The Supreme Court criticized delays by both the Centre and States in appointing Information Commissioners, which hampers the effectiveness of the Right to Information (RTI) Act.

Vacancies and Pendency

There are eight vacant posts in the Central Information Commission (CIC), resulting in a backlog of over 23,000 pending appeals. Several state commissions have been defunct since 2020, further affecting RTI petitions.

Supreme Court’s Criticism

Justice Surya Kant questioned the purpose of transparency institutions if no personnel are appointed. The court expressed concern about the dominance of retired bureaucrats in these posts, limiting diversity in the appointments.

Legal Precedents and Court’s Orders

Despite a 2019 Supreme Court judgment urging appointments from various fields, there has been little progress. The court's orders in 2023 and 2024 for timely appointments have been ignored.

Government Response

Advocate Prashant Bhushan argued that the appointment of bureaucrats undermines the RTI's intent. The Centre has been asked to file an affidavit detailing a timeline for filling vacancies.

Court’s Directives

The Department of Personnel and Training must provide a timeline for completing the appointments. States are directed to notify applicant lists, publish search committee compositions, and complete the process within eight weeks.

Implications

The Supreme Court's intervention is critical to ensuring the effective functioning of the RTI mechanism and preventing delays from undermining citizens' right to information. The ruling highlights concerns about political influence in appointments and the need for transparency in the process.
