Is Organizing a Protest Enough for UAPA?

Case Overview

The Delhi High Court is considering whether organizing a protest is sufficient to invoke the Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act (UAPA) in the case of Umar Khalid and Sharjeel Imam, who are accused in the 2020 North-East Delhi riots.

UAPA Invocation

UAPA is typically used in cases involving terrorism or activities threatening national security. The Delhi Police charged the accused with being part of a conspiracy behind the communal violence in February 2020, which resulted in 53 deaths and over 700 injuries.

Court's Observation

The court emphasized that intent is crucial under UAPA. Simply organizing a protest is not enough unless there is evidence showing intent to instigate violence. The court also questioned whether the protests were a "facade" for violence.

Evidence and Allegations

Evidence linked the accused to a WhatsApp group used for organizing protests. The prosecution argued that the protests were a cover for violent actions. The court asked for specific evidence showing how peaceful protests transitioned to violence.

Legal Path Forward

The court's inquiry reflects the need for clarity on applying UAPA in cases involving protests. The prosecution must demonstrate that the accused intended to incite violence, not just organize peaceful protests. The next hearing is scheduled for Thursday.

Way Forward

The prosecution must provide solid evidence linking the accused to terrorism or violent conspiracies. This case could set a legal precedent on the application of UAPA in cases where peaceful protests may be accused of escalating into terrorism.
