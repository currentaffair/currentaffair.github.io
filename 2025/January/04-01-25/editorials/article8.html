Farmer’s Demand for Legalizing MSP

Why in News?

The Supreme Court (SC) of India recently criticized the Central Government for failing to engage with protesting farmers and address their grievances. The court urged the Centre to consider the demands of farmers while responding to a fresh plea seeking a legal guarantee for Minimum Support Price (MSP). This development follows the prolonged protests by farmer groups at the Punjab-Haryana border.

What is the Petition for MSP Guarantee?

The petition seeks a legal guarantee for MSP on crops, based on promises made during the 2021 farmers' protests after the repeal of the farm laws. The petition demands that the MSP must be enshrined as a legal right to ensure stable incomes for agricultural producers. While the Supreme Court did not issue a direct order, it suggested using the High-Powered Committee to resolve the issue and urged the Centre to respond promptly.

Why are Farmers Protesting in India?

The protests stem from long-standing grievances related to India’s 1991 economic liberalization, which prioritized industrialization over agriculture, leading to distress in rural areas. Farmers face low crop returns and rising input costs, despite the government setting MSP for several crops. However, implementation is limited, with procurement primarily focused on rice and wheat, leaving many farmers in non-major cropping areas selling their produce below production costs.

The World Trade Organization (WTO) agreements limit India's ability to impose trade restrictions or provide subsidies to farmers, which the protesters argue hinders India's control over procurement policies and subsidies.

Key Demands of Farmers

The primary demand is for a law that guarantees MSP for all crops, following the recommendations of the Swaminathan Commission. This includes ensuring that MSP covers a 50% profit margin over the cost of production. The current MSP is based on A2+FL, which includes paid-out expenses and unpaid family labor, while the Swaminathan Commission suggests a more comprehensive cost (C2) for better coverage.

Other key demands include full debt waivers for farmers, compensation and pensions for those affected by agricultural distress, better working conditions and wages for agricultural labor, and protection of indigenous peoples' rights over land and water.

Government's Position

The government has argued that a legal guarantee for MSP would be unfeasible due to logistical challenges and the high cost of procurement. The central government is also concerned about the potential economic impact, including food inflation and budgetary constraints.

Arguments For and Against the Legalization of MSP

Arguments For Legalizing MSP

  • Addressing Farmer Distress: Legalizing MSP would ensure fair prices for crops, addressing low returns and providing financial security.
  • Promoting Formal Markets: It could encourage formal market transactions and align with government efforts to promote transparency in digital agriculture.
  • Stable Market Prices: Legal MSP could reduce price volatility, stabilizing both farm incomes and consumer prices.
  • Agricultural Investment: Predictable income could foster investment in agriculture, improving productivity and sustainability.

Arguments Against Legalizing MSP

  • Logistical Challenges: Implementing MSP for all crops nationwide is difficult due to inadequate infrastructure.
  • High Costs for Government: The cost of procuring all crops at MSP could strain the economy and lead to budgetary constraints.
  • Food Inflation: MSP could raise food prices, affecting consumers if the government buys crops at MSP.
  • Market Distortion: Legal MSP could disrupt natural market dynamics, leading to inefficiencies.
  • WTO Constraints: International trade agreements may limit India's ability to implement MSP policies effectively.

Alternatives to Legalizing MSP Nationwide

Targeted Approach

A smaller-scale implementation of MSP could stabilize prices without overwhelming the procurement system. The Pradhan Mantri Annadata Aay Sanraksan Abhiyan (PM-AASHA) is a program that ensures fair prices through MSP and price deficiency payments. Some states have successfully expanded procurement systems, and state-specific legislation could be considered for regional agricultural challenges.

Cooperatives' Role

Promoting cooperatives and farmer producer organizations (FPOs) could be an alternative. A robust legal framework, modern storage facilities, and better infrastructure are necessary to support cooperatives. The Pradhan Mantri Kisan Sampada Yojana (PMKSY) can complement this by enhancing infrastructure.

Contract Farming & Crop Insurance

Encouraging contracts between farmers and corporations or cooperatives can secure a guaranteed price for produce. Expanding crop insurance schemes like the Pradhan Mantri Fasal Bima Yojana (PMFBY) can protect farmers from losses due to natural disasters or market fluctuations.

Conclusion

The debate surrounding the legalization of MSP is multifaceted, with both benefits and challenges. While legalizing MSP could provide financial security for farmers, it raises concerns about the economic impact and logistical feasibility. Alternatives like a targeted approach, promoting cooperatives, and strengthening crop insurance could offer a balanced solution to farmer distress, ensuring sustainability and fair prices without overwhelming the system.
