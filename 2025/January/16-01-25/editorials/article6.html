India's Urea Production: Make vs. Buy Debate

Introduction

India's urea production has seen significant growth, especially with the commissioning of new plants. This increase aligns with the government’s Atma Nirbharta (self-reliance) initiative. The growth in domestic urea production aims to support the New Green Revolution, particularly in the eastern states, to boost agricultural productivity and reduce dependence on imports.

Understanding the Make vs. Buy Debate

The question of whether to "make" (domestic production) or "buy" (import) urea has become central to India's fertilizer industry. The decision hinges on factors like natural gas pricing, transportation logistics, and self-reliance initiatives. This article explores the dynamics of urea production in India, examining the expansion of domestic production and the implications of these developments on agricultural productivity and economic growth.

Key Developments in Urea Production

Expansion of Domestic Urea Production

India's domestic urea production has risen significantly, from 22 million tonnes (mt) in 2011-12 to an expected 31.4 mt in 2023-24. This expansion is a direct result of new plant commissioning, including six new plants since 2019, with a seventh under construction. The plants, located in states driving the New Green Revolution, have significantly boosted domestic production, with some exceeding their rated capacities.

Impact on Urea Imports

Urea imports have decreased from 7.8 mt in 2011-12 to 7 mt in 2023-24, marking a 31.7% reduction in the current fiscal year. It is anticipated that imports could drop below 5 mt — the lowest level since 2006-07. This reduction in imports reflects India's growing self-reliance in urea production.

New Urea Plants and Their Strategic Locations

Several new urea plants have been installed in key states such as Uttar Pradesh, West Bengal, Bihar, Jharkhand, and Telangana. These plants have contributed to the success of the New Green Revolution by improving the availability of fertilizers in regions critical for agricultural productivity. Notably, Matix Fertilisers holds a 20% market share in Eastern India and is the sole urea producer in West Bengal. The Panagarh plant in West Bengal is India’s largest and most energy-efficient urea producer.

The Talcher Project

The Talcher urea plant in Odisha, under construction at a cost of ₹17,080.69 crore, will use coal gasification technology, marking a first for India. The plant will utilize coal from the Talcher mines blended with petroleum coke to optimize efficiency. This project represents a significant technological leap in India’s urea production capabilities.

Technology and Cost Efficiency

New plants are energy-efficient, with a feedstock cost of $318 per tonne based on natural gas priced at $15.9/mmBtu. The cost of domestic urea production at this gas price is approximately $493 per tonne, though this drops to $427 per tonne when customs duties and taxes are excluded. The energy-efficient design of these plants ensures that they are competitive in comparison to imports.

Domestic Urea Production vs. Imported Urea

Domestic urea production costs are lower than imported urea, especially in regions with new plants and robust gas infrastructure. However, imported urea incurs additional costs such as handling, transport, and bagging, which add $30-$35 per tonne. The cost gap between domestic and imported urea is thus narrowing, making domestic production increasingly competitive.

Impact on LNG Infrastructure and Regional Production

India's LNG terminals play a crucial role in supporting urea production, particularly in Western and Southern India. While Western and Southern India continue to rely on urea imports, Northern and Eastern India benefit from domestic production due to the availability of natural gas infrastructure and new plants.

Economic and Employment Benefits

The Make-in-India strategy fosters domestic production, contributing to job creation and economic growth in rural and industrial areas. By focusing on domestic production, India ensures a sustainable solution for its agricultural needs while promoting economic activity in key regions.

Shifting Focus and Future Strategy

Hybrid Strategy for Urea Supply

To optimize the urea supply, India could consider a hybrid strategy: maintaining domestic manufacturing in Northern and Eastern India while increasing imports for the Peninsular region. This strategy would also involve shutting down inefficient plants and streamlining consumption to reduce overall costs.

Pricing and Consumption Trends

Urea consumption in India has risen steadily, from 29.6 mt in 2011-12 to 35.8 mt in 2023-24, fueled by the frozen subsidized farmgate price. A more rational pricing system could encourage judicious fertilizer use among farmers, reducing demand and ensuring long-term sustainability.

Conclusion

The "Make vs. Buy" debate in India’s urea industry is reflective of broader changes in the fertilizer sector. With a focus on self-reliance and technological advancements, India is well on its way to reducing dependency on urea imports. The government’s push for more domestic production, supported by the expansion of LNG infrastructure and the development of new plants, holds promise for the future of the Indian agricultural sector.
