Managing Hazardous Waste

Introduction

The management of hazardous waste is a critical concern for both human health and the environment. The improper disposal of such waste can lead to catastrophic consequences, as demonstrated by the infamous Bhopal Gas Tragedy. This article explores hazardous waste management, examining key incidents, regulatory measures, disposal methods, and ongoing challenges.

Why in News?

Four decades after the Bhopal gas tragedy (1984), hazardous waste (toxic waste) from the abandoned Union Carbide India Ltd (UCIL) factory in Madhya Pradesh was finally removed for incineration. This milestone has brought renewed attention to hazardous waste management and its long-term implications.

What Was the Bhopal Gas Tragedy?

On the night of December 2, 1984, a catastrophic chemical leak occurred at the UCIL pesticide plant in Bhopal, India. The leak involved methyl isocyanate (MIC) gas, which killed over 5,000 people and poisoned more than half a million others, making it the world's worst industrial disaster. The leak was triggered by a failed maintenance attempt and malfunctioning safety systems.

Key Facts: Reports of chemical leaks, including phosgene and methyl isocyanate, had been documented in the years leading up to the disaster.

Health Effects

The immediate effects of exposure included respiratory issues, abdominal pain, eye problems, and neurological impairments. Long-term effects included decreased lung function, genetic abnormalities, pregnancy loss, and increased infant mortality rates in affected populations.

Government and Legal Response

The Indian government passed the Bhopal Gas Leak Disaster (Processing of Claims) Act, 1985, to represent the victims in legal proceedings. Initially, UCIL offered USD 5 million in relief, but the Indian government demanded USD 3.3 billion. The case was settled in 1989 for USD 470 million. In 2010, seven Indian nationals employed by UCIL were convicted for causing death by negligence but were later released on bail.

Aftermath and Legacy

Despite the passage of time, survivors of the Bhopal Gas Tragedy still face health complications and lack access to adequate healthcare. Welfare organizations continue to demand the removal of toxic waste from the abandoned factory site, highlighting the long-lasting impact of the disaster on the community.

What is Hazardous Waste?

Hazardous waste refers to waste that poses a danger to health or the environment due to its toxicity, flammability, reactivity, or corrosiveness. This can include chemicals, industrial by-products, and other dangerous substances that can cause significant harm when not properly handled.

Sources of Hazardous Waste: Most hazardous waste is generated during chemical production and consumption. Additionally, small and medium enterprises often use outdated technologies, resulting in higher and more toxic hazardous waste.

Hazardous Waste Regulations

India has implemented regulations to manage hazardous waste, including the Environment Protection Act of 1986 and the Hazardous Waste (Management and Handling) Rules of 1989. These rules were amended several times in the following decades to address new waste types such as electronic waste and metal scrap.

India is also a signatory to the Basel Convention (1992), which aims to reduce the transboundary movement of hazardous waste.

Hazardous Waste Production in India

India generates approximately 7.66 million tonnes of hazardous waste annually, with the majority originating from the chemical and metal processing industries. Seven states—Gujarat, Maharashtra, Tamil Nadu, Andhra Pradesh, West Bengal, and Chhattisgarh—account for 83% of hazardous waste generation in the country.

How Hazardous Waste is Disposed

There are several methods of disposing hazardous waste, including:

Co-Processing

This method involves using hazardous waste materials as alternative raw materials or fuels in industries, particularly cement manufacturing. Approximately 25 cement plants in India are now using co-processing for hazardous waste disposal.

Material and Energy Recovery

This method focuses on recovering valuable materials and energy from waste, such as recovering copper from cable residues or utilizing spent oils and solvents as alternative fuels in industrial processes.

Incineration

Incineration involves burning waste at high temperatures to reduce it to bottom ash, flue gases, and heat. This process can also produce electricity. However, there are concerns about the environmental impact of the toxic gases released during incineration.

Pyrolysis

Pyrolysis is the thermal decomposition of organic materials in the absence of oxygen. It produces bio-oil, syngas, and char, all of which can be used as fuels or raw materials for other processes.

Conclusion

The Bhopal Gas Tragedy serves as a stark reminder of the dangers of industrial negligence and the long-term impact of hazardous waste on communities. Despite advancements in regulations and disposal methods, such as the Hazardous Waste Management Rules and the Basel Convention, the safe management of hazardous waste remains a significant challenge. Stricter compliance, technological upgrades, and effective remediation efforts are essential to prevent future disasters and ensure the safety and well-being of affected populations.
