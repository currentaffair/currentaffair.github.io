Addressing Air Pollution in India

Introduction

Air pollution is a critical environmental concern in India, with cities like Delhi experiencing particulate matter (PM10 and PM2.5) levels that significantly exceed World Health Organization (WHO) guidelines. This issue poses substantial risks to public health, economic productivity, and environmental sustainability. The persistence of poor air quality year-round, particularly exacerbated by winter conditions, highlights the urgency for systemic reforms and interventions across various sectors. In this article, we explore the key sources of air pollution in India, its impacts, and strategies for addressing it.

Key Sources of Air Pollution in India

Transport Sector

The transport sector remains a dominant contributor to air pollution in India.

  • Dominance of Fossil Fuels: The increasing number of vehicles, along with poor vehicular standards, significantly contributes to the emission of particulate matter (PM2.5).
  • Traffic Congestion: Despite metro systems in major cities, congestion continues to worsen emissions, primarily due to inadequate last-mile connectivity.
  • Policy Challenges: Implementation of policies like the 10-year vehicle age limit in Delhi-NCR is inconsistent. A slow transition to electric vehicles (EVs) reflects a lack of infrastructure and incentives.
  • Public Transport Deficiency: Insufficient public transportation options lead to higher reliance on private vehicles, increasing air pollution.

Industrial Sector

  • Usage of Polluting Fuels: Biomass, coal, and unauthorized fuels are commonly used by small- and medium-scale industries, leading to high emissions.
  • Poor Operational Practices: Many industries fail to properly operate air pollution control devices, undermining efforts to curb emissions.

Municipal Waste Management

  • Open Burning of Waste: The inefficient segregation of waste leads to increased landfill fires and open burning of municipal solid waste.
  • Lack of Infrastructure: Insufficient infrastructure for waste treatment remains a major challenge in urban areas.

Re-Suspended Dust

  • Urban Infrastructure Gaps: Dust from unpaved roads, construction sites, and barren lands contributes significantly to PM10 levels.

Agriculture

  • Stubble Burning: The burning of crop residues in states like Punjab and Haryana exacerbates winter air pollution in Delhi-NCR.
  • Unsustainable Practices: Excessive use of fertilizers leads to ammonia emissions, further degrading air quality.

Rural Emissions

  • Biomass Burning: In rural areas, households often burn biomass for cooking and heating, which releases high levels of particulate matter.
  • Regional Contribution: Emissions from rural areas affect the air quality of nearby urban centers due to interconnected airsheds.

Impacts of Air Pollution

Public Health

  • Air pollution contributes to respiratory diseases, cardiovascular problems, and lung cancer.
  • Marginalized populations are disproportionately affected by air pollution due to their living conditions and limited access to healthcare.

Economic Consequences

  • Pollution-related illnesses result in a significant loss of productivity due to absenteeism from work and the rising healthcare costs.
  • The economic burden of air pollution also hampers the nation's ability to grow and compete globally.

Environmental Degradation

  • Air pollution damages biodiversity, affecting ecosystems and soil quality, particularly in regions dependent on agriculture.

Strategies to Tackle Air Pollution in India

Transport Sector Reforms

  • Promote Electric Vehicles: Develop EV infrastructure and provide incentives to facilitate a transition to electric vehicles.
  • Scrappage Policy Enforcement: Implement mandatory retirement of old, polluting vehicles to curb emissions.
  • Strengthen Public Transport: Improve public transportation service quality and enhance last-mile connectivity in metro systems.
  • Intelligent Traffic Management: Use AI and data-driven systems to optimize traffic flow and reduce congestion.

Industrial Emission Control

  • Fuel Shift: Encourage the transition to cleaner fuels, such as natural gas.
  • Strict Monitoring: Mandate real-time emissions reporting for industrial facilities to ensure compliance.

Waste Management

  • Segregation at Source: Promote public awareness and systems for waste segregation to reduce landfill dependency and open burning.
  • Carbon Market Utilization: Leverage carbon credits for waste treatment infrastructure financing.

Dust Management

  • Pave Roads: Prioritize the paving and maintenance of roads in urban areas to reduce re-suspended dust.
  • Construction Guidelines: Enforce dust suppression measures at construction sites to prevent air pollution.

Agriculture

  • Biofertilizers: Encourage the use of biofertilizers to reduce ammonia emissions.
  • Incentivize Crop Residue Management: Promote sustainable practices like Happy Seeders to prevent stubble burning.

Rural Interventions

  • Biogas Plants: Establish rural biogas units to reduce biomass burning.
  • Community Awareness: Educate rural populations on the health impacts of air pollution to encourage sustainable practices.

Conclusion

India's air pollution crisis is a complex issue requiring comprehensive and coordinated efforts across sectors. While temporary solutions, such as water sprinkling and artificial rain, offer short-term relief, it is essential to address the root causes of pollution. By aligning air quality measures with climate goals, strengthening institutional frameworks, and fostering public participation, India can transform this crisis into an opportunity for a cleaner, healthier future.
