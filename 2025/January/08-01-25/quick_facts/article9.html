7.1 Magnitude Earthquake Strikes Tibetan China and Nepal

On January 7, 2025, a magnitude 7.1 earthquake struck Tibetan China and Nepal, with the epicenter 80 km north of Mt. Everest, in Tingry county, Shigatse region, Tibet. The quake caused 95 deaths, 130 injuries, and widespread destruction in China. Nepal's impact is still being assessed. Tremors were felt in Kathmandu, Thimphu, and Kolkata.

Significance of Location

The epicenter lies in the Lhasa terrane, home to China's largest hydroelectric power dam project on the Yarlung Tsangpo River, which flows into India as the Brahmaputra. Concerns arise over the dam’s impact on river flow, potential water shortages, and India’s interest in the river's management.

Hydroelectric Dam Project

China’s hydroelectric project aims to generate 300 billion kWh annually. Its location in the Lhasa terrane raises environmental and geopolitical concerns about its effect on the river’s flow and its relationship with India.

Himalayan Tectonics

The earthquake’s cause is linked to the tectonic collision between the Indian and Eurasian plates, responsible for the formation of the Himalayas 50 million years ago. The Indian plate is still pushing into the Eurasian plate at 60 mm/year, causing significant stress and frequent earthquakes.

Historical Earthquake Activity

Since 1950, over 21 significant earthquakes (magnitude 6+) have occurred in the Lhasa terrane, with the most recent major earthquake being a 6.9 magnitude event in 2017.

Implications for Water and Environment

The Himalayan region, known as the “third pole” due to its vast water reserves, is highly sensitive to seismic activity. Earthquakes can alter river courses, destabilize glaciers, and increase flood risks.

Geological Concerns

Understanding tectonic stress in the region is essential for predicting future earthquakes and their potential impact on infrastructure and the environment.
