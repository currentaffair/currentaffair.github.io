Supreme Court Calls for Legislative Reviews

Introduction

Recently, the Supreme Court (SC), while hearing a plea regarding the 45-day limitation under Section 81 of the Representation of the People Act, 1951, emphasized the necessity of periodic legislative reviews to assess the effectiveness of laws. The Court highlighted the need for an expert mechanism to evaluate laws and identify deficiencies or bottlenecks, proposing reviews every 20, 25, or 50 years.

Representation of the People Act, 1951 (RPA 1951)

The Representation of the People Act, 1951 (RPA 1951) regulates the electoral system at both the national and state levels. This Act outlines the allocation of seats for the Lok Sabha, State Legislative Assemblies, and State Legislative Councils. It also governs the delimitation of constituencies for elections and specifies qualifications and disqualifications for voters.

Key Provisions of RPA Act:

  • The Act specifies the qualifications and disqualifications for voters.
  • It provides the framework for the preparation of electoral rolls.
  • Section 81 of the Act stipulates that election petitions challenging results must be filed within 45 days from the declaration of results, addressing issues such as illegal practices, corruption, or violations of electoral law.

Need for Periodic Legislative Reviews

Identifying Deficiencies

Laws may lose relevance over time due to evolving circumstances, making regular reviews essential. For instance, the IT Act, 2000, was amended to address cybercrimes, an issue that wasn't as prevalent when the law was first enacted.

Ensuring Relevance of Legislation

Periodic reviews ensure laws remain effective, relevant, and aligned with societal needs. In some cases, laws may be hastily enacted or politically driven, leading to ineffective outcomes. For example, the Anti-liquor law in Bihar led to a surge in bail applications, burdening the judiciary, while Rajasthan's law on cow slaughter raised concerns about the misuse of power.

Addressing Unintended Consequences

Regular reviews help identify areas where laws inadvertently hinder justice or create inefficiencies. Section 81's 45-day limit may prevent valid election disputes due to procedural constraints.

Improving Accountability

Regular reviews ensure laws align with their original objectives. Section 498A of the IPC, initially aimed at protecting women from cruelty, has faced criticism for misuse, underlining the importance of periodic revisions to prevent exploitation of legal provisions.

Global Standards

Many democratic nations conduct routine legislative reviews to ensure laws align with international best practices. The US PATRIOT Act, for example, has undergone revisions to address privacy and civil liberties concerns.

Periodic Revision of Laws in Other Democratic Countries

United Kingdom

The Law Commission of England and Wales regularly reviews existing laws. Its recommendations have led to significant reforms, such as the repeal of the Witchcraft Act, 1735, showcasing its role in modernizing outdated laws.

Australia

The Australian Law Reform Commission systematically reviews the legal framework and submits reports with recommendations for legislative amendments, ensuring that laws remain relevant to contemporary issues.

Challenges in Periodically Reviewing Laws

Lack of Political Will

Legislative reviews are sometimes influenced by political agendas, which can lead to biased amendments that serve electoral interests rather than public welfare. An example is the controversial Farm Laws (2020), criticized for favoring corporate interests over farmer concerns.

Judicial Overreach

The judiciary's involvement in legislative reviews can sometimes be seen as overreach, as seen in the National Judicial Appointments Commission (NJAC) case, where the Supreme Court struck down an attempt to reform judicial appointments.

Legal Complexity

Laws are often interdependent, and isolated amendments can lead to unintended conflicts with existing legislation, complicating the review process. For example, there have been anomalies in provisions related to child pornography under the POCSO Act and the IPC.

Way Forward

Strengthening Law Commission of India

To enhance the quality and consistency of legal reforms, India must strengthen institutions like the Law Commission of India, providing them with greater independence and resources.

Leveraging Technology

Technology, such as platforms like MyGov for public consultations and AI tools for evaluating the effectiveness of laws, can improve the efficiency and inclusivity of the review process.

Resource Allocation

The government should allocate dedicated resources for legal reforms and capacity-building programs to improve the implementation of laws.

Engaging with International Best Practices

India should align its laws with international standards, particularly in environmental and technology governance, as exemplified by the National Green Tribunal (NGT).

Law Commission of India

The Law Commission of India is a non-statutory advisory body tasked with researching and recommending legal reforms. Established in 1955, it advises the government on legal matters and proposes new legislation and amendments to improve the legal system.

Conclusion

By institutionalizing periodic legislative reviews, India can foster a dynamic legal framework that remains responsive to societal needs, democratic ideals, and global standards. Judicial pronouncements and international practices will serve as guiding benchmarks in this ongoing endeavor.
