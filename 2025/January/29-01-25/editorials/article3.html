Strengthening India-Indonesia Ties

Introduction

India and Indonesia share a long-standing relationship, one that has seen numerous changes over the years. The recent visit of Indonesia's President as the Chief Guest at India’s 76th Republic Day celebrations marked a significant milestone, celebrating the 75th anniversary of diplomatic relations between the two nations. This occasion provided an opportunity for both countries to enhance their cooperation and reaffirm their commitment to strengthening their partnership across various sectors. This article delves into the key highlights of the India-Indonesia ties, how they have evolved over time, and the strategic significance of this bilateral relationship.

Key Highlights of India-Indonesia Ties

Comprehensive Strategic Partnership:
In 2018, both nations upgraded their relationship to a Comprehensive Strategic Partnership. This new framework serves as a strong foundation for deeper collaboration across a variety of sectors, including defense, trade, culture, and technology. Both leaders reaffirmed their commitment to this partnership during recent talks, emphasizing the importance of mutual cooperation for regional stability and economic growth.

Defense Cooperation:
One of the most important aspects of the partnership is defense cooperation. Both nations have taken steps to strengthen their military ties through initiatives such as the Coordinated Patrol, Ex Garuda Shakti (Army), and Ex Samudra Shakti (Naval). These exercises enhance the operational compatibility of their forces and build a closer strategic alliance. Furthermore, new dialogues such as the Bilateral Maritime Dialogue and Cyber Security Dialogue are aimed at tackling emerging security challenges.

Trade Cooperation:
Trade between India and Indonesia has flourished, reaching USD 38.8 billion in 2022-2023. Both countries are committed to resolving trade barriers and accelerating the review of the ASEAN-India Trade in Goods Agreement (AITIGA). The MoU on Local Currency Settlement Systems is an important step toward increasing trade by enabling transactions in local currencies, reducing dependence on third-party currencies.

Energy and Health Security:
The partnership also extends to energy, with both nations focusing on the joint exploration of critical minerals such as nickel and bauxite, as well as biofuels. Additionally, both countries have signed MoUs on health cooperation, emphasizing digital health and the development of healthcare professional capacity. This reflects a shared interest in ensuring the well-being of their populations while fostering long-term cooperation in critical sectors.

Technological Cooperation:
India is offering its expertise in Digital Public Infrastructure, Quantum Communication, and High-Performance Computing to Indonesia. These technological exchanges not only benefit both countries but also enhance their ability to compete in the rapidly evolving digital world. Collaborative efforts in these areas are expected to lead to breakthroughs that will have long-term positive impacts.

Cultural Cooperation:
India’s involvement in assisting Indonesia with restoring the Prambanan Temple and reaffirming the "Kashi Cultural Pathway" principles demonstrates the deep cultural bonds between the two nations. The Kashi Cultural Pathway aims to return cultural artifacts to their rightful places and preserve heritage sites for future generations.

Multilateral Cooperation:
Both countries continue to work together on multilateral platforms such as ASEAN, BRICS, and the Indian Ocean Rim Association (IORA). Their joint efforts emphasize regional stability, maritime security, and the importance of international law, especially concerning the South China Sea.

Evolution of India-Indonesia Ties

Early Post-Independence Period (1940s-1950s):
In the early years of independence, India strongly supported Indonesia's fight against Dutch colonial rule. The Treaty of Friendship signed in 1951 laid the foundation for the growing cooperation between the two nations. Both countries aligned on shared values such as non-alignment and peaceful coexistence, leading to active participation in the 1955 Bandung Conference and the formation of the Non-Aligned Movement (NAM) in 1961.

Deterioration in Ties (1960s):
Relations between India and Indonesia experienced a setback in the 1960s due to differences in foreign policy, particularly regarding China. While India faced tensions with China after the 1962 Sino-Indian war, Indonesia maintained cordial relations with China. Furthermore, Indonesia’s alignment with Pakistan during the 1965 India-Pakistan conflict further strained bilateral ties.

Cold War Era (1966-1980s):
Under President Suharto, Indonesia moved away from its previous alignment with China and sought to rebuild ties with India. Key agreements like the 1977 maritime boundary pact and Suharto’s visit to India in 1980 signaled a new phase of cooperation, focusing on economic and security concerns.

‘Look East’ Policy 1991 (1990s):
India’s ‘Look East’ policy in 1991 and its subsequent ‘Act East’ policy in 2014 marked a shift in India’s approach to Southeast Asia. These policies were crucial in strengthening India’s relationship with Indonesia, which became a central partner in India’s vision for a more interconnected and secure region.

Recent Developments (Since 2000s):
Over the past two decades, India and Indonesia’s relationship has grown significantly. Indonesia has become India’s second-largest trading partner in the ASEAN region, and both countries have worked together on critical issues such as maritime security and regional stability. A major development in recent years is the ongoing negotiations for Indonesia to acquire the BrahMos missile system, which highlights growing defense cooperation.

Strategic Importance of Indonesia to India

Strategic Importance:
Indonesia holds a pivotal position in the Indo-Pacific region due to its control over key maritime chokepoints such as the Strait of Malacca, Sunda, and Lombok. These sea lanes are vital for global trade, making Indonesia a key partner for India in ensuring maritime security and the free flow of commerce in the region.

Natural Resources:
Rich in natural resources, Indonesia is a crucial supplier of commodities such as palm oil, tin, rubber, cocoa, and minerals like nickel, copper, and bauxite. India can leverage these resources for its energy, agriculture, and infrastructure needs, benefiting from mutual trade and economic opportunities.

Defense Cooperation:
The ongoing negotiations for the USD 450 million BrahMos missile deal underscore the growing defense ties between the two countries. This cooperation addresses emerging security challenges such as cyber threats, maritime security, and counter-terrorism, making it a vital component of their partnership.

Politics and Governance:
As the world’s largest Muslim-majority nation, Indonesia has made significant strides in promoting secularism through its unique Panchashila Constitution. Indonesia’s approach to countering terrorism and promoting stability provides valuable lessons for India, which faces similar challenges in governance and security.

Global Influence:
Indonesia’s leadership role in ASEAN and its growing influence in the Indo-Pacific region make it an important partner for India. The two countries’ cooperation is crucial for regional stability and mutual economic and security interests.

Conclusion

Indonesia is an essential partner for India, offering strategic advantages in trade, defense, and regional stability. The strengthening of bilateral ties across sectors such as defense, technology, culture, and multilateral cooperation positions both nations to face emerging global challenges together. As India and Indonesia continue to deepen their Comprehensive Strategic Partnership, their collaboration will not only bolster their bilateral relations but also contribute to the broader stability and security of the Indo-Pacific region.
