India's Act East Policy

Introduction

India’s engagement with Southeast Asia and the broader Indo-Pacific region has undergone a significant transformation since the post-Cold War era. This evolving relationship is centered around economic, strategic, and cultural objectives, with an increasing focus on infrastructure, trade, defense cooperation, and regional partnerships.

Historical Context and Evolution

Look East Policy (Post-Cold War Era)

The Look East policy was introduced by Prime Minister P.V. Narasimha Rao in the early 1990s after the Cold War ended. This policy sought to rectify India's neglect of Southeast Asia, despite deep cultural and historical ties, with the goal of strengthening its engagement with the region.

The primary objectives of the Look East policy were:

  • Enhancing trade and economic development.
  • Forging stronger strategic and cultural connections with ASEAN nations.
  • Drawing inspiration from the economic success stories of East Asia.

Key initial outcomes included the reduction of trade barriers to enhance commerce and increased inbound tourism from Southeast Asia.

Act East Policy (Post-2014)

Under Prime Minister Narendra Modi, the Look East policy was elevated to the Act East policy, emphasizing greater action-oriented outcomes. The policy also integrated Northeast India as a critical component in enhancing India's connections with Southeast Asia.

PM Modi’s 3 Cs approach at the 2014 East Asia Summit were:

  • Commerce: Expanding trade and economic ties.
  • Culture: Strengthening historical and cultural linkages.
  • Connectivity: Building infrastructure and digital networks for seamless integration.

Objectives and Achievements of Act East Policy

Strategic Expansion

The Act East policy expanded India's focus beyond ASEAN, reaching the broader Indo-Pacific region. It has led to strengthened regional groupings such as BIMSTEC (Bay of Bengal Initiative for Multi-Sectoral Technical and Economic Cooperation), the Asia Cooperation Dialogue (ACD), and the Indian Ocean Rim Association (IORA). These efforts have been integral in increasing India’s strategic presence in the region.

India’s defense diplomacy has seen remarkable advancements, such as:

  • The sale of BrahMos missiles to the Philippines.
  • A military logistics pact with Vietnam.

Economic and Trade Ties

The policy has helped reduce trade barriers and promoted greater economic integration through Free Trade Agreements (FTAs) with Southeast Asian nations. There has been a notable increase in foreign investment from Southeast Asia, contributing significantly to India’s economic growth.

Cultural and Soft Power Diplomacy

India has promoted shared cultural heritage, including epical traditions like the Ramayana and Mahabharata, and Buddhist linkages. Cultural festivals like the Ramayan Festival have brought India and Southeast Asian nations closer through cultural exchange and mutual appreciation.

Connectivity

Improved infrastructure development in Northeast India has been crucial to enhancing connectivity with Southeast Asia. Key projects include the India-Myanmar-Thailand Trilateral Highway, which aims to provide seamless connectivity between India and Southeast Asia.

Challenges and Areas of Improvement

Strategic and Economic Challenges

Urbanization and industrialization in Northeast India are posing threats to ecological sustainability. Additionally, competition from China’s Belt and Road Initiative (BRI) offers Southeast Asian nations alternative financial incentives, creating a challenge for India’s strategic interests in the region.

Soft Power and Cultural Challenges

China's claim over Buddhist heritage has created challenges for India’s cultural influence in Southeast Asia. Furthermore, the lack of linguistic linkages in the region limits India’s deeper cultural and educational exchanges. Few Indian universities offer courses in key Southeast Asian languages, such as Khmer, Bahasa Indonesia, and Thai, limiting India’s engagement.

Connectivity Bottlenecks

Despite the progress, infrastructure projects like the Kaladan Multi-Modal Transit Transport Project have faced delays. The underdeveloped transport and trade facilities in Northeast India hinder its full potential as a gateway to Southeast Asia.

Key Pillars for Strengthening Act East Policy

Cultural Diplomacy

Epical Traditions

To further strengthen cultural ties, India should organize global conferences on the Ramayana and Mahabharata, involving countries with shared cultural links. Additionally, developing cultural organizations centered on these epics, akin to BIMSTEC, will further promote these traditions.

Buddhism as a Soft Power Tool

India can leverage its Buddhist heritage by promoting the Buddhist tourism circuit, which includes key sites such as Bodh Gaya and Sarnath, in a language-accessible format for Southeast Asian pilgrims. Hosting global conferences on Buddhist studies can also help India strengthen its cultural influence in the region.

Linguistic Integration

India should offer undergraduate and postgraduate courses in Southeast Asian languages at central universities. Collaborations with Southeast Asian countries to host language exchange programs can improve cultural diplomacy and strengthen bilateral ties.

Economic and Connectivity Initiatives

To bolster regional integration, India should expedite the completion of key cross-border projects, such as the India-Myanmar-Thailand Trilateral Highway and the Kaladan Multi-Modal Transit Transport Project. Enhancing trade facilitation measures in the Northeast will further boost India’s engagement with ASEAN markets.

Institutional and Defense Cooperation

India should deepen its institutional ties with ASEAN through regular security dialogues, military exercises, and intelligence-sharing mechanisms. Expanding defense exports, particularly indigenous weapon systems like BrahMos, can further elevate India’s defense diplomacy in the region.

Future Prospects: “Attract East”

India must evolve its Act East policy to the “Attract East” framework. This means positioning India as a hub for Southeast Asian investments and tourism. Enhanced student exchange programs and fostering people-to-people connections will play a crucial role in further strengthening bilateral ties.

India can also promote its image as a global leader in sustainability and green energy, areas that are of significant interest to Southeast Asian nations.

Conclusion

To fully realize the potential of the Act East policy, India must address the challenges by leveraging its cultural heritage, enhancing connectivity, and fostering economic and linguistic ties. By doing so, India can not only deepen its engagement with Southeast Asia but also emerge as a pivotal player in the Indo-Pacific region.
