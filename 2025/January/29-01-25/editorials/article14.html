Environmental Challenges in India

Introduction

The recent Dima Hasao coal mining tragedy in Assam starkly illustrates India's persistent challenge with illegal and hazardous rat-hole mining, despite its ban by the National Green Tribunal in 2014. The ongoing exploitation, driven by industrial demands for coal in cement manufacturing and thermal power plants, reflects the gap between environmental regulations and their enforcement. This constant struggle between development and environmental protection demands immediate attention, particularly as India aims to achieve its ambitious climate goals while sustaining economic growth.

Key Environmental Regulations in India

India's environmental regulations are rooted in constitutional provisions, pollution control laws, forest and wildlife protection acts, and environmental assessment frameworks. These laws aim to balance the nation's development needs with environmental preservation.

Constitutional Provisions

Article 48A directs the State to protect and improve the environment and safeguard forests and wildlife. Article 51A(g) imposes a fundamental duty on citizens to protect the environment. Article 21, recognized by the Supreme Court, includes the right to life and clean air, ensuring environmental protection is a constitutional responsibility.

Pollution Control Laws

The Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1974, aims to prevent and control water pollution by regulating effluent discharge into water bodies. The Air (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1981, aims to curb air pollution by regulating emissions from industries and vehicles. The Environment (Protection) Act, 1986, empowers the central government to take measures for environmental protection.

Forest and Wildlife Protection

India's forest conservation laws include the Indian Forest Act (1927), Forest (Conservation) Act (1980), and the Wildlife (Protection) Act (1972). These aim to regulate deforestation, conserve biodiversity, and protect endangered species. In addition, the Biological Diversity Act (2002) ensures the sustainable use of genetic resources.

Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA)

The EIA process requires prior environmental clearance for projects with significant environmental impacts. This includes public consultations and mandatory environmental management plans before granting approvals.

National Green Tribunal (NGT)

The NGT Act, 2010, establishes a specialized judicial body for resolving environmental disputes, speeding up case resolutions, and imposing strict penalties on violators.

Key Issues with India’s Environmental Regulations

While India has robust environmental regulations, the key challenges lie in weak enforcement, conflicts between development and conservation, and inadequate public participation.

Weak Enforcement Mechanisms

India's environmental laws are stringent but often poorly enforced due to underfunded and understaffed regulatory bodies. The Pollution Control Boards (PCBs) lack sufficient resources to monitor compliance effectively. A recent report highlighted that over 6% of India’s 4,40,989 operational industries fail to meet environmental standards.

Conflicts Between Development and Conservation

Economic growth often leads to the relaxation of environmental regulations. For example, the Forest (Conservation) Amendment Act, 2023, allows the diversion of forest land for non-forest purposes, prioritizing development over ecological preservation. These conflicts result in India's poor ranking on global environmental performance indices.

Inadequate Public Participation

Environmental governance in India often ignores local community input, especially in marginalized areas. The public consultation process, a key feature of the EIA, is often bypassed, leading to conflicts like the Hasdeo Arand coal mining project in Chhattisgarh, where tribal communities were displaced without adequate compensation.

Underutilization of Technology

India’s regulatory agencies have been slow to adopt advanced technologies like IoT sensors, AI, and satellite monitoring, which could improve the detection of environmental violations. For instance, only 476 out of 4,041 cities in India have real-time air quality monitoring stations.

Judicial Overreach and Delayed Litigation

Excessive judicial intervention can delay policy enforcement. In Tamil Nadu, ongoing sand mining litigation has led to unchecked illegal mining due to a lack of clear policy enforcement. As of 2022, over 88,400 environment-related cases were pending trial in India.

Lack of Focus on Climate Adaptation

While India's policies focus on mitigation, such as renewable energy and emissions reduction, the country has insufficient emphasis on adaptation measures like ecosystem restoration and disaster preparedness. This has worsened the impacts of climate-related disasters, such as the 2023 floods in Himachal Pradesh.

Unsustainable Urbanization

Rapid urbanization has led to environmental degradation in cities. In Haryana, natural conservation zones have been reduced by 47%, worsening air quality and groundwater recharge. Similarly, India’s e-waste has surged by 73% in the last five years, and the implementation of e-waste management rules remains underdeveloped.

Illegal Mining

Illegal mining continues to undermine environmental regulations, causing severe ecological damage. In 2022, only 6% of illegal mining cases led to FIRs, highlighting the limited legal action against such activities.

Measures to Strengthen Environmental Regulations

India can strengthen its environmental regulations by improving enforcement, leveraging technology, and adopting sustainable practices that balance development with ecological protection.

Strengthening Enforcement Mechanisms

To ensure better compliance, regulatory bodies like the Pollution Control Boards must be adequately funded and staffed. Implementing AI-based sensors for pollution monitoring and drone surveillance for forest encroachment will enhance the enforcement capabilities of environmental laws.

Promoting Carbon Credit Markets

India can develop a domestic carbon credit trading market to incentivize industries to adopt low-carbon technologies. This could help reduce emissions without stifling industrial growth. Revenue generated from carbon credits could fund renewable energy projects and afforestation programs.

Promoting Climate-Resilient Infrastructure

India must prioritize the development of disaster-resilient infrastructure, especially in vulnerable areas. Eco-friendly building designs, green roofing, and permeable pavements can minimize environmental harm and increase the country’s resilience to climate impacts.

Revamping the EIA Process

The EIA framework must be more transparent, participatory, and evidence-based. Public consultations should be conducted rigorously, with marginalized communities included at every stage of the process. Digital platforms like PARIVESH can streamline EIA approvals.

Incentivizing Clean Energy Transitions

India should expand subsidies for renewable energy and transition industries to clean energy solutions. This includes promoting decentralized renewable energy projects like solar rooftops and mini-grids, especially in rural areas. The Green Hydrogen Mission and electric vehicle incentives should be scaled up to decarbonize key sectors.

Integrating Circular Economy Principles

Adopting circular economy principles can reduce waste and resource extraction while creating economic opportunities. Extended producer responsibility (EPR) for e-waste and plastics should be enforced to incentivize recycling and reduce environmental impact.

Expanding Urban Green Spaces

Cities should allocate a fixed percentage of their area to green spaces, promoting urban forestry to mitigate pollution and improve community well-being. These spaces can serve as carbon sinks, especially in densely populated urban areas.

Promoting Public-Private Partnerships in Conservation

Public-private partnerships can mobilize resources for environmental protection projects. These collaborations can enhance the execution of afforestation, waste management, and clean energy initiatives while ensuring efficient use of resources.

Fostering Community-Led Conservation

Empowering local communities to take part in environmental governance can lead to more inclusive and sustainable development. Community-led afforestation programs and conservation projects, supported by government initiatives, can ensure biodiversity preservation.

Restoring Degraded Ecosystems

Large-scale restoration projects focusing on degraded forests, rivers, and wetlands should be prioritized. Reforestation with indigenous species and wetland restoration can help restore ecosystem services and improve biodiversity, aligning with India’s climate commitments.

Strengthening Judicial Mechanisms

India must improve the capacity of the National Green Tribunal (NGT) to resolve environmental disputes more swiftly. Introducing alternative dispute resolution mechanisms like mediation and arbitration can also help manage industrial conflicts related to environmental violations.

Monitoring Mining Activities

Advanced technologies like satellite imagery and drones can help monitor mining activities in real time. These tools should be integrated with centralized data systems to track and prevent illegal mining operations.

Conclusion

India’s environmental regulations face significant challenges due to weak enforcement, developmental conflicts, and lack of public participation. To address these issues, India must adopt a holistic approach that combines technological advancements, transparent governance, and community-led conservation efforts. A balanced strategy that integrates environmental protection with economic growth is crucial to achieve sustainable development.
