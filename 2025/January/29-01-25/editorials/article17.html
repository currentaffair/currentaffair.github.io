India Indonesia Relations

Introduction

India and Indonesia share a long-standing historical and cultural relationship, which has evolved through multiple phases, including ancient trade, colonial solidarity, and post-independence cooperation. In recent years, the two nations have strengthened their geopolitical and economic ties, particularly in response to global challenges. This article explores the multifaceted relationship between India and Indonesia, examining historical connections, current developments, and future prospects for collaboration.

Historical Context

Ancient Ties

India and Indonesia's relationship dates back to ancient times, with robust cultural and religious exchanges fostered by trade and travel. One of the significant aspects of this relationship was the adaptation of Hinduism in Javanese traditions, along with the incorporation of Vedic scriptures. The use of Sanskrit as a linguistic bridge in both historical texts and cultural interactions further strengthened these ties, creating a shared cultural foundation.

Colonial and Post-Independence Period

During the colonial era, India and Indonesia demonstrated solidarity against imperialism. India provided active support for Indonesia's independence struggle, including banning Dutch overflights and aiding the evacuation of Indonesian leaders. Sukarno, Indonesia's first president, acknowledged India’s critical role in his article in The Hindu (January 5, 1950). This period marked the beginning of a deep sense of mutual support and camaraderie between the two nations.

Cold War and Non-Alignment

Both India and Indonesia were co-founders of the Non-Aligned Movement (NAM), a significant platform during the Cold War era. Their collaboration started at the Bandung Conference, where both nations promoted Asian-African solidarity and neutrality in global power politics. This collaboration helped to forge a diplomatic path that resisted alignment with either the U.S. or Soviet Union during the Cold War.

Strategic and Geopolitical Context

Recent Concerns

India and Indonesia continue to share common interests in addressing critical challenges. The U.S.-China rivalry and China's assertiveness in the Indo-Pacific region are areas of concern for both countries. Moreover, global issues such as food, fertilizer, and fuel security, which have been exacerbated by the ongoing Ukraine conflict, require coordinated efforts. Both countries remain committed to multilateralism, despite weakening global frameworks like the WTO and UN, advocating for a rules-based international order.

Collaborative Efforts

The relationship between India and Indonesia has been marked by close coordination in global forums. For instance, their leadership during the G-20 presidencies in Bali (2022) and Delhi (2023) showcased a strong partnership in addressing global issues. Indonesia’s recent decision to join BRICS further signals an expansion of their geopolitical cooperation, reinforcing the strategic importance of their relationship in the global arena.

Key Developments and Challenges

Prabowo Subianto’s Visit

The visit of Indonesian Defense Minister Prabowo Subianto to India marked a significant step in strengthening bilateral relations. During his visit, both nations signed five Memoranda of Understanding (MoUs) in areas such as health and traditional medicine, maritime security, digital development, and cultural cooperation. While there was mention of defense cooperation, no concrete agreements were reached during the visit, leaving certain aspects of the relationship underdeveloped.

Missed Opportunities

Despite the positive developments, some opportunities were missed. Notably, there was no progress in the sale of India’s Brahmos missile to Indonesia, which had been a topic of interest in previous discussions. Additionally, delays in the Sabang Port project and the Aceh-Andamans connectivity initiatives have raised concerns about the pace of bilateral cooperation. Moreover, the joint statement on geopolitical conflicts was non-committal, despite both nations sharing similar concerns.

Shared Aspirations

Economic Potential

As two of the largest economies in South Asia and Southeast Asia, India and Indonesia hold immense potential for trade and investment growth. The strengthening of economic ties could unlock new avenues for collaboration, particularly in sectors such as infrastructure, technology, and energy. Both nations are keen on leveraging their economic complementarities to boost growth and stability in the region.

Cultural and Religious Harmony

Both India and Indonesia have rich traditions of religious and cultural tolerance, with examples of harmonious coexistence between religious majorities and minorities. These historical examples serve as important models of pluralism, promoting mutual understanding and respect in contemporary times. As the world’s largest democracy, India has often pointed to Indonesia's pluralistic society as an example of religious harmony and tolerance in the region.

Geostrategic Cooperation

In the Indo-Pacific, both India and Indonesia recognize the importance of strengthening maritime security ties to ensure regional stability. Their cooperation in safeguarding maritime routes and addressing common challenges such as piracy and territorial disputes is essential for maintaining peace and prosperity in the region. Platforms like BRICS and G-20 provide valuable opportunities for the two nations to collaborate on global and regional issues, aligning their strategic priorities for long-term success.

Conclusion

India and Indonesia share a deep-rooted historical connection that has evolved into a robust geopolitical and economic partnership. Despite facing challenges, their relationship remains one of the most promising in the Asia-Pacific region. With continued collaboration in key areas such as trade, defense, and maritime security, the future of India-Indonesia relations looks bright. Both nations have the potential to become pillars of stability and growth in the Indo-Pacific, and their ongoing efforts to strengthen bilateral ties will be critical in shaping the region’s future.
