India's Act East Policy

Introduction

India’s engagement with Southeast Asia and the broader Indo-Pacific region has undergone a significant transformation since the post-Cold War era. The shift in policy, starting with the Look East Policy and evolving into the Act East Policy, has led to stronger economic, strategic, and cultural ties with the region. This article explores the history, achievements, challenges, and future prospects of India’s engagement with Southeast Asia through these policies.

Historical Context and Evolution

Look East Policy (Post-Cold War Era)

Introduced by Prime Minister PV Narasimha Rao in the early 1990s after the Cold War ended, the Look East Policy aimed to rectify India’s neglect of Southeast Asia despite deep cultural and historical ties. The policy focused on enhancing trade and economic development while forging stronger strategic and cultural connections with ASEAN nations. It was also inspired by the economic success stories of East Asia.

Initial outcomes included the reduction of trade barriers to enhance commerce and the increase in inbound tourism from Southeast Asia.

Act East Policy (Post-2014)

The Act East Policy was introduced under Prime Minister Narendra Modi in 2014, elevating the previous Look East Policy to a more action-oriented agenda. The policy placed greater emphasis on outcomes and integrated Northeast India as a critical element in its implementation. PM Modi’s three-fold approach at the 2014 East Asia Summit emphasized:

  • Commerce: Expanding trade and economic ties.
  • Culture: Strengthening historical and cultural linkages.
  • Connectivity: Building infrastructure and digital networks for seamless integration.

Objectives and Achievements of Act East Policy

Strategic Expansion

The Act East Policy broadened India's focus from ASEAN to the wider Indo-Pacific region. The policy strengthened regional groupings such as BIMSTEC, the Asia Cooperation Dialogue, and the Indian Ocean Rim Association (IORA). Additionally, India has enhanced its defense diplomacy through initiatives like:

  • The sale of BrahMos missiles to the Philippines.
  • A military logistics pact with Vietnam.

These actions have solidified India’s strategic ties with Southeast Asia, promoting peace and security in the region.

Economic and Trade Ties

The Act East Policy has resulted in a reduction of trade barriers and greater economic integration with Southeast Asia through Free Trade Agreements (FTAs). This has led to enhanced foreign investment flows into India from the region, boosting trade and industrial cooperation.

Cultural and Soft Power Diplomacy

India has also utilized cultural diplomacy to foster stronger relationships with Southeast Asia. This includes promoting shared cultural heritage, such as the Ramayana and Mahabharata epics, and organizing cultural festivals like the Ramayan Festival, which involves Southeast Asian nations.

Connectivity

Infrastructure development in Northeast India has been key to facilitating connectivity with Southeast Asia. Key projects include the India-Myanmar-Thailand Trilateral Highway, aimed at improving physical and economic connectivity.

Challenges and Areas of Improvement

Strategic and Economic Challenges

Despite the successes of the Act East Policy, several challenges remain. Urbanization and industrialization in Northeast India threaten ecological and cultural sustainability. Additionally, competition from China’s Belt and Road Initiative (BRI) presents significant financial incentives to Southeast Asian countries, creating a potential rival for India’s engagement.

Soft Power and Cultural Challenges

China’s active claim over Buddhist heritage has posed a challenge to India’s cultural narrative in the region.
The lack of linguistic linkages also hinders deeper engagement, as few universities in India offer courses in key Southeast Asian languages such as Khmer, Bahasa Indonesia, and Thai.

Connectivity Bottlenecks

Despite various efforts, the Kaladan Multi-Modal Transit Transport Project and other infrastructure initiatives have faced delays. Furthermore, Northeast India’s underdeveloped transport and trade facilities limit its potential role as a gateway to Southeast Asia.

Key Pillars for Strengthening Act East Policy

Cultural Diplomacy

India should focus on organizing global conferences on shared cultural traditions, particularly the Ramayana and Mahabharata, and develop cultural organizations centered on epical heritage. Buddhism, as a tool of soft power, could be promoted by enhancing the Buddhist tourism circuit, focusing on key destinations like Bodh Gaya and Sarnath.

Linguistic Integration

To foster deeper engagement, India should offer undergraduate and postgraduate courses in Southeast Asian languages at central universities. Further, the development of tourist guides, subtitles for films, and children’s literature in languages such as Thai, Khmer, and Bahasa Indonesia could enhance cultural exchange.

Economic and Connectivity Initiatives

It is critical to expedite the completion of infrastructure projects like the India-Myanmar-Thailand Trilateral Highway and the Kaladan Multi-Modal Transit Transport Project.
Additionally, improving trade facilitation measures for the Northeast region will ensure better engagement with ASEAN markets.

Institutional and Defense Cooperation

India should continue to deepen its defense ties with ASEAN nations through joint security dialogues, military exercises, and intelligence-sharing mechanisms. Expanding defense exports, particularly through indigenous systems like BrahMos, will further solidify India’s position in the region.

Future Prospects: "Attract East"

Looking ahead, India must evolve the Act East Policy into the “Attract East” policy. This can be achieved by making India a hub for Southeast Asian investments and tourism, fostering people-to-people connections, and promoting India as a leader in sustainability and green energy to appeal to Southeast Asian nations.

Conclusion

To fully realize the potential of the Act East Policy, India must address ongoing challenges by leveraging its cultural heritage, enhancing connectivity, and strengthening economic and linguistic ties. By doing so, India can deepen its engagement with Southeast Asia and emerge as a pivotal player in the Indo-Pacific region.
