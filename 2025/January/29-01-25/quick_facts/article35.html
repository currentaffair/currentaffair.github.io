Union Budget Components Explained

Overview of Budget Components

The Union Budget consists of two main parts: expenditure and receipts.

Expenditure

  • Capital Expenditure: Focuses on long-term assets, such as infrastructure projects like schools and hospitals.
  • Revenue Expenditure: Ongoing costs like wages and subsidies that don’t create assets.

Receipts

  • Revenue Receipts: Includes tax and non-tax revenue that doesn’t increase liabilities.
  • Non-Debt Capital Receipts: Includes loan recovery and disinvestment.
  • Debt-Creating Receipts: Involves loans and borrowings, increasing liabilities.

Deficit Indicators

  • Fiscal Deficit: The difference between total expenditure and the sum of revenue receipts and non-debt receipts.
  • Primary Deficit: Fiscal deficit minus interest payments.
  • Revenue Deficit: Difference between fiscal deficit and capital expenditure.

Implications for the Economy

The budget affects economic variables such as aggregate demand, income distribution, and fiscal health:

  • Aggregate Demand: Government expenditure boosts demand, while taxes reduce private sector income.
  • Economic Trends: Expenditure and revenue rise with GDP, analyzed relative to growth rates.
  • Fiscal Deficit Analysis: A reduction signals the government’s attempt to reduce aggregate demand.
  • Income Distribution: Welfare spending supports poorer sections, while tax cuts benefit corporations.

Fiscal Rules and Policy Impact

  • Fiscal Rules: Set by the N.K. Singh Committee, guiding the debt-GDP ratio and fiscal deficit targets.
  • Adjusting Expenditure: To meet fiscal targets, government may adjust expenditure, even if it contradicts economic expansion needs.
  • Limitations of Current Rules: The fiscal framework may limit flexibility to address challenges like unemployment.

Re-examining Fiscal Policy

Given contemporary challenges, India’s fiscal rules may need to be revisited to allow more flexibility in responding to economic needs.
