Bombay HC Loudspeaker Ruling

Case Context

The Bombay High Court directed the Maharashtra government to implement measures controlling decibel levels from loudspeakers, public address systems (PAS), and other sound-emitting gadgets used at religious places or institutions, irrespective of religion.

Background of the Case

  • Petition Filed: The Jaago Nehru Nagar Residents Welfare Association filed a petition over noise pollution caused by loudspeakers at religious places, especially during early mornings and late nights.
  • Noise Pollution Norms: The permissible decibel level in residential areas is 55 decibels during the day and 45 decibels at night. The decibel levels at two mosques exceeded 80 decibels.

Court's Judgment

  • Freedom of Religion: The court ruled that loudspeakers are not essential to any religion and do not fall under the protection of Article 25 (freedom of religion) of the Constitution.
  • Public Peace: The court emphasized the need to protect public peace and health in a cosmopolitan city like Mumbai, ensuring the enforcement of noise pollution laws.
  • Action by Police: The court directed Mumbai Police to use mobile apps to measure decibel levels, issue warnings, impose fines, and seize equipment for repeated violations.

Essential Practices Doctrine

  • Doctrine Origin: The doctrine, introduced by B.R. Ambedkar, distinguishes between secular and religious practices. The state can intervene if practices aren't essential to the religion.
  • Past Cases: Examples include Bijoe Emmanuel (1986) where the court allowed students to abstain from the anthem due to religious beliefs and Ananda Marga (2004) ruling against public street dances as non-essential.

Way Forward

  • Balance Between Religious Freedom and Equality: The state and court must respect religious practices but prioritize equality, dignity, and access to basic rights over practices that harm others.
