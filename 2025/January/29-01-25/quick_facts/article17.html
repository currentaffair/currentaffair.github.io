Secular Inheritance Law Debate

Context

Chief Justice of India, CJI Sanjiv Khanna, stated that freedom to renounce faith and choose secular inheritance laws should apply to all religions, not just one.

Petitioner's Case

  • Safiya P.M., a woman from Kerala, petitioned to be governed by the secular Indian Succession Act, instead of Sharia law.
  • She renounced her Muslim faith and now identifies as a non-believer.

Equality Across Faiths

CJI emphasized that secular inheritance laws should apply universally, irrespective of religion, and not favor one faith over another.

Hindu Succession Act Issue

  • CJI pointed out that under the Hindu Succession Act, converting from Hinduism results in losing inheritance rights.
  • Safiya argued that this should not apply to individuals who renounce their faith.

Will as a Solution

Solicitor-General Tushar Mehta argued that inheritance through a Will is still possible, despite the restrictions under the Hindu Succession Act.

Intestate Succession Consideration

CJI highlighted complications with intestate succession (without a Will) and joint Hindu family property in inheritance cases.

Union’s Response

The Union Government was asked to file a counter-affidavit in response to the petition, due to the importance of the issue.

Religion in Official Forms

CJI suggested that if the petition succeeds, individuals should have the option to not mention their religion on official forms, protecting their right to privacy.

Advocate's Argument

Advocate Prashant Padmanabhan argued that Safiya, as a non-believer, should not face any disqualification in inheritance rights or civil rights due to her religious choice, invoking secularism under the Constitution.

Inheritance Under Muslim Law

Safiya argued that under Muslim law, she would inherit only one-third of her father’s property, which complicates her situation due to her care responsibilities for her autistic brother.

Legal Vacuum

Safiya’s petition highlighted a legal gap, where even with a "no-religion, no-caste" certificate, she faced barriers to inheriting her parental property, violating her fundamental right to belief under Article 25 of the Indian Constitution.
