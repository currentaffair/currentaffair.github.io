Landmark Press Freedom Cases

Romesh Thapar v. State of Madras (1950)

Why in News? The case set a precedent for free speech under Article 19(1)(a), curbing arbitrary state powers and shaping India's press freedom laws.

Key Facts

  • Background: The Madras government banned the magazine CrossRoads for reporting on police violence, which the SC later ruled unconstitutional.
  • Supreme Court Verdict: The SC declared the Madras Maintenance of Public Order Act unconstitutional, limiting arbitrary censorship and linking speech restrictions to "state security."
  • First Constitutional Amendment (1951): In response, the amendment added "reasonable restrictions" to free speech, covering public order and incitement to offense.

Other Landmark Cases

  • Brij Bhushan v. State of Delhi (1950): SC struck down prior censorship on a magazine, reinforcing no censorship without clear public order threats.
  • Sakal Papers Ltd vs Union of India (1961): SC invalidated restrictions on newspaper pricing, reinforcing press freedom under Article 19(1)(a).
  • Bennett Coleman & Co. v. Union of India (1973): SC struck down restrictions on newspaper page limits, affirming that such limitations violate press freedom.
  • Indian Express Newspapers v. Union of India (1985): SC invalidated excessive taxation on newspapers to curb press freedom, emphasizing the importance of free expression.
  • Shreya Singhal vs Union of India (2015): SC struck down Section 66A of the IT Act, decrying vague and broad laws restricting free speech online.
