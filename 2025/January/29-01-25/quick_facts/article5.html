Understanding Party Whip

The Vice President of India recently expressed concerns about the impact of party whips in Parliament, questioning how they may limit the independent judgment of elected representatives.

What is a Party Whip?

A whip is a directive issued by a political party to its members in the legislature, instructing them on how to vote on specific bills or resolutions. It also refers to the designated party member responsible for ensuring attendance and adherence to these directives.

Objective of a Party Whip

The main goals of a whip are to maintain party discipline, ensure uniformity in decision-making, and avoid defections or dissent within the party.

Constitutional Status

The whip is not mentioned in the Constitution, House Rules, or any parliamentary statute, but is based on parliamentary conventions.

Types of Whips
  • One-Line Whip: Informs members of an important vote, allowing abstention without penalty.
  • Two-Line Whip: Mandates presence during a vote but does not require a specific vote.
  • Three-Line Whip: The strictest form, requiring attendance and alignment with the party's vote directive.
Functions of a Whip
  • Ensures Attendance: Ensures members' presence and maintains quorum.
  • Secures Support: Helps gather support for or against specific issues.
  • Maintains Discipline: Ensures adherence to party guidelines.
  • Identifies Discontent: Monitors signs of discontent among MPs and reports them.
  • Internal Party Coordination: Acts as a unifying force for party cohesion in Parliament.
Violation of the Whip

If MPs or MLAs defy the party's whip, they may face expulsion under the Anti-Defection Law, unless more than two-thirds dissent, causing a split within the party.

Quorum in Parliament

The quorum refers to the minimum number of members required for a session to be valid, as specified in Article 100 of the Indian Constitution. The quorum is one-tenth of the total members: 55 for Lok Sabha and 25 for Rajya Sabha.
