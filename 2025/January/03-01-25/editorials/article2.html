India’s Climate Change Response and Progress

Introduction

The announcement by the India Meteorological Department (IMD) that 2024 is projected to be the hottest year since 1901 underscores the urgent need for global attention on climate change. India’s vulnerability to extreme weather events, such as heatwaves and floods, calls for a comprehensive response to mitigate the economic and environmental impacts. In this context, India’s progress on meeting its climate goals becomes crucial in the face of challenges posed by climate change.

India’s Progress on Climate Goals

India has made strides in fulfilling its international climate commitments, with several initiatives aimed at addressing climate risks. The Climate Risk Information System, set up by the Reserve Bank of India (RBI), is an important step in better understanding regional climate vulnerabilities. This system focuses on local-level scenarios, which is crucial given the varying climate risks across different regions of India.

Challenges: Despite India’s progress, global climate goals have not been fully met, especially by Western countries, which have historically contributed more to global warming.

Impact of Climate Change in India

Economy: Over 30% of India’s GDP comes from sectors highly dependent on nature, such as agriculture, water utilities, and construction, all of which are vulnerable to climate change.

Agriculture: Crop yields are expected to decline by 16% over the next five years, which could reduce the GDP by over 2.5%.

Infrastructure: Around 50% of India’s infrastructure is at risk due to extreme weather events.

Financial Stability: Global warming could potentially destabilize financial markets, as pointed out by the RBI.

Challenge: One of the main hurdles in addressing climate risks is the lack of a unified system for quantifying climate vulnerabilities. The fragmented nature of data collection and varying metrics further complicates efforts.

Shortcomings of the Current System

Despite the progress, the current climate resilience systems in India face several challenges. Climate risks like floods, heatwaves, and droughts are often overlooked in political agendas and development plans. This neglect is evident in issues such as outdated drainage systems and civic planning that disregards local hydrology. The country also faces a reactive approach to disaster management, rather than one that anticipates and prepares for these risks in advance.

Role of Political Leadership

The political leadership in India has often downplayed warnings from key institutions, including the IMD and RBI. Environmental issues such as floods, landslides, and air quality do not feature prominently in election agendas. This gap needs to be addressed by shifting the focus towards integrating climate resilience into national policies and long-term development plans.

Way Forward: Recommended Strategies

Immediate Actions

  • Develop and implement heat action plans to combat rising temperatures.
  • Construct sea walls and update drainage systems to manage extreme weather.
  • Improve disaster management systems and weather-alert mechanisms.

Long-term Measures

  • Invest in climate-proof agricultural techniques such as advanced irrigation systems.
  • Enhance infrastructure to withstand the impacts of extreme weather events.
  • Improve data collection and local-level climate risk quantification.

Conclusion

The need for urgent, sustainable, and inclusive climate policies in India has never been greater. With the country being the most populous in the world, ensuring climate resilience will not only safeguard India’s economy and infrastructure but will also set an example for global climate action.
