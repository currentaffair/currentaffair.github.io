Core Technologies: The Path to India's Technological Future

Introduction

India has repeatedly demonstrated its capability to develop groundbreaking core technologies, yet the nation has struggled to commercialize these breakthroughs. From the Raman Effect in 1930 to modern semiconductor technology, India's scientific achievements are impressive, but the translation of these innovations into market-leading products has been a challenge. The historical disconnect between academia, industry, and government has left India reliant on foreign imports for critical technologies, despite its potential to develop them independently. This gap between scientific excellence and commercial success remains a significant hurdle, and addressing it is crucial for India's vision of becoming a technology-led, developed economy.

Understanding Core Technologies

Core Technology (Core Tech) refers to the foundational and advanced technologies that are essential to drive innovation and growth across various industries, economies, and national priorities. These technologies form the backbone of critical sectors and offer the potential to reshape the future of economies and societies.

Key Characteristics of Core Tech

  • Foundational Nature: Core tech serves as the building blocks for other technologies and applications.
  • Wide Applicability: These technologies have a broad range of applications, including defense, healthcare, education, and manufacturing.
  • Strategic Importance: Core tech determines a nation's economic, military, and geopolitical power.

Examples of Core Tech

  • Semiconductors: Critical for electronics, computing, and AI.
  • Artificial Intelligence (AI): Drives automation, machine learning, and natural language processing.
  • Quantum Computing: Harnesses quantum mechanics for unprecedented computational power.
  • Cybersecurity: Protects digital infrastructure and sensitive data.
  • Space Technology: Enables space exploration and satellite communications.
  • Advanced Manufacturing: Includes 3D printing, robotics, and Industry 4.0 technologies.
  • Green Technologies: Includes renewable energy solutions like solar power and wind turbines.
  • Telecommunications: Enabling 5G and next-generation communication systems.

Why Investing in Core Technologies is Crucial for India

Economic Growth and Global Competitiveness

Investment in core technologies such as AI, semiconductors, and quantum computing is essential for India's economic growth. These technologies spur innovation, improve productivity, and create high-value jobs. The global AI market is expected to reach $900 billion by 2030, with India poised to capture a significant share through initiatives like the National AI Mission. Similarly, the Production-Linked Incentive (PLI) scheme for semiconductors aims to make India a global chip manufacturing hub, addressing a $110 billion semiconductor market deficit by 2030.

Strengthening National Security

Technologies such as cybersecurity, AI-driven surveillance, and drone technology are vital for national security. In 2022, CERT-In reported tackling over 1.39 million cybersecurity incidents, emphasizing the need for indigenous cybersecurity solutions. The Defense Ministry's collaboration with private firms under the iDEX initiative has led to advances in drone technology, enhancing security capabilities along critical borders.

Reducing Technological Dependence

India's dependence on foreign imports for critical technologies, such as semiconductors and advanced electronics, hampers its strategic autonomy. By investing in domestic R&D and manufacturing, India can reduce this reliance. For instance, the Semicon India program, inaugurated in 2023, aims to make India self-sufficient in chip production.

Building a Knowledge-Based Society

Investment in core technologies can transform education, healthcare, and governance. Initiatives like Digital India have brought rural populations online, improving access to e-learning and telemedicine services. AI-driven tools are also helping predict disease outbreaks and improve agricultural productivity.

Geopolitical Leverage

Strategic investments in core technologies strengthen India's global position. Partnerships such as the India-US iCET initiative and semiconductor collaborations with the Quad countries underscore the growing importance of technology in shaping international relations and positioning India as a key player in global supply chains.

Challenges in Advancing Core Technologies in India

Despite its potential, India faces several challenges in advancing core technologies. These include:

Inadequate R&D Investment

India spends just 0.65% of its GDP on research and development, well below the global average. This limited investment stifles innovation in critical sectors like AI and semiconductors, and reduces the ability of the private sector to collaborate with academia.

Shortage of Industry-Ready Workforce

India faces a skills gap, with only 51% of graduates employable, according to the Economic Survey 2023-24. This shortage of skilled professionals hampers the growth of emerging sectors like AI and quantum computing, limiting India's ability to compete globally.

Dependence on Imports

India's reliance on imported technologies, particularly semiconductors, limits its strategic autonomy. The COVID-19 pandemic highlighted the risks of such dependence when global supply chains were disrupted, affecting industries across India.

Conclusion

To realize its vision of becoming a technology-driven economy, India must address the barriers hindering the growth of core technologies. By increasing R&D investment, improving workforce skills, reducing reliance on imports, and strengthening public-private collaborations, India can foster innovation and build a robust technology ecosystem. These efforts will contribute to economic growth, national security, sustainability, and geopolitical influence, positioning India as a global leader in technological innovation.
