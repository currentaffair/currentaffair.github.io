2025 as Year of Reforms for Defence Force

Introduction

In a significant move, the Ministry of Defence has designated 2025 as the ‘Year of Reforms’ to enhance the operational efficiency and technological prowess of India's Armed Forces. The goal is to ensure that the forces are prepared for multi-domain, integrated operations by incorporating cutting-edge technologies and fostering jointness among services. This move is part of the broader vision to strengthen India's national defense in response to emerging security challenges.

Key Areas for Reform

Several key areas have been identified for reform in 2025, focusing on integration, technology, and indigenous production:

Jointness & Integration

The reform agenda stresses strengthening cooperation among the Army, Navy, and Air Force through the establishment of Integrated Theatre Commands (ITCs). The ITCs will be strategically placed to focus on key regions such as the northern command in Lucknow, the western command in Jaipur, and the maritime command in Thiruvananthapuram. Joint operational capabilities will be developed through collaborative training and tactical coordination.

Emerging Technologies

The armed forces will focus on technologies such as Artificial Intelligence (AI), Machine Learning (ML), Cyber Warfare, Hypersonics, and Robotics. These technologies will be integrated into military strategies to ensure that India remains at the forefront of modern warfare. Additionally, fostering public-private partnerships will facilitate knowledge transfer and accelerate the development of defence technologies.

Simplifying Acquisition

The Ministry will streamline defense procurement processes, making them more time-efficient and less bureaucratic. This will ensure that the armed forces are equipped with the necessary technology and hardware to stay prepared for future conflicts.

Defence Exporter

India aims to transition from being a major importer of defense technology to a credible exporter. In recent years, India's defense exports have grown significantly, from Rs 2,000 crore in 2014 to over Rs 21,000 crore. By 2028, India targets a defense export worth Rs 50,000 crore.

Veteran Welfare and Indigenous Culture

Veterans will be provided with enhanced welfare measures, while indigenous capabilities will be promoted to instill pride and boost global confidence in India’s defense sector.

Current Status of India's Defence Forces

India has made substantial progress in its defense sector over the past decade. From being the largest importer of arms, India has made significant strides to become a defense exporter. The country’s defense production turnover increased by more than 45% from 2016 to 2023. The private sector’s involvement in defense production also grew, with companies like TATA establishing India's first private sector final assembly line for military aircraft.

Challenges in Reforming the Defence Forces

Despite the progress, there are several challenges that need to be addressed:

Lack of National Security Strategy

India currently lacks a cohesive National Security Strategy (NSS), which leads to gaps between military and political objectives. This undermines the country’s readiness against emerging threats, particularly from China and Pakistan.

Cyber Warfare Threats

Cybersecurity has become a new domain of warfare, with state-sponsored attacks targeting critical infrastructure. This vulnerability needs to be addressed to ensure national security.

Dependence on Imports

Despite India’s progress in defense manufacturing, the country still relies heavily on imports for sophisticated defense systems. The slow pace of indigenization and insufficient infrastructure continue to be significant challenges.

Cultural Resistance to Jointness

Each branch of the military (Army, Navy, Air Force) operates independently, which has created cultural resistance to integrated operations. This needs to change to enhance operational efficiency.

Ad-hoc Procurement Processes

Emergency procurements, though necessary, have highlighted India’s lack of preparedness in certain areas. There is a need for more strategic, long-term procurement processes.

Way Forward: Institutional and Technological Reforms

To fully realize the vision for 2025 as the ‘Year of Reforms,’ several steps need to be taken:

Institutional Reforms

The establishment of the Chief of Defence Staff (CDS) and Department of Military Affairs (DMA) will help streamline defense decision-making. The CDS will serve as the key figure to bridge the gap between civilian leadership and military personnel.

Technological Integration

Integrating autonomous systems, AI, and cyber warfare capabilities will help India maintain a technological edge over adversaries. Expanding drone capabilities and enhancing intelligence, surveillance, and reconnaissance (ISR) will provide operational flexibility in potential conflicts.

Boost Domestic Defence Industry

Fostering public-private partnerships and increasing foreign collaborations will help build a self-reliant and competitive defense sector. India should prioritize technologies that offer asymmetrical advantages in future conflicts.

Maximize Defence Cooperation

Expanding defense cooperation with countries like the United States will help bolster India’s strategic autonomy and security. Regional partnerships are also essential to strengthen defense readiness.

Conclusion

India’s defense sector is on the verge of a major transformation, with the ‘Year of Reforms’ set to usher in a new era of technological advancements and military integration. However, challenges such as cultural resistance and the slow pace of indigenization remain. By addressing these issues and promoting jointness and innovation, India can build a defense force that is capable of handling future threats while fostering global strategic alliances.
