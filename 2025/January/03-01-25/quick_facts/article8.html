The Panama Canal Dispute

The Panama Canal dispute arose from U.S. President-elect Donald Trump's controversial statements regarding the canal, citing unfair treatment of U.S. interests, specifically concerns over transit fees and Chinese influence.

Trump's Concerns

In 2023, droughts reduced shipping slots by 36%, causing Panama Canal Authority to increase fees, affecting U.S. vessels. Trump also expressed concerns over Panama’s growing ties with China, fearing operational influence and surveillance.

Historical Context

The Panama Canal, crucial for global trade, was controlled by the U.S. until 1999 when it was handed over to Panama under the Torrijos-Carter Treaties, which declared the canal neutral.

Panama's Response

President José Raúl Mulino rejected Trump’s claims, emphasizing Panama’s sovereignty and denying allegations of undue Chinese influence or military presence near the canal.

China's Position

China's Foreign Ministry dismissed Trump’s accusations, affirming Panama’s sovereignty and the canal's neutral status, which is essential for international trade.

Potential Developments

Diplomatic negotiations are anticipated to address U.S. concerns over fees and Chinese involvement. Panama may seek international support to reinforce its sovereignty and the neutrality of the canal.

Global Implications

The canal is vital for global trade, handling 6% of maritime trade. Tensions may affect U.S.-China-Panama relations and broader geopolitical dynamics in Latin America.
