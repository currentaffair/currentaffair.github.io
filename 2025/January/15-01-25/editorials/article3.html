Landslide and Preventive Measures

Introduction

The Wayanad district in Kerala was struck by a severe landslide in July 2024, triggered by extreme rainfall and fragile ecological conditions. A study published in *Nature Natural Hazards* analyzed this disaster to highlight the importance of implementing more effective disaster management strategies in regions susceptible to landslides.

Key Facts Regarding the Study

The study focused on understanding the behavior of rapid debris flows, utilizing advanced techniques such as run-out modelling and the Rapid Mass Movement Simulation (RAMMS) to track debris flow paths. These findings aim to enhance risk assessment and help devise preventative measures for future disasters.

Important Findings

  • Significant debris accumulation was observed at lower elevations of slopes, which poses a future hazard downstream.
  • Vulnerability mapping is essential for identifying high-risk zones, especially in areas prone to rapid debris flows.
  • The 2024 landslide mirrored the events of previous disasters, including the deadly 1984 landslide in the same region.

What Are Landslides?

Landslides are a natural disaster characterized by the downward movement of rocks, soil, and debris on a slope, often triggered by rainfall, earthquakes, volcanic activity, or human interventions. There are several types of landslides:

  • Slides: Movement along a rupture surface.
  • Flows: Mixture of soil and water moving like a fluid.
  • Spreads: Lateral extension and cracking due to liquefaction.
  • Topples: Forward rotation and free-fall from steep slopes.
  • Falls: Free-fall of detached material from cliffs.

Landslide-Prone Areas in India

India has significant areas prone to landslides, particularly in the Himalayan and Western Ghats regions. The *Landslide Atlas of India* estimates that around 12.6% of India's land area is vulnerable to landslides, including regions in the North East Himalaya, Western Ghats, and Konkan hills.

What Are the Causes of Landslides?

Landslides occur due to several factors, including:

  • Gravitational Forces: When the pull of gravity overcomes the material's strength, it triggers landslides.
  • Natural Factors: Heavy rainfall, earthquakes, and water seepage.
  • Anthropogenic Factors: Deforestation, mining, and urban development.
  • Geological Factors: Composition and structure of the terrain.

Comparison: Himalayan vs. Western Ghats Landslides

The characteristics of landslides in the Himalayan region differ from those in the Western Ghats. The Himalayas are more tectonically active, with steep slopes and high-altitude terrain that are more prone to landslides due to seismic activity. In contrast, the Western Ghats have less tectonic activity but experience heavy rainfall during the monsoon season, making them vulnerable to rainfall-triggered landslides.

Government Initiatives in India

The Indian government has implemented various initiatives to mitigate landslide risks, including:

  • National Landslide Risk Management Strategy (2019)
  • Landslide Risk Mitigation Scheme (LRMS)
  • Flood Risk Mitigation Scheme (FRMS)
  • National Guidelines on Landslides and Snow Avalanches

Preventive Measures for Landslides

To prevent landslides, both engineering and natural methods can be applied:

  • Engineering Solutions: Slope stabilization, grading, terracing, and drainage systems.
  • Natural Solutions: Vegetation control and effective water management practices.
  • Early Warning Systems: Monitoring rainfall, soil moisture, and using technology like LiDAR for early detection.

Conclusion

Improved disaster management strategies are essential to mitigate landslide risks, particularly in vulnerable areas like Wayanad. By utilizing a combination of engineering solutions, natural methods, and early warning systems, we can reduce the impact of landslides and enhance resilience against climate change-induced rainfall extremes.
