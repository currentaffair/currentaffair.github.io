Topic: Disputes Related to the Indus Water Treaty

Introduction

The Indus Waters Treaty (IWT) is one of the oldest and most significant water-sharing agreements between India and Pakistan. Signed in 1960 under the World Bank's mediation, the treaty divides the waters of the Indus River system between the two countries. It ensures that India and Pakistan share the water of the rivers Indus, Jhelum, Chenab, Sutlej, Beas, and Ravi, though disputes and challenges related to its implementation have persisted, particularly regarding the Kishanganga and Ratle hydroelectric projects. This article examines the ongoing disputes, the challenges in the treaty's current framework, and the way forward.

Key Disputes Regarding the Indus Waters Treaty

Water Sharing Dispute

The Kishanganga Hydroelectric Project, located on the Kishanganga River (a tributary of Jhelum) in Jammu and Kashmir (J&K), is at the center of a water-sharing dispute. Pakistan has raised concerns, claiming that India's diversion of water for power generation violates the terms of the IWT.

Similarly, the Ratle Hydroelectric Project, located on the Chenab River in J&K, has caused friction. Pakistan has objected to the dam's design, claiming that it grants India excessive control over river flow, especially in relation to the spillway gates.

Dispute Over Resolution Procedure

Pakistan initially requested the appointment of a Neutral Expert (NE) under the IWT in 2015 to resolve disputes related to both projects. However, Pakistan later sought adjudication through the Permanent Court of Arbitration (PCA), a step India opposed, arguing that the IWT’s dispute resolution framework prioritizes the NE. In 2022, the World Bank initiated both NE and PCA processes. India chose to engage with the NE, asserting that only the NE had the authority to resolve disputes under the treaty.

The Indus Waters Treaty: Overview

The IWT is a historic agreement between India and Pakistan, intended to regulate the use of the Indus River system's water resources. It was signed in 1960 and mediated by the World Bank. The treaty allocates the water of the Indus, Jhelum, and Chenab rivers to Pakistan, while India has access to the Beas, Ravi, and Sutlej rivers.

Key Provisions of the Treaty

The treaty allows India to use the waters of the three eastern rivers (Beas, Ravi, and Sutlej) without restrictions, while Pakistan is allocated the waters of the western rivers (Chenab, Indus, and Jhelum) with certain provisions for India’s use for non-consumptive purposes such as irrigation and hydropower generation.

Permanent Indus Commission

The treaty established the Permanent Indus Commission (PIC), a bilateral body responsible for overseeing the treaty’s implementation. The commission meets annually to address any issues related to the usage of the rivers and ensures both parties comply with the agreement.

Dispute Resolution Mechanism

Article IX of the IWT outlines a three-tier dispute resolution mechanism. Initially, disputes are addressed by the PIC. If unresolved, the matter can be referred to a Neutral Expert appointed by the World Bank. In cases where the issue extends beyond the NE’s jurisdiction, a Court of Arbitration, also established by the World Bank, can be called upon.

Challenges with the Indus Waters Treaty

Outdated Provisions

The IWT was drafted in 1960, and much has changed in the region since then. For instance, climate change has significantly altered the hydrological patterns in the Indus basin. Glacial melt, erratic rainfall, and increased evaporation have reduced water availability, creating challenges for the treaty, which was designed based on historical water flow data.

Lack of Flexibility

The rigid water-sharing arrangement under the treaty limits flexibility, preventing adaptive water management practices that could address new environmental and geopolitical challenges.

Geopolitical Tensions

Geopolitical tensions between India and Pakistan further complicate cooperation under the IWT. The mistrust between the two countries has led to challenges in negotiating water-sharing agreements and resolving disputes amicably.

Way Forward

Renegotiation of the Treaty

There is an urgent need to revisit the treaty and incorporate provisions that account for climate change, water scarcity, and evolving hydrological patterns. This would ensure that the treaty remains relevant in the face of new challenges.

Enhanced Dialogue and Trust-Building

India and Pakistan must prioritize dialogue and work on building trust to resolve their differences over the Indus waters. The revival of the Permanent Indus Commission could play a critical role in facilitating communication and cooperation.

Third-Party Mediation

Neutral parties such as the World Bank can act as mediators, facilitating negotiations and ensuring that both countries comply with the treaty’s provisions. Such mediation could reduce the risk of escalating disputes.

Focus on Technical Solutions

Both India and Pakistan should adopt a technical and scientific approach to resolving disputes related to the hydroelectric projects. Joint studies and data sharing could help mitigate tensions and promote cooperative water management practices.

While the Indus Waters Treaty has provided a framework for water-sharing between India and Pakistan for over six decades, its relevance in the current geopolitical and environmental landscape is being questioned. The ongoing disputes surrounding the Kishanganga and Ratle projects highlight the need for a revised approach that can address modern challenges such as climate change and resource scarcity. Both countries must engage in dialogue, supported by neutral third-party mediation, to ensure a sustainable and cooperative management of the Indus waters.
