Misuse of Section 152 of the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita: A Critical Analysis

Introduction

The Rajasthan High Court, in the case of Tejender Pal Singh v. State of Rajasthan (2024), raised concerns over the misuse of Section 152 of the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS). This section criminalizes acts that incite secession, armed rebellion, subversive activities, and endanger India's sovereignty, unity, and integrity. While the Supreme Court had suspended criminal proceedings under Section 124A (sedition) of the IPC in 2022, Section 152 presents a different set of challenges due to its vague definitions and potential for misuse in stifling free speech.

Key Issues with Section 152

Vagueness in Definition

One of the major criticisms of Section 152 is the lack of clarity in its wording, particularly around the terms "sovereignty, unity, and integrity of India." The absence of precise definitions leaves the law open to wide interpretation, allowing authorities to use it expansively, potentially curbing legitimate dissent. For instance, the term "unity" could be interpreted in various ways, leading to arbitrary actions against individuals without concrete evidence of subversive intent.

Lower Threshold for Offence

Section 152 uses the term "knowingly," which significantly lowers the threshold for prosecution. For example, sharing a social media post, even without the intent to incite prohibited activities, can lead to prosecution. This could result in wrongful arrests, particularly as the section is cognizable and non-bailable, allowing immediate arrests without substantial evidence.

The potential for misuse of Section 152 has been highlighted by past data. Between 2015 and 2020, there were 548 arrests under Section 124A IPC (sedition), but only 12 convictions, showing a pattern of overreach by law enforcement under similar provisions.

Chilling Effect on Free Speech

Section 152 raises concerns about the chilling effect it can have on free speech. The law does not require a direct causal link between speech and its impact, which means that individuals can be deprived of their liberty without clear evidence that their words caused harm. This undermines the fundamental democratic principle that free speech is essential for societal progress and open debate.

Judicial Precedents and Free Speech

Balwant Singh v. State of Punjab (1995)

In this case, the Supreme Court distinguished between casual sloganeering and acts with real consequences. The court emphasized that free speech should not be equated with actions that directly harm public order.

Javed Ahmad Hazam v. State of Maharashtra (2024)

The Court stated that the impact of speech should be judged from the perspective of reasonable, strong-minded individuals. It cautioned against interpreting words from the perspective of weak or vacillating minds, which could lead to over-broad applications of the law.

Kedar Nath Singh v. State of Bihar (1962)

The Court drew a clear distinction between disloyalty to the state and the strong criticism of government policies. This case reinforces the principle that criticism of government actions should not be equated with sedition or threats to national security.

Way Forward and Need for Guidelines

Given the potential for misuse of Section 152, it is imperative for the Supreme Court to issue guidelines that clearly define the scope and application of this section. Much like in the case of D.K. Basu v. State of West Bengal (1997), where the Court issued guidelines for arrests, a similar framework for interpreting Section 152 could help prevent its abuse.

Enforcement authorities must ensure that there is a direct causal nexus between speech and its consequences before prosecuting individuals under Section 152. This will safeguard against wrongful arrests and help protect the fundamental right to free speech.

Importance of Free Speech in a Democratic Society

Free speech is one of the cornerstones of a democratic society. The "marketplace of ideas" theory, as articulated by Justice Holmes in Abrams v. United States, posits that truth emerges from open debate, where ideas can be freely expressed without fear of repression. Limiting free speech, especially in the age of social media, poses a grave threat to the democratic fabric of the nation.

In conclusion, while Section 152 BNS aims to protect national sovereignty and integrity, its vague language and lower threshold for prosecution can lead to significant misuse, stifling free speech and dissent. Clear judicial guidelines and a careful balance between national security and individual rights are essential for safeguarding democratic freedoms in India.
