The Eklavya Model Residential Schools (EMRS) Scheme: Challenges and Solutions

Introduction

The Eklavya Model Residential Schools (EMRS) scheme was launched to provide quality education to Scheduled Tribe (ST) students in remote areas, spanning from Class VI to XII. Despite the significant budget allocations and efforts to ensure quality education, many challenges persist. Notably, around one-third of the 716 approved EMRS remain non-functional, and dropout rates, especially among Particularly Vulnerable Tribal Groups (PVTGs), underscore the barriers tribal communities face in accessing education.

Issues with Eklavya Model Schools (EMRS)

1. Infrastructure Gaps

As of now, 240 EMRS are non-functional due to land issues, lack of hostels, and absence of basic facilities. Many existing schools face significant infrastructure challenges such as:

  • Insufficient drinking water, electricity, and functional toilets.
  • Lack of internet access, with only 53.9% of schools connected to the internet, hampering access to modern learning tools.
Key Statistic: 240 schools are non-functional primarily due to land and infrastructure issues.

2. Teacher Shortages and Retention Issues

EMRS face acute shortages of qualified teachers, especially subject specialists. A significant number of teachers are hired on a temporary or contractual basis, leading to:

  • Inconsistent teaching quality due to high turnover rates.
  • Language barriers, as many teachers are not proficient in local tribal dialects, which affects their ability to communicate effectively with students.

3. High Dropout Rates Among PVTGs

One of the most concerning issues is the high dropout rate, particularly among students from PVTGs. The dropout rates tend to rise significantly as students move to higher classes, with only half of those enrolled in Class VI continuing to Class XII. In states such as Madhya Pradesh, Gujarat, and Chhattisgarh, the enrolment of PVTGs is well below the targeted 5% quota.

Key Statistic: Dropout rates increase as students progress from Class VI to XII, with only 12,480 students remaining by the end of Class XII.

4. Socio-Economic Barriers

Poverty, landlessness, and migration for work contribute to the struggles faced by PVTGs in continuing their education. Factors such as:

  • Seasonal migration disrupting the academic year.
  • Illiterate parents who cannot offer adequate support to their children.
  • Lack of awareness about the importance of education leading to lower enrolment.

Reasons Behind the Dropout Rates

1. Economic Pressures

The economic pressures faced by tribal communities, particularly the need for seasonal migration and the necessity to work for daily wages, often force children to drop out of school and contribute to the family's income.

2. Language Barrier

Many tribal students face difficulty in comprehending the medium of instruction, which is often bilingual, and teachers are often not fluent in the local tribal dialects. This communication gap hampers their learning progress.

3. Entrance Exam Challenges

Admissions to EMRS are based on competitive exams, and PVTG students, who often lack preparatory resources, find these exams difficult to clear, putting them at a disadvantage.

Policy Gaps and Governance Issues

1. Ineffective Implementation of PVTG Quota

The 5% PVTG quota for EMRS remains underutilized, especially in states like Madhya Pradesh, Gujarat, and Chhattisgarh, where the allocated quota is not fully achieved.

2. Lack of Monitoring and Accountability

The National Educational Society for Tribal Students (NESTS), established in 2019, has limited capacity to oversee operations, and poor coordination at the state level results in inconsistent reporting on school performance and enrolment data.

3. Budget Utilization and Allocation

Despite increased budget allocations for tribal welfare, the funds often remain underutilized due to poor execution. The Tribal Sub-Plan (TSP) has not effectively addressed the gaps in secondary and higher secondary education.

Potential Solutions

1. Improving Infrastructure

Before approving new schools, prioritize ensuring functional infrastructure in existing schools, including basic amenities such as clean drinking water, electricity, and internet access.

2. Local Teacher Recruitment

Recruit teachers from local tribal communities to overcome language barriers and offer incentives for teachers to stay and work in remote areas.

3. Revising Admission Process

Modify the entrance exam pattern to be more inclusive and provide preparatory resources for PVTG students to better equip them for exams.

4. Strengthening Awareness and Community Support

Community outreach programs to increase awareness about the importance of education can help improve enrolment rates and reduce dropout rates. Engaging parents in the educational process is also crucial.

Conclusion

The EMRS scheme holds significant potential to address educational disparities among tribal communities. However, persistent challenges such as high dropout rates, non-functional schools, and infrastructure gaps need urgent reforms. By addressing systemic issues like teacher shortages, infrastructure development, and socio-economic barriers, India can make significant strides toward achieving its educational goals.
