Supreme Court Judgment on Accessibility and the Rights of Persons with Disabilities

Introduction

The Supreme Court's recent judgment in the case of Rajive Raturi v. Union of India (2024) has had significant implications for the rights of persons with disabilities in India. This landmark ruling addressed the deficiencies in the existing accessibility guidelines that were found to violate the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (RPwD) Act, 2016. The judgment focused on the mandatory nature of accessibility requirements for public infrastructure and the need for more robust and comprehensive guidelines to protect the rights of persons with disabilities.

Background

Case Context

The Supreme Court held Rule 15 of the RPwD Rules, 2017, as violative of the RPwD Act, 2016. Rule 15 allowed discretion for accessibility measures, which conflicted with the Act's requirement for mandatory compliance. The Court emphasized the necessity of clear and enforceable rules to ensure the accessibility of public spaces, transport, and services for people with disabilities.

Relevant Sections of the RPwD Act

The following sections of the RPwD Act were key to the judgment:

  • Section 40: Accessibility standards for public buildings, services, etc.
  • Section 44: Accessibility in transport.
  • Section 45: Accessibility in information and communication technology.
  • Section 46: Accessibility in various services and facilities.
  • Section 89: Power of the government to make rules.

Issues with the Existing Guidelines

Discretionary Nature of the Guidelines

The existing accessibility guidelines allowed various ministries to exercise discretion in their implementation. This flexibility, while offering some room for adaptation, led to fragmented efforts without a unified framework, hindering the progress of accessibility standards across India.

Accessibility vs. Reasonable Accommodation

While accessibility refers to creating universally accessible environments from the start, reasonable accommodation offers context-specific solutions for individuals. Both concepts complement each other, and a balanced approach to both is crucial in fostering inclusive environments.

Technological Developments and Evolving Accessibility Concepts

With advancements in artificial intelligence (AI), Internet of Things (IoT), and other technologies, accessibility is no longer just about physical spaces but also includes digital environments. The need for adaptive and dynamic accessibility tools is increasing, and the Court has highlighted the importance of setting minimum standards that evolve over time.

Phased Realization of Accessibility

The Court recommended adopting a phased approach to accessibility, where periodic updates to the accessibility standards will reflect the current state of technological and societal progress. A roadmap similar to Canada's 2040 full accessibility goal was suggested as a model.

Universal Design and Inclusivity

Universal design principles aim to make spaces, products, and services accessible to all, including vulnerable groups like women, children, and the elderly. The definition of disability itself should expand to include environmental factors, recognizing that accessibility challenges are not solely individual but also societal and infrastructural.

Compliance Through Social Audits

Social audits are essential for monitoring the implementation of accessibility standards, but current guidelines lack uniformity in their execution. Training auditors and enhancing awareness are necessary steps to ensure effective and consistent audits across states and regions.

Simplifying Accessibility Rules

Current bureaucratic complexities, overlapping guidelines, and fragmented mandates from various ministries have made it difficult for businesses and public authorities to comply with accessibility requirements. The new rules must be clear, concise, and practical, ensuring ease of implementation while maintaining effectiveness.

Deadline for New Guidelines

The government has been given a deadline of February 2025 to finalize and implement new accessibility guidelines that will be mandatory for both public and private sectors. These guidelines will address not only social services but also sectors such as finance, transport, and technology, ensuring widespread accessibility across the economy.

Conclusion

The Supreme Court judgment has brought much-needed attention to the gaps in India's accessibility standards and the need for immediate, enforceable guidelines. While progress has been made, the challenge lies in ensuring comprehensive implementation across all sectors, from public services to private businesses. The upcoming deadline for new guidelines presents a critical opportunity to make India a more accessible and inclusive society for persons with disabilities.
