India’s Draft Digital Personal Data Protection Rules: A Comprehensive Overview

Introduction

On January 3, 2025, the Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology (MeitY) released the much-anticipated Draft Digital Personal Data Protection (DPDP) Rules — a key moment in India’s journey to regulate digital personal data. This step follows the passage of the DPDP Act, 2023, bringing India closer to operationalising its framework for safeguarding personal data.

Departure from the Personal Data Protection Bill

The draft rules represent a departure from the earlier and controversial Personal Data Protection Bill, which many deemed was overly restrictive and even hostile to industry interests. The Bill underwent extensive framing, reframing, and consultations over nearly a decade before being rescinded. The DPDP Act reflects a less prescriptive, principles-based approach, which has been largely well received.

Refreshing Alternative to the EU's Policies

India’s approach contrasts with the European Union's interventionist policies, offering a more flexible and business-friendly alternative to the GDPR. The GDPR, despite its early acclaim, has faced criticism for stifling smaller enterprises and failing to significantly enhance public trust in the Internet.

The Hits: Pragmatism and Flexibility

One of the standout features of the draft rules is their simplicity and clarity, particularly around notice and consent. Unlike the cumbersome requirements of the GDPR, such as cross-border data transfers and automated decision-making, India’s rules focus on empowering users without overwhelming them with unnecessary details. This approach helps reduce consent fatigue, a significant issue in Europe.

India’s Approach to User Rights and Consent Mechanisms

India sidesteps the pitfalls of prescriptive regulation by focusing on outcomes rather than processes, empowering users without drowning businesses in complexity. The rules also respect business autonomy, avoiding the detailed prescriptions of the GDPR about user interfaces and how rights should be exercised.

Protection of Children’s Personal Data

The draft rules provide stricter protection for children's personal data, but also allow for exemptions for certain industries, such as educational institutions, healthcare providers, and child-care centers. This reflects a nuanced understanding of industry-specific needs, ensuring a balance between protection and practical utility.

The Misses: Data Localisation and Overreach

Despite the positive features, the draft rules are not without their flaws. There is ambiguity around cross-border data flow restrictions and localisation mandates for Significant Data Fiduciaries (SDFs), which could create regulatory arbitrage and investment deterrence. While localisation is important for security, it must be carefully balanced with economic competitiveness.

Localisation Provision and Law Enforcement Challenges

The localisation provisions stem from law enforcement agencies' challenges in accessing cross-border data for investigations. However, a more sector-specific approach, similar to the Reserve Bank of India’s 2018 data localisation mandate, may offer a more balanced solution.

Areas Requiring Greater Clarity

Some areas still require greater clarity, such as safeguards for verifying user requests and the handling of excessive or incessant requests. Additionally, there is ambiguity regarding the government’s access to sensitive business data, which needs to be addressed to protect intellectual property.

What Lies Ahead

India must move beyond reliance on notice-and-consent mechanisms, which work well in controlled environments but are impractical in public spaces. As India progresses, it should consider innovative frameworks that balance privacy with convenience, particularly in the age of IoT, 5G, and AI.

Conclusion

With the increasing use of IoT, 5G, and AI technologies, India must design privacy frameworks that do not solely rely on the outdated principle of consent. As the draft rules undergo further refinement, it is crucial to maintain flexibility and industry-specific accommodations to balance innovation with individual rights.
