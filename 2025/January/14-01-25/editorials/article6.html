Groundwater Crisis in India: Challenges and Solutions

Introduction

India is facing a critical groundwater crisis, exacerbated by excessive extraction for irrigation, rapid urbanization, and industrial contamination. With the country's population rising steadily, there has been an increasing demand for groundwater, especially in agriculture. This situation is further complicated by the unpredictable climate and irregular monsoons. In this article, we will explore the current scenario of groundwater usage in India, the causes behind its depletion, government initiatives, and potential solutions for sustainable management.

Current Scenario of Groundwater Usage in India

Extraction and Availability

India is the largest user of groundwater, extracting nearly 25% of the world's groundwater annually. As of 2023, the country extracted 241.34 billion cubic meters (bcm), with over 736 overexploited assessment units. Groundwater is primarily used for irrigation, which constitutes 62% of the country's water needs. It is also essential for rural water supply (85%) and urban water needs (50%).

Dependency on Groundwater

Groundwater is the backbone of India's water needs, especially in regions where surface water is scarce. However, this dependency is unsustainable, and over-exploitation of aquifers is leading to significant environmental challenges.

Regulatory Framework for Groundwater Management

Central-Level Regulation

The Central Ground Water Authority (CGWA) and the Central Ground Water Board (CGWB) play key roles in regulating groundwater extraction in India. These bodies have been responsible for issuing guidelines and monitoring over-exploited zones to ensure sustainability.

State-Level Regulation

Several states have enacted their own groundwater acts to regulate extraction and ensure the long-term availability of water. These laws typically empower local authorities to monitor and manage groundwater resources.

Key Factors Responsible for Groundwater Shortage and Contamination

Water-Intensive Agricultural Practices

India's reliance on water-intensive crops like paddy and sugarcane has led to excessive groundwater extraction. In some states, the subsidies for electricity and water have exacerbated this issue by encouraging unregulated groundwater pumping.

Population Growth and Urbanization

With the increasing population and urban migration, the demand for groundwater has surged, putting immense pressure on existing aquifers. Major cities like Bengaluru are now relying on tankers due to declining groundwater levels.

Climate Change and Erratic Rainfall

Climate change has worsened the groundwater crisis by disrupting the monsoon cycle, which is crucial for recharging aquifers. Reduced rainfall has forced communities to exploit deeper aquifers.

Industrial Effluent Discharge and Pollution

Unregulated industrial discharges, especially in areas like Kanpur, have led to severe contamination of groundwater with heavy metals and chemicals. The indiscriminate use of fertilizers and pesticides has also caused nitrate contamination in many regions.

Government Initiatives for Groundwater Management

Jal Shakti Abhiyan (JSA)

Launched in 2019, the JSA focuses on rainwater harvesting and water conservation across rural and urban areas, aiming to reduce pressure on groundwater resources.

Atal Bhujal Yojana

Under this scheme, the government has successfully reduced groundwater depletion in several districts by promoting water management techniques and encouraging community participation.

Measures for Effective Groundwater Management

Promoting Water-Efficient Agriculture

Introducing water-saving technologies like drip irrigation and zero tillage can significantly reduce the pressure on groundwater resources.

Aquifer Mapping with AI and IoT

Using advanced technologies like AI and IoT to map aquifers and monitor groundwater health can provide critical data for sustainable water management.

Rainwater Harvesting and Aquifer Recharge

Scaling up rainwater harvesting and aquifer recharge projects across urban and rural areas can help replenish over-exploited groundwater reserves.

Conclusion

The groundwater crisis in India requires immediate and sustained action. While government initiatives have made some progress, comprehensive strategies, such as promoting water-efficient agriculture, improving groundwater monitoring, and scaling up aquifer recharge, are necessary to secure water for future generations.
