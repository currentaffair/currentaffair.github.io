Harvest Festivals in India

Why in News?

The President of India has greeted citizens on the eve of Lohri, Makar Sankranti, Pongal, and Magh Bihu. These are harvest festivals celebrated in diverse forms across the various regions of India.

What are the Harvest Festivals in India?

These festivals mark the end of the harvesting season across India under various names such as Makar Sankranti, Pongal, Magh Bihu, Lohri, etc. They follow the solar cycle, unlike lunar-based festivals, with a fixed date of 14th January.

Astronomical Significance: The festivals mark the transition of the Sun into the Capricorn (Makara) zodiac sign and the start of the Sun's northward journey (Uttarayan). It symbolizes the shift from winter to warmer months, marking the end of inactivity.

Cultural Significance: Rituals during Makar Sankranti include bathing, offering Naivedhya (food) to Lord Surya, charity, performing Shraddha, and breaking fast. Devotees bathe in sacred rivers like the Ganga, Yamuna, Godavari, Krishna, and Cauvery.

Regional Celebrations:

Tamil Nadu (Pongal): The four-day festival marks the rice harvest, and Tamilians decorate their homes with traditional kolams made from rice powder.

Karnataka: Locals share sesame and jaggery mixture symbolizing harmony and goodwill. Farming communities dress their cattle in colorful costumes and make them jump over a fire pit in a display called 'Kicchu Haisodu'.

Punjab (Lohri): Involves bonfires, folk songs, and offerings like groundnuts and popcorn to the fire.

Bihar: A festival called ‘Khichdi’ is celebrated with rice and lentils, and sesame and jaggery ladoos or chikkis are distributed.

Rajasthan and Gujarat: These regions celebrate with kite-flying competitions and festivals, including the International Kite Festival in Ahmedabad.

Assam (Magh Bihu): Celebrates the annual harvest and marks the beginning of the Assamese new year.
