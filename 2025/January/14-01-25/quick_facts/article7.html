Gene Duplication and Evolution

In 1970, biologist Susumu Ohno proposed that gene duplication is a key mechanism for creating new genes. When genes are duplicated, one copy maintains the original function, while the other is free to mutate and potentially gain new functions.

Recent Research on Gene Duplication

Researchers at the University of Nevada, Reno, discovered that duplicated genes have more methylated DNA than non-duplicated genes. This methylation helps prevent over-expression of proteins, reducing harmful effects of duplication and enabling the new gene to survive and evolve.

Exogenous DNA and Incipient Genes

A study by the Max Planck Institute involved inserting random DNA sequences into human cells, observing their effects on cell growth. Over time, 40% of the new DNA sequences influenced cell growth, behaving like incipient genes that could become relevant for evolution.

Gene Retention in Evolution

Genes must serve a functional purpose or allow beneficial mutations to be retained in the genome. The ABO blood group system is an example of gene retention, where variants (A, B, O) have persisted, even though some are non-essential.

Key Concepts of Gene Function and Structure

The human genome contains 20,000 protein-coding genes and 20,000 genes for RNA regulation. Promoters and enhancers play a crucial role in gene transcription. The human genome consists of 24 chromosomes, with males having one X and one Y chromosome, and females having two X chromosomes.

Implications for Evolutionary Biology

These studies highlight the complexity of gene creation, duplication, and evolution, offering new perspectives on how new genes arise and are preserved over time. Random DNA insertions contribute to the adaptation of organisms.
