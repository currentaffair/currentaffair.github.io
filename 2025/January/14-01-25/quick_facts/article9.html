Gene Duplication and Evolutionary Insights

Why in News? In 1970, biologist Susumu Ohno proposed that gene duplication is a key mechanism for creating new genes. Gene duplication allows one copy of a gene to maintain its function while the other copy can mutate and develop new functions. This theory has evolved with recent research into gene duplication and its implications for evolutionary biology.

Recent Research on Gene Duplication

University of Nevada, Reno: Researchers have found that promoters of duplicated genes exhibit more methylated DNA than non-duplicated genes. Methylation helps prevent protein overproduction from duplicated genes, reducing harmful effects and enabling new genes to evolve. This process also increases mutation rates, contributing to gene evolution.

Max Planck Institute Study: A study inserted random DNA sequences into human cells, observing their impact on cell growth. About 40% of the sequences affected cell growth, behaving like incipient genes with potential evolutionary significance.

Gene Retention and Evolution

For a gene to persist in the genome, it must serve a beneficial function or undergo beneficial mutations. An example of gene retention is the ABO blood group system, where different variants have persisted across species despite some variants not being essential.

Gene Function and Structure: The human genome contains approximately 20,000 protein-coding genes and 20,000 genes that regulate other genes. Gene transcription is governed by promoters and enhancers that determine when and where genes are transcribed into mRNA for protein production.

Implications for Evolutionary Biology: These studies offer new insights into how genes are created, duplicated, and evolve over time. Random DNA insertions and duplications contribute to the adaptation and survival of organisms.
