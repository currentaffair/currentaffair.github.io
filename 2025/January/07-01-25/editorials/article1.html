India's Role in the Evolving Global Order

Introduction

India, as the world’s largest democracy and the fifth-largest economy, finds itself at a critical juncture in international relations. This moment is defined by a rapidly evolving global order shaped by geopolitical realignments, economic uncertainties, and technological advancements. To fully realize its potential, India must adeptly manage complex global dynamics while addressing significant domestic challenges.

Geopolitical Challenges and Relationships with Global Powers

Strategic Autonomy: Modern Non-Alignment

India has prioritized strategic autonomy, allowing it to maintain flexibility in foreign policy decisions. This approach helps balance relations with major global powers like the US, China, and Russia without being bound by rigid alliances. By maintaining this flexible stance, India continues to navigate the complex global political landscape, ensuring its sovereignty while engaging with global powers on key issues.

The Indo-US Relationship: Collaborative Yet Brittle

Strengths: Strategic cooperation through initiatives like the Quad has reinforced India’s position in the Indo-Pacific, promoting a free and open maritime environment amid China's rising influence. Additionally, growing collaboration in emerging technologies, such as AI, semiconductors, and defense, strengthens the bilateral relationship. Trade between the two nations surpassed $100 billion, underscoring significant economic interdependence.

Challenges: Diplomatic tensions have arisen, especially regarding India’s relations with Russia and Iran. US legal proceedings involving Indian officials, as well as shifting domestic political landscapes, could create uncertainty in trade and immigration policies. Issues like H1-B visa restrictions and market access for Indian IT firms also complicate the economic ties.

India-China Relations: Managing Rivalry

Post-Galwan Standoff: Despite some disengagement along the border, significant military presence remains on both sides. India’s trade with China reached $136 billion in 2023, but a trade deficit exceeding $85 billion reflects India’s heavy dependence on Chinese imports, particularly in electronics and pharmaceuticals.

Challenges: China’s Belt and Road Initiative (BRI) continues to expand its influence in South Asia, encircling India with infrastructure projects in Pakistan, Nepal, and Sri Lanka. The China+1 strategy presents opportunities for India but requires fostering a competitive environment for growth and innovation.

India-Russia Relations: A Strategic Necessity

Despite Russia’s growing proximity to China, India continues its strategic cooperation with Russia, particularly in defense and energy sectors. Discounted oil imports from Russia are vital for India’s energy security, and ongoing defense collaborations, such as missile systems and naval assets, remain crucial to India’s military strategy.

Expanding Economic Engagements and Investment Horizons

Trade Agreements and Global Integration

Bilateral trade agreements with Australia and the UAE have significantly enhanced India’s role in global value chains. India is also positioning its Unified Payments Interface (UPI) as a model for secure and efficient financial transactions. However, India’s decision to withdraw from the Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership (RCEP) reflects a cautious approach to trade liberalization, slowing its integration into broader Asian markets.

Challenges in Attracting Investments

India has seen a decline in Foreign Direct Investment (FDI), reaching a 16-year low of $10.58 billion in 2023-24. This decline can be attributed to bureaucratic inefficiencies, high tariffs, and inconsistent policy implementation. Free Trade Agreement negotiations, particularly with the UK and EU, have been delayed due to disagreements over tariffs, data localization, and labor mobility.

Sustainability and Green Economy

India’s commitment to achieving net-zero emissions by 2070 requires a delicate balance between economic growth and sustainability. India has played a leading role in advocating for global climate action, particularly through initiatives aimed at limiting global warming to 1.5°C. However, the Production-Linked Incentive (PLI) schemes, while promising, have yet to achieve consistent results across various sectors.

Leadership on the Global Stage

Multilateral Reforms and Advocacy

India has long advocated for reforms in global institutions such as the UN Security Council, IMF, and World Bank, seeking greater representation for developing nations. India’s tenure as a non-permanent UNSC member demonstrated its commitment to counterterrorism and climate action, although the lack of progress on Security Council reforms highlights the challenges of global power politics.

Global South Leadership

India’s leadership in sustainable development is evident through initiatives like the International Solar Alliance (ISA) and the Coalition for Disaster Resilient Infrastructure (CDRI). Its engagement with BRICS and the Shanghai Cooperation Organization (SCO) reinforces its role in promoting South-South cooperation, although challenges persist in balancing partnerships with the Global North on issues like technology transfer and geopolitical tensions.

Regional Stability: Challenges in Neighbourhood Diplomacy

India’s neighborhood diplomacy faces significant hurdles, particularly with countries like Bangladesh, Nepal, Maldives, and Pakistan. Issues such as anti-India rhetoric, pro-China policies, and regional infrastructure projects backed by China are complicating India’s traditional influence in the region. Diplomatic relations with Pakistan remain frozen, with ongoing tensions over terrorism and the Kashmir issue.

Conclusion

India is at a decisive moment in its history, where the next decade will shape its global standing. By balancing geopolitical ambitions, advancing economic diplomacy, and addressing regional and domestic challenges, India has the potential to emerge as a key architect of a stable and inclusive global order. With strategic vision, India can leverage its demographic dividend, economic strength, and cultural influence to play a leading role on the world stage.
