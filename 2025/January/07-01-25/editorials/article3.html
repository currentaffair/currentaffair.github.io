India's Migrant Workers and Welfare Challenges

Introduction

The welfare of migrant workers is essential for India’s economic growth. The country's migrant workforce, especially in the unorganized sector, faces significant challenges. The government has launched several initiatives like the e-Shram portal, but hurdles such as lack of legal protections, gender inequalities, and lack of portability of benefits across states persist. This article explores these issues and offers insights on possible solutions to ensure the inclusion of migrant workers in India's development journey.

Who are Considered as Migrant Workers?

The term "migrant workers" is complex and varies in its definition. The International Convention on the Protection of the Rights of All Migrant Workers and Members of Their Families (1990) defines a migrant worker as a person who is engaged in a remunerated activity in a country where they are not a national. In India, the Inter-State Migrant Workmen (Regulation of Employment and Conditions of Service) Act, 1979 further defines migrant workers as those employed in one state but living in another, often recruited through contractors.

Push and Pull Factors Driving Migration in India

Push Factors

Economic Distress and Rural Unemployment: Persistent poverty and disguised unemployment in rural areas drive people to cities. The rural unemployment rate stood at 7.8% in April 2024 (CMIE), while agriculture's contribution to GDP is dwindling. Erratic monsoons and declining farm incomes are major factors.

Healthcare and Educational Deficiencies: Poor access to healthcare and education forces families to migrate. Rural areas are severely lacking in essential services, contributing to migration trends toward urban areas.

Climate Change and Environmental Degradation: Climate-induced disasters like droughts and floods displace millions. Reports like the NDMA’s 2021-22 report indicate that 68% of India’s cultivable land is drought-prone, highlighting the vulnerability of rural populations.

Pull Factors

Urban Job Opportunities: Cities offer better job prospects with improved wages, particularly in sectors like construction, manufacturing, and services. Cities like Bengaluru and Delhi have thriving job markets attracting migrants from rural areas.

Improved Healthcare and Educational Opportunities: Urban areas provide better access to healthcare and educational services. Urban hospitals account for 63.5% of India’s total beds (National Health Profile 2022), attracting people from rural areas seeking better living conditions.

Social Mobility and Infrastructure: Urban areas provide marginalized communities with better opportunities for social mobility, and cities offer superior infrastructure, digital connectivity, and amenities, making them attractive destinations for migrants.

Legal Framework for Migrant Welfare

Several legislative measures address migrant worker welfare, including the Inter-State Migrant Workmen Act, 1979, which mandates registration and protections for migrant workers. However, implementation remains a challenge. Newer labor codes, such as the Code on Wages (2018) and the Code on Social Security (2020), aim to expand welfare coverage, but the lack of effective enforcement is a significant barrier.

Key Issues Faced by Migrant Workers

Lack of Social Security and Legal Protections: The informal nature of migrant employment means that workers are often excluded from social security systems like EPF, health insurance, and maternity leave.

Exploitation and Wage Discrimination: Migrant workers are often subject to wage theft, long working hours, and exploitative conditions due to weak enforcement of labor laws. A survey during the COVID-19 lockdown found that 64% of migrant workers did not receive full wages.

Poor Living Conditions and Housing: Migrants often live in overcrowded, unsanitary conditions, as affordable housing is scarce in urban areas. Nearly half of India's urban population lives in slums (UN-Habitat 2021).

Measures to Ensure Welfare and Integration of Migrant Workers

Portable Social Security Systems

There is a pressing need to create a nationwide system for the portability of social security benefits, ensuring that migrant workers can access welfare schemes like EPF, health insurance, and food security regardless of where they live.

Affordable Housing and Livelihood Clusters

Developing affordable rental housing and creating livelihood clusters close to job opportunities can significantly improve the quality of life for migrant workers. Integrating these initiatives with urban planning and industrial hubs would ensure better living and working conditions.

Digitization of Welfare Delivery

To ensure seamless access to entitlements like ration and healthcare, the development of mobile applications and digital platforms for migrant workers is essential. This would integrate welfare schemes, making them more accessible to this population.

Conclusion

In conclusion, India’s migrant workforce plays a crucial role in the nation’s economic and industrial growth. However, the challenges they face—such as poor living conditions, lack of social security, and legal protections—must be addressed urgently. By implementing portable social security, improving housing, and digitalizing welfare delivery, the welfare of migrant workers can be enhanced, ensuring their inclusion in the country's economic growth. Effective policy harmonization across states, along with gender-sensitive measures, can further help integrate migrant workers into India's development trajectory.
