Sovereign AI in India

Introduction

The rise of artificial intelligence (AI) has sparked global discussions about the significance of foundational AI models. These models, like ChatGPT and DeepSeek, require substantial investments in computing power, skilled talent, and robust infrastructure. India now faces a critical decision: whether to build its own sovereign AI model or rely on existing open-source and proprietary solutions.

Arguments for a Sovereign AI Model

Technological Sovereignty and National Security

AI is considered a strategic asset.
Relying on foreign AI models exposes India to risks like sanctions, access restrictions, and data sovereignty issues. For instance, the U.S. has imposed restrictions on semiconductor exports, significantly impacting AI development in several countries. Developing indigenous AI capabilities can ensure long-term self-reliance and reduce geopolitical vulnerabilities.

Indigenous Innovation and Economic Growth

AI is projected to contribute $500 billion to India’s economy by 2025 (NASSCOM Report, 2023). A sovereign AI model can stimulate local research, foster startups, and strengthen India’s AI ecosystem. With a robust IT sector, India is well-positioned to leverage AI for economic expansion. Moreover, existing AI models are predominantly trained on Western datasets and may not accurately capture India’s linguistic diversity and socio-cultural nuances.

A localized AI model can enhance digital inclusion by catering to India’s 22 official languages and numerous dialects.

Challenges in Building a Foundational AI Model

High Cost and Resource Constraints

Training a foundational AI model demands significant financial resources. For instance, DeepSeek V3 cost $5.6 million to train, while major tech firms invest over $80 billion annually in AI infrastructure (McKinsey, 2023). India currently lacks access to high-performance semiconductor manufacturing, dominated by companies like Taiwan’s TSMC.

Infrastructure and Talent Gaps

India requires extensive GPU clusters for AI training, but local availability remains limited. Furthermore, AI talent is concentrated in top firms abroad.

To bridge this gap, India must enhance domestic AI education and bolster research institutions.

Limited Market Size for Enterprise AI Adoption

The primary market for AI services is currently the U.S., where businesses possess higher purchasing power. Indian enterprises may not yet be ready to invest heavily in AI solutions, limiting potential financial returns for domestic AI initiatives.

The Middle Path: Strategic Investments in AI

Rather than focusing on building a single large-scale foundational model, India can adopt a strategic and phased approach.

Public-Private Collaboration

Encouraging firms like Infosys, TCS, and startups to co-develop AI models with government support is essential. Partnerships with academia can drive innovation in AI research, creating a collaborative ecosystem that leverages both industry expertise and academic rigor.

Focused AI Development for Key Sectors

Investments should target AI models tailored for specific sectors such as governance, healthcare, agriculture, and local language processing. Programs like AI for Bharat, which focus on Indic language processing, should receive increased support to ensure inclusivity and relevance.

Government Infrastructure and Policy Support

The IndiaAI Mission’s GPU cluster initiative should be expanded to provide affordable computing power to startups and researchers. Additionally, clear AI regulations are necessary to balance innovation with ethical AI deployment, ensuring responsible development and use of AI technologies.

Conclusion

While India should aspire to develop foundational AI capabilities, a cautious and strategic approach is essential.

Rather than focusing solely on building sovereign AI models, India should prioritize investments in applied AI, indigenous innovation, and collaborations with global partners to foster a robust and sustainable AI ecosystem.
