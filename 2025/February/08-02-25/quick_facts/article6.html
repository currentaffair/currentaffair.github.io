High Courts Ad-Hoc Judges

Supreme Court's Latest Order

The Supreme Court's order on January 30, 2024, allows High Courts to appoint retired judges on an ad-hoc basis to hear only criminal appeals. These judges must serve on a Bench led by a sitting judge.

The Lok Prahari (2021) rule, which limited such appointments to High Courts with more than 20% judicial vacancies, has been relaxed. However, the number of ad-hoc judges is capped at 10% of the High Court's sanctioned strength (typically 2 to 5 judges).

Historical Context of Ad-Hoc Appointments

  • 1972: Justice Suraj Bhan appointed to Madhya Pradesh High Court (election petitions).
  • 1982: Justice P. Venugopal appointed to Madras High Court.
  • 2007: Justice O.P. Srivastava appointed to Allahabad High Court (Ayodhya title suits).

Constitutional Provision: Article 224-A

Introduced via the 15th Constitutional Amendment Act, 1963, Article 224-A permits the appointment of retired judges to High Courts on an ad-hoc basis. This requires the consent of both the retired judge and the President of India. Ad-hoc judges have the same powers and privileges as sitting judges, with remuneration determined by a Presidential order.

Reason for the New Order

With 62 lakh cases pending in High Courts as of January 25, 2024—including 18.2 lakh criminal cases—the Supreme Court removed the 20% vacancy requirement to expedite judicial processes. Ad-hoc judges are restricted to handling criminal appeals to ensure faster case resolutions.
