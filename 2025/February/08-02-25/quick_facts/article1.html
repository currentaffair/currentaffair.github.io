IBCA and Big Cat Conservation

Introduction

The International Big Cat Alliance (IBCA) officially comes into force on January 23, 2025 as a treaty-based inter-governmental international organization. Five countries — Nicaragua, Eswatini, India, Somalia, and Liberia — have ratified the Framework Agreement under Article VIII (1).

Membership and Objectives

27 countries, including India, have agreed to join IBCA. The alliance serves as a global conservation platform for collaboration among big cat range and non-range countries, conservation organizations, and international stakeholders. It aims to reverse the decline of seven major big cat species and restore their habitats, ensuring their long-term survival and contributing to global ecological balance.

Big Cats Covered by IBCA

  • Tiger (Panthera tigris)
  • Lion (Panthera leo)
  • Jaguar (Panthera onca)
  • Leopard (Panthera pardus)
  • Snow Leopard (Panthera uncia)
  • Puma (Puma concolor)
  • Cheetah (Acinonyx jubatus)

Scientific Classification & Roaring Ability

The Panthera genus includes Tigers, Lions, Jaguars, Leopards, and Snow Leopards, all capable of roaring. Puma and Cheetah are not part of Panthera but are often listed as big cats. The Lion’s roar is the loudest, audible up to 8-10 km.

India’s Role in Big Cat Conservation

India has historically been home to the Bengal Tiger, Asiatic Lion, Indian Leopard, Indian/Asiatic Cheetah, and Snow Leopard. The Indian Cheetah was declared extinct in 1952. In 2022, the Government of India reintroduced African Cheetahs in Kuno National Park, Madhya Pradesh.

Conclusion

The establishment of IBCA marks a significant global step in big cat conservation. India’s leadership in this initiative reinforces its commitment to wildlife protection and ecological balance.
