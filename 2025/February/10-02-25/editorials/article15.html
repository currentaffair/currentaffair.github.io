India-Middle East Corridor

Introduction

The India-Middle East-Europe Economic Corridor (IMEC), unveiled at the G-20 Summit 2023, marks a pivotal step in India's strategic outreach to West Asia and Europe. Positioned as more than just a trade initiative, IMEC reflects India's ambition to solidify its role in reshaping global supply chains and enhancing diplomatic influence across key regions. However, the corridor faces challenges, notably the Israel-Gaza conflict, which has impeded progress despite strong support from partners like Greece, which heralds it as a "project for peace." This article delves into the multifaceted significance of the Middle East in India's foreign policy, the obstacles it faces, and the strategic measures necessary to navigate this complex geopolitical landscape.

Why is the Middle East Crucial to India's Foreign Outlook?

Energy Security and Economic Stability

India's dependence on Middle Eastern energy resources is a cornerstone of its economic stability.

In January 2025, India's crude oil imports from the Middle East rose to 53.89%, up from 51% in December 2024
, underscoring the region's critical role in fueling India's growth. Strategic agreements further cement this relationship:

  • India-UAE MoU (January 2023) on green hydrogen development and the 'One Sun, One World, One Grid' initiative.
  • India-Qatar LNG Deal Extension (February 2024): Ensuring 7.5 million tonnes per annum supply until 2048.

Trade, Investment, and Economic Corridors

The Middle East is a vital trade partner for India, significantly contributing to its foreign trade dynamics:

  • India-GCC Bilateral Trade (FY 2023-24): Valued at USD 161.59 billion.
  • UAE: India's third-largest trading partner and second-largest export destination with exports exceeding US$ 35.62 billion.

Free Trade Agreements (FTAs) and initiatives like IMEC aim to streamline supply chains, reduce trade costs, and enhance market access.

Remittances and Workforce Contributions

The Middle East hosts a substantial Indian diaspora, contributing significantly to India's economy through remittances:

  • Over 66% of India's 1.34 crore NRIs reside in the UAE, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Qatar, Oman, and Bahrain.
  • Remittances (2022): India received $111 billion, the highest globally, predominantly from the Gulf region.

Geopolitical and Strategic Cooperation

India's diplomatic agility in balancing relations among rival blocs in West Asia is pivotal for its strategic autonomy:

  • Defense Collaborations: Joint military exercises like Al-Mohed Al-Hindi with Saudi Arabia (2021) and defense exports.
  • Chabahar Port Agreement: Enhances connectivity to Central Asia, bypassing Pakistan.

Food and Maritime Security

India relies heavily on the Middle East for agricultural exports and maritime trade routes:

  • UAE: Second-largest importer of Indian agricultural products, accounting for $1.9 billion in FY 2022-23.
  • Exports to Saudi Arabia include rice, textiles, machinery, cereals, automobiles, and gems.

Cultural, Religious, and Soft Power Diplomacy

India's historical and cultural ties with the Middle East enhance bilateral relationships:

  • BAPS Temple in UAE: A symbol of India-UAE cultural ties, built with pink sandstone from Rajasthan.
  • Soft power through Bollywood, yoga, and Ayurveda fosters strong cultural connections in Gulf nations.

Conclusion

India's engagement with the Middle East transcends traditional energy ties, evolving into a multifaceted partnership encompassing trade, strategic connectivity, defense collaboration, and cultural diplomacy. To maximize its potential, India must accelerate infrastructure development, finalize trade agreements, and deepen security cooperation while maintaining strategic autonomy. The IMEC represents a pivotal step in this direction, but navigating the complex geopolitical landscape of West Asia remains a critical challenge for India's foreign policy.
