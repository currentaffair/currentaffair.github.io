India's Agricultural Reforms

Introduction

The Union Budget 2025-26 introduces key initiatives aimed at transforming India's agricultural sector. With a focus on sustainability, technological innovation, and infrastructure development, these reforms seek to enhance productivity and address longstanding structural challenges.

Key Initiatives in the Budget

Focus on 100 Districts:

Special emphasis on augmenting agricultural productivity, promoting sustainable farming practices, and crop diversification in 100 identified districts.

Kisan Credit Card (KCC) Expansion:

Credit access increased from Rs 3 lakh to Rs 5 lakh, providing farmers with greater financial support to invest in modern farming techniques.

Pulses Mission:

Aimed at achieving atma nirbharta (self-sufficiency) in tur, moong, and urad production, reducing dependency on imports.

Makhana Board in Bihar:

Established to promote the cultivation and marketing of makhana (foxnut), boosting local economies and traditional farming.

High-Yielding Crop Varieties:

Introduction of 109 climate-resilient varieties of 32 field and horticulture crops to combat climate change impacts.

Mission for Vegetables and Fruits:

An allocation of Rs 500 crore to enhance production, strengthen supply chains, and boost processing industries.

Agriculture Infrastructure Fund:

Allocation increased from Rs 600 crore in FY25 to Rs 900 crore in FY26 to improve post-harvest infrastructure and reduce losses.

Budget Allocation and Inflation Impact

Total allocation for agriculture and allied sectors stands at Rs 1.49 trillion, marking a 4% increase over the previous year. However,

inflation projected at 4-5% in FY26
may erode the real value of this allocation.

PM-Kisan Scheme:

Despite its significance, the allocation remains stagnant at Rs 60,000 crore since 2019, effectively declining in real terms due to inflation.

Missed Opportunity:

The budget fails to integrate PM-Kisan with direct transfer of fertiliser subsidies, a move that could have streamlined agricultural support and increased efficiency.

Structural Challenges in Agriculture

Declining Share in GDP vs. Rising Workforce Dependency

Agriculture's share in GDP has declined to 17.7%, while its share in the workforce has increased from 42.5% (2018-19) to 46.1% (FY24). This reverse trend highlights the

inability of non-agricultural sectors to absorb surplus labour
, leading to depressed real wages in farming.

Low R&D Investment

R&D expenditure remains below 0.5% of agri-GDP, far short of the 1% required for sustainable growth. A marginal increase in R&D allocation in FY26 may not be sufficient to drive significant productivity gains or enhance climate resilience.

MSP-Centric Policies and Crop Diversification

Heavy reliance on rice and wheat due to MSP procurement policies crowds out high-value crops like pulses, oilseeds, and horticulture. The

lack of crop-neutral incentives
hinders sustainable diversification.

Post-Harvest Losses

Post-harvest losses remain significant, with fruits losing 8.1% and vegetables 7.3%, contributing to

37% of total post-harvest losses, amounting to Rs 1.53 trillion annually
. Inadequate cold chains, processing facilities, and logistics infrastructure exacerbate these losses.

Global and Market Challenges

India faces several global and market challenges in agriculture:

Import Dependence

Shortages in pulses, oilseeds, cotton, and maize have led to a rise in imports, increasing vulnerability to global price fluctuations.

Fragmented Value Chains

Farmers receive only 30% of consumer spending on fruits and vegetables due to inefficiencies in the value chain.

Logistical Bottlenecks

Limited processing capacity and poor market linkages hinder effective price discovery and reduce farmer incomes.

Way Forward: Opportunities and Recommendations

Strengthening Market Linkages

Integrating e-NAM with the ONDC platform can improve price discovery and reduce inefficiencies caused by middlemen. Expanding warehousing and cold storage infrastructure will be crucial to reducing post-harvest losses.

Promoting Labour-Intensive Sectors

Encouraging skill upgradation in labour-intensive sectors can help absorb surplus agricultural labour and improve productivity. Deregulating MSMEs could further boost employment outside agriculture.

Private Sector Participation

Incentivising private investment in oilseed production, processing facilities, and marketing infrastructure can enhance sector growth. Expanding the Agriculture Infrastructure Fund will attract more private capital.

Subsidy Rationalisation and Investment-Driven Growth

Shifting from subsidy-heavy interventions to investment-driven growth strategies is essential. Rationalising subsidies will free up resources for infrastructure development and R&D.

Conclusion

While the Union Budget 2025-26 addresses several agricultural challenges, its incremental approach may fall short of the transformational changes needed. A paradigm shift towards

investment-driven growth, private sector participation, and technology-led efficiency improvements
is crucial. To make Indian agriculture more resilient and globally competitive, bold reforms in subsidy rationalisation, infrastructure development, and market linkages are imperative.
