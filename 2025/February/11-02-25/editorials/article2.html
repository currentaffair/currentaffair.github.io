India and Global South

Introduction

In January 2025, during the 18th Pravasi Bharatiya Divas convention, Prime Minister Narendra Modi declared, “Today’s India not only firmly asserts its own point but also strongly amplifies the voice of the Global South.” This sentiment reflects India’s growing ambition to be a leader and advocate for developing nations. The 3rd Voice of Global South Summit held in August 2024 further emphasized India’s commitment to reform global governance structures to be more inclusive of developing countries.

India’s Renewed Enthusiasm

India’s motivation to champion the cause of the developing world differs from the historical Non-Alignment Movement (NAM). Unlike NAM’s focus on decolonization and critique of Western dominance, India’s current approach balances its relationships with both the Global South and traditional partners like the United States and Europe. This is evident from high-profile visits, such as Jake Sullivan’s visit in January 2025 and Mr. Modi’s trip to Poland in August 2024, indicating strategic alliance-building beyond ideological lines.

The China Factor

While some argue that India’s efforts are aimed at countering China’s growing dominance, this perspective oversimplifies the situation.

Foreign Direct Investments (FDIs) in Africa show India focusing on regions with significant Chinese influence.
However, India seeks to establish an independent identity as an emerging power, advancing its own strategic interests in trade, defense, and geopolitics.

Global South countries, burdened by debt and restrictive economic paradigms, are not seeking another China or a new Western institution. India’s unique position allows it to act as a bridge between the Global North and South, provided it backs rhetoric with concrete strategies.

Strategic Steps Forward

India must emphasize an alternative development cooperation model that moves away from the top-down approaches typical of the Global North. Although India promotes equal partnerships, its strategies often reflect an India-first approach, as seen in the Global Development Compact, rooted in Indian experiences.

For greater acceptance,

India should also be open to learning from other Global South nations to address its domestic challenges.
Countries are more receptive to partnerships that are collaborative rather than hierarchical. To avoid being perceived as a ‘big brother,’ India needs to foster genuine mutual relationships.

A Human-Centric Approach

New Delhi emphasizes human-centric development to tackle global challenges. Initiatives like Mission LiFE (Lifestyle For Environment) advocate for sustainable living and behavioral change.

India’s focus on capacity building, especially through programs like Skill India and initiatives promoting women entrepreneurship, aligns with the developmental needs of the Global South.

While the Indian Technical and Economic Cooperation (ITEC) program offers sector-specific short-term activities,

India could enhance its impact by helping countries build long-term institutional capacities.
Leveraging experiences from micro, small, and medium enterprises, India can promote knowledge exchange and strengthen digital infrastructure, climate solutions, and food security initiatives.

The Path Ahead

India’s call for more inclusive global governance was exemplified by its role in the inclusion of the African Union in the G-20 during its 2023 presidency. However,

India must not only influence global institutions but also strengthen its domestic capacity for international cooperation.

Establishing norms and standards in collaboration with experienced partners like the United Nations, Germany, and France can serve as a foundation.

India’s long-term goal should be to develop robust domestic systems and lead global initiatives through trilateral partnerships and increased engagement with new allies.

Conclusion

India’s aspiration to be the ‘Voice’ of the Global South must be balanced with an ability to ‘listen.’ Historically, India’s leadership in the Non-Alignment Movement provided developing countries with a third option. Today, India has the opportunity to redefine this role by fostering genuine partnerships and inclusive development, ensuring its position as a respected global leader.
