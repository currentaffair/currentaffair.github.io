Short Neck Clams & Marine Stewardship

Context

The ICAR-Central Marine Fisheries Research Institute (CMFRI) has initiated a stock enhancement programme to combat the declining population of short-neck clams (Paphia malabarica).

About Short Neck Clams

A fast-growing, valuable bivalve species found in Ashtamudi Lake, Kerala. Known for its peak spawning season from December to February, with a maximum lifespan of 3 years.

Key Features

  • Sexually matures in the first year.
  • Important fishery resource in Ashtamudi Lake.
  • India’s first Marine Stewardship Council (MSC) certified fishery.

Economic Importance

The clam shells are used in cement production, carbide for welding, and other industrial applications. About 80% of the clams are exported to Vietnam, Malaysia, and Thailand.

Usage

  • Human consumption: Popular in Kerala.
  • Shrimp feed: Used in Tamil Nadu, Pondicherry, and Andhra Pradesh.
  • Industrial applications: Cement, calcium carbide, sand-lime bricks.

Reasons for Decline

Environmental pollution, invasive species like the charru mussel, and climate change have reduced clam populations.

About MSC Certification

The short-neck clam fishery in Kerala’s Ashtamudi Lake received MSC certification in 2014, recognizing sustainable practices through efforts of CMFRI, WWF, and the local community.

About Ashtamudi Lake

A Ramsar Wetland with diverse mangrove habitats. It has supported the clam fishery since 1981, providing livelihoods for around 3,000 fishers.
