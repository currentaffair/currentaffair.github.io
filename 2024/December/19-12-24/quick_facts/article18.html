Religion and Reservations in India

Supreme Court Stance on Religion and Reservations

The Supreme Court of India has ruled that reservations cannot be based solely on religion. This judgment emphasizes that backwardness should remain the primary criterion for reservations, rather than religious affiliation.

Religious Groups in OBC Reservations

Some states, including Kerala, Karnataka, and Tamil Nadu, have included Muslims in their OBC quotas. However, the Indra Sawhney case (1992) ruled against religion-based reservations, prioritizing social and economic backwardness as the determining factor for OBC eligibility.

Scheduled Caste Status and Religious Conversion

The Constitution allows Scheduled Caste (SC) status for individuals practicing Hinduism, Sikhism, or Buddhism. In the Soosai v/s Union of India case (1985), the court ruled that converts to Christianity or Islam lose their SC benefits, though they may retain their caste status if they continue to face social disadvantages.

OBC Reservations for Religious Groups

The Supreme Court is still deliberating on whether OBC reservations can be extended to entire religious groups. Past attempts, like Andhra Pradesh's attempt to grant Muslims 5% OBC reservations, have been overturned for lack of objective criteria to assess backwardness.
