GST Council's 55th Meeting: Uncertainty and Policy Delays

Introduction

The 55th meeting of the GST Council has recently brought attention to several ambiguities and delays in India's Goods and Services Tax (GST) system. Despite efforts to simplify the system, key issues remain unresolved, affecting various sectors and leading to growing concerns about policy certainty. This article analyzes the main highlights from the meeting, the challenges faced in implementing GST reforms, and the potential impacts on India's economy.

Key Highlights of the 55th GST Council Meeting

Clarification of Ambiguities

Several ambiguities were clarified during the meeting, including:

  • The classification of farmers' supplies, such as pepper and raisins, which were deemed non-taxable under GST.
  • The treatment of gift vouchers and penalties by banks/NBFCs, which were clarified as outside the scope of GST.

These clarifications are essential for improving compliance but raise concerns about the delayed resolution of such issues, undermining the efficiency of GST, which was marketed as a “Good and Simple Tax.”

Complex Tax Adjustments

One of the more complex adjustments made was the introduction of a three-tiered GST levy on popcorn. The rationale behind this move was to impose higher taxes on sweetened varieties for health considerations. While this may be seen as a positive step for public health, it adds further complexity to an already convoluted tax structure.

This change highlights the need for comprehensive GST rate rationalization, which has been delayed for over three years. Simplifying the GST system remains crucial to enhancing the ease of doing business in India.

Failure to Address Key Recommendations

Despite discussions on reform, the Council failed to address several key recommendations from various panels:

  • The recommendations from the Ministerial Panel on GST rate rejigging were ignored.
  • Proposals on insurance policy taxation were deferred, despite long-standing promises from the government.

These delays have caused a dip in life insurance sales in November 2024, as consumers awaited potential GST cuts. This lack of action affects both consumer behavior and industry planning, highlighting a reactive approach to policy-making.

Economic and Policy Implications

The ongoing uncertainties surrounding GST policy have broader economic implications:

  • Rate Rationalisation has been stalled, discouraging consumption in an already weak economic recovery phase.
  • Private investment is dampened due to the lack of tax certainty.
  • Delays in reforms affect industries that rely on predictable taxation to forecast demand and investment.

The delays and inconsistencies in GST policy contribute to a lack of confidence among businesses and consumers, hindering economic growth.

Retroactive Legal Reversals

A notable event occurred in October 2024 when the Supreme Court allowed realty players to claim input tax credits (ITCs) for commercial construction meant for leasing/renting. However, the GST Council reversed this ruling with retrospective effect from July 2017.

This retroactive change evokes memories of past taxation misadventures, such as the Vodafone tax cases, and erodes investor confidence. The unpredictability of tax policies is detrimental to India's investment climate and may deter foreign direct investment (FDI).

Perceived Governance Issues

The casual handling of critical matters, such as a panel chair leaving the meeting early, has raised questions about the GST Council’s approach to prioritizing key reforms. Promises made during the Budget session remain unfulfilled, and there is a growing concern over the alignment between state and central governments in the long-term GST rationalisation process.

Way Forward

Urgent Rate Rationalisation

To simplify the GST structure and improve clarity, India needs to:

  • Reduce GST slabs and clarify existing ambiguities.
  • Prioritize recommendations from expert panels to address structural issues within the system.

Enhancing Tax Certainty

To boost investor confidence, the government must avoid retroactive tax changes. It is also crucial to provide clear timelines for decisions on pending reforms, ensuring a predictable tax environment for businesses and consumers.

Coordination Between Stakeholders

A better alignment between the Centre and States is essential for smoother decision-making and effective implementation of GST reforms. A more coordinated approach will expedite the resolution of key issues and foster a conducive environment for economic growth.

Conclusion

The GST Council’s 55th meeting has brought to light the ongoing uncertainties and policy delays that continue to plague India’s GST system. While some progress was made in clarifying ambiguities, key reforms remain stalled, and retroactive tax changes have raised concerns. To ensure that India’s GST system functions effectively and promotes economic growth, it is crucial to implement rate rationalisation, provide tax certainty, and improve coordination between stakeholders. Only then can India build a more stable and predictable tax system for the future.
