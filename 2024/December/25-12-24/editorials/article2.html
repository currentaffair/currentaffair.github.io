India-China Relations and WTO's Shifting Trade Dynamics

Introduction

The 23rd meeting between India’s National Security Adviser (NSA) and the Chinese Foreign Minister as Special Representatives (SRs) represents a significant effort to address ongoing tensions between the two nations. This diplomatic engagement seeks to ease the strain that has characterized India-China relations, particularly after the 2020 Galwan Valley clash. Simultaneously, the WTO is facing an existential crisis, as global trade dynamics shift away from a rule-based system toward more unilateral actions. This article explores these two developments and their far-reaching implications for international relations and trade governance.

India-China Relations: Diplomatic and Economic Implications

Special Representatives Mechanism

Established over two decades ago, the Special Representatives (SR) mechanism has played a pivotal role in addressing the longstanding boundary dispute between India and China. This framework enables the two countries to discuss sensitive issues and maintain peace along the Line of Actual Control (LAC). The recent meeting between the NSA and the Chinese Foreign Minister reflects the ongoing importance of this platform in navigating bilateral relations.

Key Outcomes of the Meeting

  • Resumption of Kailash-Mansarovar Yatra: Symbolizing cultural diplomacy, this initiative aims to strengthen people-to-people connections between India and China.
  • Revival of Border Trade in Sikkim: The restoration of cross-border trade in the Sikkim region is expected to boost local economies and rebuild trust in bilateral economic exchanges.
  • Trans-boundary River Data Sharing: An important development in environmental cooperation, this agreement addresses India's concerns about water security, particularly in relation to China's upstream dam activities.

These steps indicate a commitment to normalizing relations, though challenges remain, particularly regarding the boundary dispute and strategic mistrust between the two nations.

Challenges in India-China Relations

While there have been positive developments, several core issues continue to strain India-China relations:

  • Boundary Dispute: Despite some disengagement, full de-escalation and demilitarization along the LAC have not been achieved. The border dispute remains the primary source of tension.
  • Trust Deficit: The 2020 Galwan clash severely damaged mutual trust. Aggressive actions by China, including frequent patrolling near disputed areas, exacerbate India's concerns.
  • Global Strategic Rivalry: India's growing strategic alliances with countries such as the U.S. and Japan through forums like QUAD are viewed with suspicion by China, further complicating bilateral ties.

WTO's Shifting Dynamics: A Legal Crisis

The WTO's Dispute Settlement System

The World Trade Organization (WTO) has long served as a cornerstone of global trade governance. Its two-tiered dispute resolution system, comprising the Panels and the Appellate Body (AB), was designed to ensure binding and enforceable resolutions. However, the system has been in crisis since 2019 due to the United States blocking appointments to the Appellate Body, rendering it non-functional.

Underlying Causes of the Crisis

Several factors have contributed to the WTO's current impasse:

  • U.S. Resistance: The United States has consistently resisted reforms to the Appellate Body, driven by protectionist policies and dissatisfaction with the WTO’s ability to address its concerns, particularly regarding China's trade practices.
  • China's Role: Since its accession to the WTO in 2001, China's state-led industrial policies have been a point of contention. The U.S. perceives that the WTO system has enabled China to exploit trade rules to its advantage, undermining fair competition.

The WTO’s Institutional Erosion

Scholars argue that the WTO is undergoing a "thinning of legality," where countries are increasingly reclaiming autonomy and reducing reliance on multilateral frameworks. This shift is evident in actions such as the U.S. imposing tariffs on China, bypassing WTO rules, and opting for unilateral measures.

As international politics increasingly influences trade relations, the WTO's authority is being undermined. The global trade system is experiencing a movement away from a rule-based order toward GATT-style diplomacy, where negotiations are more politically driven than legally binding.

Way Forward: India-China and the WTO

India-China Relations: A Delicate Path Forward

The path forward for India and China involves:

  • Confidence-Building Measures: Continued disengagement and de-escalation at the LAC are essential to maintaining stability.
  • Broadening Cooperation: Deepening ties in areas like climate change, public health, and infrastructure will help build trust.
  • Economic Realignment: India should work on addressing the trade imbalance and reducing dependency on China through diversified supply chains.

Reforming the WTO

The WTO’s future hinges on its ability to adapt to the changing global trade landscape. Key steps include:

  • Restoring the Appellate Body: Efforts must be made to address the crisis in the dispute settlement system to preserve the WTO’s credibility.
  • Shifting Trade Dynamics: The WTO must navigate the growing trend of unilateral trade policies and industrial actions by member states.
  • Towards a New Multilateralism: Global trade governance must reconcile national interests with international law to ensure that trade remains fair and transparent.

Conclusion

The ongoing dialogue between India and China, coupled with the crisis facing the WTO, illustrates the complexities of contemporary international relations. For India and China, a cautious but strategic approach will be necessary to stabilize bilateral relations. At the same time, the WTO must undergo reforms to maintain its relevance in a world where trade is increasingly influenced by national interests rather than international rules.
