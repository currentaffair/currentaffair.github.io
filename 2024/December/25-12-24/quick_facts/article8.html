Hydrothermal Vents in Indian Ocean

Why in the News?

India's Deep Ocean Mission recently imaged an active hydrothermal vent 4,500 meters below the Indian Ocean's surface, aiding mineral exploration and deep-sea research.

What are Hydrothermal Vents?

Hydrothermal vents are underwater hot springs formed near tectonic plates, where minerals and hot water emerge from the Earth's crust. They were first discovered in 1977 near the Galapagos Islands.

Formation Process

  • Seawater percolates through cracks in the ocean crust.
  • Heated by magma to over 370°C, it forms hydrothermal fluids.
  • Seawater can reach temperatures over 700°F but doesn’t boil due to high pressure.

Types of Hydrothermal Vents

  • Black Smokers: Emit iron sulfides, creating black chimneys.
  • White Smokers: Emit barium, calcium, and silicon, forming white chimneys.

Significance

Hydrothermal vent deposits are rich in valuable minerals like copper, gold, and silver. These vents create ecosystems supporting chemosynthetic organisms.

Similar Geothermal Features

  • Hot Springs: Heated groundwater on land, e.g., Manikaran and Gaurikund.
  • Geysers: Eruptions of water and steam from underground cavities, e.g., Yellowstone.
  • Fumaroles: Volcanic gas and steam vents, e.g., Barren Island.
  • Mudpots: Bubbling pools of mud formed from geothermal activity, e.g., Yellowstone.
