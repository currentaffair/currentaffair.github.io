India’s 100% Electrification Mission: A Critical Analysis

Introduction

India’s ambitious mission of 100% railway electrification, under the leadership of RITES Ltd. (the consultancy arm of Indian Railways), has garnered attention due to its environmental and economic promises. This mission involves converting broad gauge diesel-electric locomotives for export, notably to African nations, after a significant modification. The broader context reveals critical issues regarding asset wastage, the premature redundancy of operational locomotives, and the environmental impact of this electrification drive.

Railway Electrification: An Overview

Indian Railways has been undergoing a rapid electrification process with the goal of achieving a "green" transport system. By 2023, over 760 diesel locomotives had been idled, prematurely rendered obsolete by this initiative. These locomotives, some still with more than 15 years of usable life, are being decommissioned, leading to a significant loss of assets.

RTI Data and Policy Justification

The government's electrification drive, in the face of escalating diesel locomotive redundancy, raises pertinent questions about policy and financial management. According to RTI data, over 585 diesel locomotives were idling by 2023, a number that has now increased to around 760. The government's fast-tracked mission for 100% electrification, though commendable in its green ambitions, has led to the premature disposal of locomotives with substantial remaining operational life.

Environmental Impact and Energy Considerations

One of the primary justifications for railway electrification is its potential to reduce environmental pollution and reliance on fossil fuels. However, India's energy landscape complicates this claim. Nearly 50% of the nation's electricity is coal-generated, which offsets the "green" benefits of electrification. Electrifying the railways shifts the pollution from diesel locomotives to coal-fired power plants, thus maintaining the status quo in terms of environmental impact.

The reality is that Indian Railways, which transports a significant amount of coal, still faces a dichotomy where the use of "green" electric trains is sustained by non-renewable energy sources.

Economic Viability: The Hidden Costs

While electrification promises savings in fuel costs and environmental benefits, its long-term economic viability remains in question. The railways' dependency on coal for freight, particularly coal transportation for thermal plants, constitutes around 40% of their total freight earnings. Moreover, 100% electrification may not be financially sustainable without the continued use of diesel locomotives for specific strategic and operational needs, including disaster management.

The Redundancy Dilemma: A Waste of Assets

One of the most pressing concerns surrounding the electrification drive is the large number of serviceable diesel locomotives being prematurely decommissioned. Approximately 2,500 diesel locomotives are retained for disaster management, and 1,000 more will remain operational for traffic commitments. However, a large portion of locomotives in serviceable condition are at risk of being scrapped unnecessarily.

Chasing the Mirage of a “Green Railway”

The 100% electrification mission, while noble in its pursuit of reducing carbon emissions, is far from practical under current energy constraints. The heavy reliance on coal for electricity generation undermines the environmental benefits of switching from diesel to electric locomotives. As a result, the true "greenness" of the project remains in question, with the project serving more as a symbolic gesture rather than a solution to India's energy challenges.

Conclusion

India’s 100% electrification mission, while aligned with global green transport initiatives, is fraught with contradictions. The premature scrapping of serviceable diesel locomotives, the reliance on coal for power, and the financial unsustainability of the initiative raise serious concerns. This project exemplifies the pitfalls of pursuing headline-grabbing goals without comprehensive planning or regard for economic and environmental realities. If the mission continues at this pace, it may lead to colossal wastage of public funds, without achieving the desired environmental outcomes.
