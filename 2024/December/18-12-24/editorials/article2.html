Indian PM’s First Visit to Kuwait in Four Decades: A New Era in Middle East Diplomacy

Introduction

Prime Minister Narendra Modi’s visit to Kuwait this weekend will close the last gap in India’s expanded diplomatic engagement with the Gulf region that is vital to the country’s security and prosperity. This visit holds significant importance as it marks a historic moment for India’s foreign relations in the Middle East, further solidifying India's relationship with Kuwait and the wider Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC) countries.

Historical Context and Challenges

India’s Neutral Stance in the Gulf Crises

India’s approach to the Gulf region has been historically neutral, particularly during major regional crises. The 1990 Gulf War is a prime example, where India refrained from unequivocally condemning Saddam Hussein's invasion of Kuwait, largely due to its strategic relationship with Iraq. This cautious stance was also mirrored during the Soviet invasion of Afghanistan in 1979 and Russia’s invasion of Ukraine in 2022. Such neutrality has often shaped India’s diplomatic position, especially regarding conflicts in the Middle East.

Alignment with Ba’athist Leaders

India’s foreign policy during the late 20th century was closely aligned with Ba’athist regimes in Iraq and Syria, as both were viewed as key players in the Arab world. However, this alignment, especially with Iraq under Saddam Hussein, resulted in strained relations with Kuwait during the 1990-91 Gulf crisis. After Prime Minister Indira Gandhi's visit to Kuwait in 1981, high-level exchanges were limited until Vice President Hamid Ansari’s visit in 2009.

Transformation of India-Gulf Relations

Key Areas of Enhanced Engagement

  • Economic Ties: Trade relations between India and the Gulf have seen significant growth, particularly in energy exports and labor migration.
  • Security Partnerships: India and Gulf countries have strengthened cooperation in counterterrorism efforts and defense.
  • Connectivity Projects: Collaborative infrastructure initiatives such as ports and regional development projects have bolstered bilateral relations.

Geopolitical Alignment Post-Assad Era

The fall of Syria’s Assad regime has further altered the geopolitical dynamics in the Gulf. With the diminishing influence of Ba’athist ideologies, India’s relations with Kuwait and other moderate Arab states have entered a new phase of cooperation. These nations are seen as key allies for India, especially in the context of shared interests in modernization and stability.

Role of Moderate Arab States

Reform-Oriented Monarchies

Countries like Saudi Arabia and the UAE have embraced reforms focused on religious moderation, social modernization, and diversifying their economies away from oil dependency. These shifts in policy have aligned with India’s priorities, paving the way for stronger diplomatic and economic ties. India is increasingly viewed as a strategic partner by these Gulf monarchies, due to shared goals of modernization and stability.

Shifting Dynamics with Pakistan

Pakistan’s influence in the Gulf region has waned over the years, while India’s relations with these moderate Arab states have flourished. The changing dynamics have allowed India to foster stronger ties with key Gulf nations such as Kuwait, which further underscores the transformation of India’s diplomatic engagement in the region.

Challenges and Opportunities for India

Addressing Regional Contradictions

India faces a delicate balancing act in the Gulf region. The relationships between Iran, Israel, and Turkey remain complex, with ideological differences influencing diplomatic and economic engagements. Additionally, the Palestinian issue continues to be a point of contention for several Gulf states. India’s approach must consider these sensitivities while also strengthening ties with Israel and Arab nations.

Leveraging the Abraham Accords

The Abraham Accords, which have led to the normalization of relations between Israel and several Arab states, provide India with an opportunity to deepen its strategic partnerships. India must work closely with Israel and moderate Arab states to advance regional peace and stability, leveraging these agreements to promote broader regional cooperation.

Conclusion

Prime Minister Modi’s visit to Kuwait signifies a crucial step in India’s evolving role in the Middle East. By focusing on fostering stronger relationships with moderate Arab states and navigating the complexities of regional diplomacy, India can play a pivotal role in shaping the future of the Gulf region. While the Abraham Accords provide a promising framework for peace, India’s diplomatic efforts must continue to be guided by a nuanced understanding of regional dynamics and constitutional principles. This visit is just the beginning of India’s deeper engagement in the Middle East.
