POSH Act and Political Parties

The Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace (Prevention, Prohibition, and Redressal) Act (POSH Act), 2013, addresses sexual harassment in the workplace. A recent PIL urges the Actâ€™s extension to political parties, highlighting the lack of Internal Complaints Committees (ICCs) in most political organizations.

Key Provisions of the POSH Act

  • It applies to all women in workplaces, both public and private.
  • Organisations with 10+ employees must establish ICCs with a majority of female members.
  • Complaints must be filed within 3 months of an incident, and investigations must conclude within 90 days.
  • It aims to create safer environments for women in workspaces, addressing unwanted sexual conduct.

Political Parties and the POSH Act

Although the Kerala High Court ruled that political parties are not required to form ICCs, there are discussions about whether the Act could apply to political parties. The issue revolves around defining "workplace" and "employer" in the context of party workers and volunteers.

Challenges and Uncertainties

Political parties have no clear employer-employee relationship, complicating the implementation of the POSH Act. Furthermore, the Election Commission of India (ECI) has no clear role in enforcing compliance with the POSH Act in political parties.
