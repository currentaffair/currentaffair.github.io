Air Pollution and Health Risks

A recent Lancet study has highlighted the devastating impact of air pollution, with 1.5 million deaths annually linked to high PM2.5 levels in India. No area in India meets the WHO PM2.5 standard, and 81.9% of the population lives in areas exceeding the NAAQS of 40 µg/m³.

Key Findings

  • WHO PM2.5 Standard: No area in India meets the recommended level of 5 µg/m³.
  • Health Impact: Long-term exposure increases risks of respiratory diseases, heart attacks, and developmental delays in children.
  • Risk Increase: A 10 µg/m³ increase in PM2.5 levels correlates with an 8.6% higher risk of death.
  • Regional Pollution Levels: Areas like Ghaziabad and Delhi exceed safe limits, with pollution levels over 119 µg/m³.
  • Potential for Improvement: Meeting NAAQS could prevent 0.3 million deaths annually.

Sources of Pollution

Air pollution is caused by multiple sources, including household fuel burning, industrial emissions, traffic exhausts, and crop burning. Identifying these sources is crucial for improving air quality.

Air Quality Guidelines

The WHO updated its air quality guidelines in 2021, recommending levels to protect public health. These guidelines include limits for PM, O3, NO2, and SO2.

National Ambient Air Quality Standards (NAAQS)

In India, the CPCB is responsible for setting air quality standards under the Air (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1981. These standards aim to mitigate the health impacts of air pollution.
