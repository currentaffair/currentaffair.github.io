EPA's Ban on TCE and PCE

Context

The US Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) banned Trichloroethylene (TCE) and Perchloroethylene (PCE) under the 2016 Toxic Substances Control Act (TSCA). This decision supports the Cancer Moonshot initiative aiming to reduce cancer rates in the US.

Details

  • All manufacturing, processing, and consumer uses of TCE are banned.
  • Many commercial uses of PCE are prohibited, with limited industrial applications allowed under strict safety guidelines.

Key Chemicals

Trichloroethylene (TCE): Used for degreasing metal parts, adhesives, and paint removers. It is toxic with potential carcinogenic effects.

Perchloroethylene (PCE): Primarily used in dry cleaning, textile processing, and metal degreasing. It also poses risks to liver and kidney health.

Health Effects

TCE: Short-term exposure causes dizziness and nausea. Long-term exposure can result in liver and kidney damage, and cancer.

PCE: Short-term exposure irritates the skin, eyes, and respiratory system. Long-term exposure may lead to kidney damage and increased cancer risk.

Environmental Impact

Both chemicals contaminate groundwater, causing long-lasting environmental damage. The Woburn, Massachusetts case exemplifies TCE's harmful effects on communities.

Regulatory Framework

  • EPA: Both TCE and PCE are classified as probable human carcinogens.
  • WHO: Drinking water guidelines for TCE (20 μg/L) and PCE (40 μg/L).

Alternatives

For TCE: Water-based and safer solvent alternatives like alcohols.

For PCE: Hydrocarbon-based solvents for dry cleaning.
