Ken-Betwa River Interlinking Project: Ecological and Political Implications

Introduction

The Ken-Betwa River Interlinking Project, launched by Prime Minister Narendra Modi, aims to transfer water from the Ken basin to the Betwa basin to support agriculture and settlements. Despite its ₹44,605 crore budget, the project faces strong opposition due to environmental concerns, legal violations, and questions of sustainability. This has raised doubts about its ecological and socio-political implications.

Ken-Betwa River Interlinking Project

Flagging off the work on the Ken-Betwa river interlinking project, Prime Minister Narendra Modi indicated that the national government is largely unaffected by the widespread opposition to it.

Project Budget: ₹44,605 crore.

Scope: The project aims to draw water away from the Ken river basin and redirect it towards the Betwa river basin, with the intention of benefiting farmland and human settlements.

Approval Amid Ongoing Deliberations

When the Union Cabinet approved the project in 2021, the National Green Tribunal was still deliberating a challenge to its green clearance. This approval came despite critical comments from experts, including members of an empowered committee appointed by the Supreme Court, and bypassed due process.

Legal and Environmental Violations

The law has strict terms for allowing hydroelectric power projects in ecologically sensitive areas. The Daudhan Dam will be erected inside the Panna Tiger Reserve, raising concerns about ecological impact. Work on the dam will destroy thousands of trees and destabilize fragile ecosystems.

The government has also refused to release hydrological data of the basins, citing the sensitivity of the data as a subset of the international Ganga basin.

Sustainability Concerns

While it is undeniable that a river interlink can provide agricultural and water benefits, its long-term sustainability is uncertain. Both the Ken and Betwa basins are prone to floods and droughts, and studies suggest that the interlinking may alter rainfall and sedimentation patterns across the subcontinent.

Furthermore, experts argue that the Betwa basin could be replenished more affordably by maintaining environmental flows and bolstering natural storage instead of relying on the interlinking project.

Government’s Principal Claim

The government claims that the Ken basin is water-surplus, while the Betwa basin is water-deficient. However, experts argue that this reasoning is misleading, as the Betwa basin’s deficiency is due to the extensive irrigated cropland, and an increase in demand in the Ken basin will lead to scarcity in both regions.

Experts suggest that the project may be a political maneuver to pacify the electorate in Bundelkhand, especially given its approval just months before state elections in Uttar Pradesh.

Way Forward

The project appears more driven by political expediency and self-image than by ecological considerations. As the government sinks more resources into it, the likelihood of altering or reversing course becomes more unlikely in the face of any adverse developments.

When negative consequences inevitably arise, the burden of mitigating these will fall to the people. This is also true for consequences from other projects like the Parbati-Kalisindh-Chambal link.

Conclusion

The Ken-Betwa River Interlinking Project promises agricultural and water benefits, but faces significant criticism for its potential ecological damage, lack of transparency, and apparent political motives. Given the fragile ecosystems at risk and the questions surrounding its sustainability, the project's long-term viability remains uncertain. Balancing development with environmental preservation, while addressing expert concerns, is essential to ensure its success without jeopardizing future generations.
