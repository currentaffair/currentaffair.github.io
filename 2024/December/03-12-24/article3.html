State Commissioners and Disability Rights in India

Introduction

The 2011 Census of India revealed that persons with disabilities constitute 2.21% of the total population, a figure that is widely considered underestimated. According to the 2019 Brief Disability Model Survey conducted by the World Health Organization (WHO), the prevalence of severe disability among Indian adults is as high as 16%. These figures underscore the importance of robust measures to safeguard disability rights.

India ratified the United Nations Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (UNCRPD) in 2007. In response, the Rights of Persons with Disabilities Act (RPWD Act) 2016 was enacted, replacing the outdated 1995 legislation. This law aims to align Indian disability rights with international standards, promoting a social and human rights model.

The Role of State Commissioners

The RPWD Act introduced the office of State Commissioners for Disabilities, a unique feature among developing countries. These commissioners are entrusted with review, monitoring, and quasi-judicial functions to ensure effective implementation of the law.

Quasi-Judicial Powers: Section 82 of the RPWD Act grants State Commissioners the powers of a civil court under the Civil Procedure Code, 1908. Their proceedings are deemed judicial under Sections 193 and 228 of the Indian Penal Code.

Challenges in Implementation

Appointment Issues

The effectiveness of State Commissioners has been hampered by the appointment of civil servants rather than independent experts. Only 8 States have appointed commissioners outside the mainstream civil service, as per the 2021-22 report of the Chief Commissioner for Persons with Disabilities. This undermines the impartiality needed for oversight and accountability.

Lax Government Support

State governments often delay appointments or fail to empower commissioners, as highlighted in the Seema Girija Lal vs. Union of India writ petition (WPC 29329/2021). Such inaction erodes trust in these statutory offices.

Limited Engagement

State Commissioners rarely engage with persons with disabilities and their organizations, limiting their understanding of discriminatory policies and practices.

Recommendations for Improvement

Inclusive Appointments

States should appoint representatives from civil society, particularly women with disabilities, to address intersectional discrimination.

Capacity Building

Training for commissioners, as seen in Karnataka, can enhance their quasi-judicial capabilities. Collaborations with law schools and legal experts are crucial.

Innovative Practices

Mobile Adalats, pioneered in Karnataka, can bring justice to remote areas. These courts address grievances on the spot, increasing accessibility for persons with disabilities.

Monitoring Mechanisms

The District Disability Management Review (DDMR) in Karnataka exemplifies effective monitoring of disability-related programmes. Such models should be adopted nationwide to ensure accountability.

Conclusion

State Commissioners play a pivotal role in safeguarding disability rights. However, their effectiveness depends on proper appointments, capacity building, and innovative practices. By fostering stakeholder engagement and adopting inclusive policies, India can uphold the dignity and rights of its disabled population while meeting international commitments.
