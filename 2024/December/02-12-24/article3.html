Impact of Air Pollution on Schools: Analysis and Solutions

Introduction

In November 2024, Delhi’s decision to shift schools to online learning under the Graded Response Action Plan (GRAP), triggered by severe air pollution, ignited widespread debate. While GRAP aims to mitigate poor air quality, school closures have raised questions about their scientific validity, practicality, and socio-economic consequences. This article examines the multi-faceted impacts of such measures and explores sustainable alternatives.

Understanding Delhi’s Air Quality Crisis

Air Quality Index (AQI): Air pollution begins harming health when AQI exceeds 50 under Indian norms. Levels between 51 and 399, though normalized, also pose health risks. GRAP focuses primarily on AQI above 400 (‘severe’ or ‘severe plus’), overlooking persistent harm from lower levels.

Delhi’s Air Quality in Numbers:

  • 2024: No ‘good’ air quality days recorded so far.
  • 2023: Only one day with ‘good’ air quality.
The near-constant exposure of children to harmful air calls for structural, long-term solutions instead of temporary fixes like school closures.

Impacts of School Closures

Learning and Development Loss:

  • Online education struggles to replicate in-person classroom experiences, critical for engagement and critical thinking.
  • Young children face long-term developmental delays due to screen-based learning.

Social and Emotional Development: Schools play a pivotal role in providing life skills, emotional support, and social interaction, which online learning cannot replace. Prolonged isolation, as seen during COVID-19, disrupts socialization.

Equity Issues

Digital Divide: Marginalized families lack stable internet access and digital devices, widening educational disparities.

Nutritional Impact: For underprivileged children, mid-day meals at schools are a vital source of nutrition. School closures exacerbate malnutrition and inequality.

Science-Based Alternatives

  • Keep Schools Functional: Suspend outdoor activities like sports or assemblies during poor AQI days. Upgrade classrooms with air purifiers and proper ventilation.
  • Customized Mask Usage: Avoid blanket mandates. Use N95 masks selectively for children above 12 under medical advice.
  • Hybrid Options: Allow parents to opt-out of physical attendance while maintaining in-person learning for others.
  • Enhance Monitoring: Regularly monitor indoor air quality in schools to ensure safety compliance.

Long-Term Strategies

Strengthen Healthcare: Annual health check-ups and vaccinations for pollution-aggravated diseases like influenza and pneumonia.

Community Awareness: Educate families about air quality, mask usage, and preventive measures.

Infrastructure Investments: Subsidized air purifiers and green barriers to improve indoor air quality.

Global Best Practices

  • Beijing, China: Schools suspend outdoor activities during severe pollution but keep indoor learning active.
  • South Korea: Mandates high-efficiency air purifiers in classrooms, subsidizing installation and maintenance.
  • Germany: Encourages green barriers like hedges to filter particulate matter near schools.
  • California, USA: Parents can keep children at home on poor AQI days while schools remain open for others.

Conclusion

As Nelson Mandela once said, “There can be no keener revelation of a society’s soul than the way in which it treats its children.” Addressing air pollution while safeguarding children’s education and well-being is critical. A balanced, long-term approach combining health-focused infrastructure, inclusive policies, and community engagement is essential to create resilient and child-friendly cities.
