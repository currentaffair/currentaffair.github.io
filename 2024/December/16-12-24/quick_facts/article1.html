Can We Make Black Holes Reveal Themselves in Echoes of Light?

A recent study proposes a method to measure black hole properties using light echoes, a phenomenon predicted by Einstein’s general relativity.

What are Light Echoes?

When light passes near a black hole, its path bends, creating time-delayed "echoes" that depend on the black hole’s mass and spin.

New Measurement Technique

Traditional methods face interference from hot gases around black holes. Light echoes offer a clearer signal for determining black hole mass and angular momentum.

Testing the Method

Using simulations and data from the Event Horizon Telescope, researchers analyzed light around the M87 black hole, measuring time delays and correlating them to the black hole's characteristics.

Technological Requirements

Detecting light echoes requires long-baseline interferometry, using telescopes spaced far apart, including one on Earth and one in space.

Implications

This method could confirm general relativity's predictions about black holes, providing a new approach to studying spacetime geometry and supermassive black holes.
