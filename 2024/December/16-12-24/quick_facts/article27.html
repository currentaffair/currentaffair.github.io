SC Prioritising SLPs Disposal

Overview

The Supreme Court (SC) is prioritizing Special Leave Petitions (SLPs) to reduce the backlog of cases. As of December 2024, over 82,000 cases are pending in the SC, prompting the Chief Justice of India (CJI) to adopt this strategy.

What is an SLP?

SLP is a discretionary appeal mechanism under Article 136 of the Constitution of India, allowing the SC to hear appeals against judgments or orders of lower courts or tribunals. It is not applicable to Armed Forces Tribunals.

Key Features

SLPs allow the SC to address cases where no direct appeal exists. It is granted at the SC's discretion, applicable to both civil and criminal matters. It is used for cases involving substantial legal questions or miscarriage of justice.

Filing an SLP

Any aggrieved party can file an SLP within 90 days of a High Court judgment, or 60 days if the certificate of fitness for appeal is refused. The SLP procedure enables a detailed examination of the case by the SC.

Notable SC Cases on SLPs

Several cases have shaped the approach towards SLPs:

  • Laxmi & Co. v. Anand R. Deshpande (1972): SC may consider developments during appeals to expedite proceedings.
  • Kerala State v. Kunhayammed (2000): Declining an SLP doesn't invoke SC's appellate jurisdiction.
  • Pritam Singh v. The State (1950): SC should exercise its powers sparingly, intervening only in exceptional cases.
  • N. Suriyakala v. A. Mohandoss & Ors. (2007): Article 136 grants broad discretionary powers, not a right of appeal.
