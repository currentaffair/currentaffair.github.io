Critical Analysis: Women’s Safety and Workforce Participation

Context:

The brutal killing of a female doctor at Kolkata’s R.G. Kar Hospital on August 9 has triggered the usual response of empty words and tired guidelines. The West Bengal government has issued guidelines, including minimizing night shifts for women, which raises important questions about safety and gender equality.

Relevance:

Examined under the following key areas of General Studies:

  • GS2: Welfare Schemes for Vulnerable Sections, and the Performance of these Schemes
  • GS2: Issues Relating to Development and Management of Human Resources
  • GS3: Growth, Development, and Employment

Mains Question:

In light of the recent violence against women in their workplaces, reducing work hours of women and minimizing night shifts for them presents a way to ensure their safety. Comment critically. (15 Marks, 250 Words).

Women in Workforce:

  • The Worker Population Ratio (WPR) for women aged 15 years and above was estimated at 28.7% in 2019-20, according to the National Statistical Office (NSO).
  • The Labour Force Participation Rate (LFPR) for women reached 32.8% in 2021-22, but remains below the global average of 47%.
  • The female LFPR in China is 60%, while in South Asian neighbors like Sri Lanka and Bangladesh, it is higher than India's.

Avoiding Night Duty - A Regressive Approach:

  • How does the guideline to “avoid night duty for women wherever possible” improve workplace safety?
  • This regressive approach may push women out of the workforce, rather than addressing the root causes of workplace violence.
  • The Labour Force Participation Rate for urban women aged 15 and above in India was only 25.2% in April-June 2024, according to the Periodic Labour Force Survey. Reducing work hours risks diminishing their financial independence.
  • Other measures, such as safe zones, CCTV surveillance, and separate restrooms for women, should already have been in place to ensure safety without discrimination.

Existing Notifications on the Issue:

  • States like Telangana, Madhya Pradesh, Tamil Nadu, and Haryana allow women to work night shifts under regulated conditions.
  • States like Himachal Pradesh, Odisha, and Uttar Pradesh outline conditions for exemptions to the Factories Act, ensuring women's equal opportunities in night shifts.
  • Legal provisions that restrict female night shifts have been criticized as unconstitutional, with the Madras High Court striking down such laws in 1999.
  • Ensuring workplace diversity requires equal opportunities for all genders, including women in night shifts.

Way Forward:

  • The Supreme Court's national task force, formed after the Kolkata case, must address the larger issue of gender violence across sectors.
  • Past reforms after high-profile cases like the 2012 Delhi rape incident have been inadequate to prevent crimes against women, with over 4.45 lakh cases still registered in recent years.
  • Chief Justice D.Y. Chandrachud emphasizes that safety protocols must be actionable, not just words on paper.
  • Public awareness and societal shifts are critical to promoting gender justice, as highlighted in key court judgments.

Conclusion:

The "reclaim the night" campaigns reflect a strong demand for action on women’s rights and safety. Governments must heed these calls to ensure equality, security, and meaningful opportunities for all women.
