India’s Manufacturing Sector: Growth, Challenges, and Strategic Solutions

Introduction

India stands at a critical juncture in its manufacturing journey, with an ambitious vision to elevate the sector's contribution to GDP from 15% to 25%. The introduction of the Machinery and Electrical Equipment Safety (Omnibus Technical Regulation) Order, 2024, marks a transformative approach to standardizing product quality, enhancing global competitiveness, and creating a robust ecosystem for industrial growth. This intervention reflects India's commitment to fostering an innovative, sustainable, and globally competitive manufacturing sector.

Current Status of India’s Manufacturing Sector

Manufacturing plays a pivotal role in India’s economic growth, contributing significantly to GDP and employment. Prior to the pandemic, it contributed around 16-17% to GDP and employed over 27 million workers. The government envisions increasing this share to 25% by 2025, with India planning to add more than $500 billion annually to the global economy by 2030.

The sector has demonstrated robust growth, with the HSBC Manufacturing PMI reaching a 16-year high of 59.1 in March 2024. Manufacturing exports reached an all-time high of $447.46 billion in FY23, showing a growth rate of 6.03% over the previous year. Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) in the manufacturing sector has also increased significantly, reaching $165.1 billion, a 69% increase over the last decade.

Key Growth Drivers of India's Manufacturing Sector

Government Initiatives and Policy Reforms

The Indian government has launched flagship initiatives such as “Make in India” and “Gati Shakti” to boost manufacturing competitiveness and infrastructure. The Production Linked Incentive (PLI) scheme, covering 14 sectors, is expected to generate $500 billion in manufacturing output. In FY22, India’s manufacturing sector attracted $21.34 billion in investments, reflecting the effectiveness of these policies.

Rising Domestic Demand

The growing middle class, urbanization, and rising disposable incomes in India are driving demand for automobiles, consumer goods, and electronics. The electronics market alone is expected to reach $400 billion by 2025. Additionally, the auto sector is benefiting from government incentives, including the Faster Adoption and Manufacturing of Electric Vehicles (FAME) scheme, with significant growth in 2023.

Technological Advancements

The adoption of automation, IoT, AI, and robotics is transforming Indian manufacturing into a high-value, technology-driven sector. More than two-thirds of Indian manufacturers are expected to embrace Industry 4.0 by 2025, with the government’s Digital India Campaign complementing this digital transformation.

Rising Investments in Green Manufacturing

Focus on sustainability is also driving green manufacturing practices. The National Green Hydrogen Mission aims to produce 5 million metric tons of green hydrogen annually by 2030, and India’s renewable energy capacity stands at 125 GW in 2023.

Challenges Facing India’s Manufacturing Sector

Weak Infrastructure and Logistics Bottlenecks

Despite government initiatives like “Gati Shakti,” India continues to struggle with poor logistics infrastructure. Logistics costs account for 14-18% of GDP, much higher than the 8-10% in developed nations. Power outages and inadequate transport networks hinder the efficiency of manufacturing and production processes.

Policy Inconsistencies and Bureaucratic Hurdles

Frequent changes in trade and taxation policies, combined with bureaucratic hurdles and complex land acquisition laws, create an unstable investment climate. India’s position in the Ease of Doing Business rankings (63rd in 2020) highlights these issues.

Labor Market Challenges

The informal workforce dominates Indian manufacturing, leading to inefficiencies. Additionally, the lack of implementation of the Four Labour Codes restricts scalability and flexibility in the industry.

Conclusion

India’s manufacturing sector holds immense potential to drive economic growth and global competitiveness. However, overcoming key challenges such as weak infrastructure, policy inconsistencies, and technological lag is critical for aligning the sector with global standards. The government’s ongoing initiatives, including the PLI scheme and green manufacturing efforts, lay a strong foundation for sustainable growth in this sector.
