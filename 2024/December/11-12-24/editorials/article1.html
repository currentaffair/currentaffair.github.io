National Mission for Natural Farming (NMNF) - A Transformative Agricultural Approach

Introduction

The National Mission for Natural Farming (NMNF) was launched by the Government of India on November 25, 2024, to reduce the country's dependency on chemical fertilizers and promote organic farming practices. The mission aims to support one crore farmers in transitioning to organic methods by using cow dung-based manure and other locally available non-chemical fertilizers. This initiative also integrates urban waste management systems under the Swachh Bharat Mission (SBM), which promises to be a novel solution to both agricultural and waste management challenges.

Challenges in NMNF Implementation

Exclusion of Urban Organic Compost

India generates 5.8 crore tonnes of solid waste annually, with the potential to produce 1 crore tonnes of organic compost. Despite this, urban compost from segregated waste has not been incorporated into NMNF, which could meet the manure needs of 15-20 lakh farmers each year.

Environmental Impact of Current Waste Management

Much of the municipal waste is unprocessed and ends up in landfills, particularly near rural areas. This contributes to environmental degradation and methane emissions. Urban Local Bodies (ULBs) process only 30-40% of waste, relying heavily on operational subsidies to maintain their facilities.

Farmers' Transportation Costs

Farmers require 2-3 tonnes of organic manure per acre, a significant amount compared to the 100-150 kg of chemical fertilizers traditionally used. Despite being more affordable (₹2,000-3,000 per tonne), the transportation of organic manure remains a major cost barrier for farmers.

City-Farmer Partnership Model

The City-Farmer Partnership Model links segregated urban wet waste with farms, facilitating direct composting on-site. This model offers an innovative solution to waste management while promoting sustainable agriculture.

Key Benefits:

  • For Farmers: Access to free organic manure, reducing transportation costs and enhancing soil health.
  • For ULBs: Savings on operational subsidies and improved waste processing efficiency.
  • Environmental Impact: Reduction in landfill waste and greenhouse gas emissions.

Case Study: Chikkaballapura, Karnataka

In Chikkaballapura, the City-Farmer Partnership for Solid Waste Management (SWM) supplied 2,300 tonnes of segregated waste to over 200 farmers, producing 600 tonnes of compost. This initiative reduced chemical fertilizer use by 50-60 tonnes and improved soil health through regular testing before and after composting.

Challenges and Solutions

Waste Segregation

Challenge: Effective segregation of wet waste at the source remains a significant hurdle. Solution: Public awareness campaigns and strict enforcement of segregation norms can help address this challenge.

Farmer Training

Challenge: Farmers often lack the capacity for on-farm composting. Solution: Extensive training programs through self-help groups (SHGs), NGOs, and government bodies will empower farmers to adopt organic methods.

Coordination Between Stakeholders

Challenge: Effective collaboration between ULBs, farmers, and waste-processing agencies. Solution: Establishing strong local partnerships and incentivizing farmer participation can help ensure smooth operations.

International Best Practices

Countries such as Germany, Japan, Sweden, and Singapore have implemented advanced waste management systems that focus on recycling organic waste for sustainable agriculture. Germany uses a circular economy model, Japan promotes decentralized composting, Sweden adopts bio-cycle farming, and Singapore has set up community composting hubs in urban areas.

Way Forward

Policy Recommendations:

  • Integrate urban compost into NMNF to meet the manure needs of farmers.
  • Promote decentralized waste management and city-farmer partnerships for localized composting solutions.
  • Strengthen farmer training programs in composting techniques and soil health management.
  • Enhance waste segregation practices through public awareness and infrastructure investment.
  • Provide subsidies for composting infrastructure and reduce operational costs for ULBs.

Conclusion

The National Mission for Natural Farming (NMNF) and the City-Farmer Partnership Model present a transformative approach to tackling India’s agricultural and environmental challenges. However, achieving NMNF’s goal of supporting one crore farmers requires collaboration across government agencies, urban bodies, and local communities to ensure a sustainable outcome for agriculture, urban governance, and the environment.
