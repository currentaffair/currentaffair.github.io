Rapid Expansion of Himalayan Glacial Lakes

Introduction

The National Green Tribunal (NGT) has recently drawn attention to the rapid expansion of Himalayan glacial lakes, which have grown by approximately 10.81% over the past 13 years. This alarming development is primarily attributed to rising temperatures and global warming, triggering increased glacier melting. The NGT's notice to the central government sheds light on the need for greater awareness and action to address this growing threat, as the expansion of glacial lakes poses severe risks to both infrastructure and human life in the region.

What Are Glacial Lakes?

A glacial lake is a body of water formed from a glacier, typically located at its base, although it can develop within, on, or beneath the glacier. These lakes are formed when glaciers erode the land, creating depressions that are filled by meltwater as the glacier retreats. Natural dams, consisting of ice or moraines, can form glacial lakes, but these dams are often unstable and prone to failure, leading to potential flooding.

Glacial Lake Expansion in India

According to a report, the surface area of glacial lakes in India has increased by 33.7% from 2011 to 2024. The report also identifies 67 lakes as high-risk for Glacial Lake Outburst Floods (GLOFs), particularly in regions such as Ladakh, Himachal Pradesh, Uttarakhand, Sikkim, and Arunachal Pradesh. The rapid expansion of these lakes poses significant threats to human life, infrastructure, and local ecosystems.

Causes of Glacial Lake Expansion

The increase in glacial lake size is largely driven by global warming, which is accelerating glacier melting in the Himalayas. As glaciers retreat, they contribute water to lakes and expose new land surfaces, promoting the formation of additional glacial lakes. Furthermore, thawing permafrost, which creates depressions that collect water, is also expanding these lakes as it loses its natural drainage barrier.

What is a GLOF?

A Glacial Lake Outburst Flood (GLOF) occurs when a glacial lake's dam fails, releasing large volumes of water, often due to rapid glacier melting or heavy rainfall. These floods can also be triggered by fluctuations in lake water levels, glacier volume changes, or even seismic activity. GLOFs pose significant risks to downstream communities and ecosystems.

Historical Instances of GLOFs

  • June 2013, Uttarakhand: Unusual rainfall led to the melting of the Chorabari glacier, resulting in the eruption of the Mandakini River.
  • August 2014, Ladakh: A GLOF struck the village of Gya, causing widespread damage.
  • October 2023, Sikkim: South Lhonak Lake ruptured due to continuous rainfall, causing significant flooding.

Impacts of Glacial Lake Expansion

Impact on Communities and Ecosystems

Downstream communities are facing displacement, loss of life, and destruction of property, especially in areas with inadequate monitoring systems. Agriculture, a vital livelihood for many, is severely affected by the floods caused by GLOFs. The NGT report emphasizes the need for stronger enforcement of disaster preparedness laws, as many high-risk lakes in Ladakh, Himachal Pradesh, and Uttarakhand remain poorly monitored.

Vulnerability of Infrastructure

Critical infrastructure, including roads, bridges, and hydropower plants, is vulnerable to damage caused by GLOFs. This vulnerability leads to significant economic losses, delays in development projects, and complications in managing regional infrastructure.

Climate Change and Its Role

The rapid expansion of glacial lakes is directly linked to climate change. As global temperatures rise, glaciers are melting at unprecedented rates, leading to the formation of more glacial lakes. The Himalayan glaciers, vital for rivers like the Yangtze and Ganges, support over a billion people, making the ongoing changes to these glaciers crucial for both environmental stability and human livelihoods.

Risk Mitigation Strategies

Enhanced Monitoring Systems

Establishing comprehensive monitoring systems is essential. Satellite surveillance, along with ground-based assessments, can track changes in the volume and surface area of glacial lakes, helping to identify potential risks early on.

Early Warning Systems

Developing and integrating early warning systems can greatly reduce disaster risks. By combining meteorological data with hydrological models, these systems can predict potential GLOFs and communicate risks effectively to local communities.

Transboundary Water Management

International cooperation is necessary for managing the water resources of transboundary rivers. Sharing data and resources between countries can help mitigate the risks of glacial lake expansion, promoting a collaborative response to this global issue.

Conclusion

The rapid expansion of Himalayan glacial lakes poses a growing threat to communities, ecosystems, and infrastructure in the region. Climate change is driving the accelerated melting of glaciers, leading to the formation of new lakes and increased risks of GLOFs. To address these challenges, a multi-faceted approach involving enhanced monitoring, early warning systems, and international cooperation is essential.
