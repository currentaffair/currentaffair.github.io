National Mission for Natural Farming and City-Farmer Partnership

Introduction

On November 25, 2024, the Government of India launched the National Mission for Natural Farming (NMNF) to reduce dependency on chemical fertilisers and promote organic farming among one crore farmers. The mission aims to train and support farmers transitioning to organic farming, with a special focus on using cow dung-based manure and other locally available non-chemical fertilisers. A key part of the mission is its potential integration with urban waste management systems under the Swachh Bharat Mission (SBM), offering an innovative solution that addresses challenges in both agriculture and waste management.

Challenges in NMNF Implementation

While NMNF has a noble vision, several challenges hamper its effective implementation:

  • Exclusion of Urban Organic Compost: India generates 5.8 crore tonnes of solid waste annually, which could produce up to 1 crore tonnes of organic compost. However, this urban compost is not integrated into NMNF, limiting its potential impact on farmers.
  • Environmental Impact of Current Waste Management: Unprocessed municipal waste, often sent to landfills, not only leads to environmental degradation but also causes methane emissions. The process of waste management is currently inefficient, with only 30-40% of waste being processed.
  • Farmers’ Transportation Costs: Organic manure requires 2-3 tonnes per acre, significantly higher than the 100-150 kg of chemical fertilisers. The cost of transporting organic manure remains a challenge despite its lower price.

City-Farmer Partnership Model

Concept

The City-Farmer Partnership Model links segregated urban wet waste with farmlands, allowing composting on-site at farms. This model not only solves waste management problems but also promotes sustainable farming.

Operational Framework

Waste Collection and Supply: Urban Local Bodies (ULBs) deliver segregated wet waste directly to farmlands, bypassing traditional waste-processing plants.

On-Farm Composting: Farmers use traditional pit composting methods, combining wet waste with cow dung slurry to produce compost within 2-3 months.

Impact Metrics: A city with 1 lakh people generates 10-15 tonnes of wet waste daily, which could produce enough compost for one farmer’s crop cycle.

Key Benefits

  • Farmers: Access to free organic manure at the farm, reducing transportation costs and improving soil health.
  • ULBs: Savings on operational subsidies, reduced methane emissions, and improved waste processing efficiency.
  • Environmental Impact: Less waste in landfills and a reduction in greenhouse gas emissions, aligning with SBM and NMNF objectives.

Case Study: Chikkaballapura, Karnataka

In Chikkaballapura, the City-Farmer Partnership Model successfully supplied 2,300 tonnes of segregated wet waste to over 200 farmers. This produced 600 tonnes of organic compost, leading to a 50-60 tonne reduction in chemical fertiliser use and improved soil health through rigorous testing.

Challenges and Solutions

Waste Segregation

Challenge: Effective segregation of wet waste at the source remains a significant hurdle.

Solution: Public awareness campaigns and strict enforcement of segregation norms can address this.

Farmer Training

Challenge: Limited capacity for on-farm composting.

Solution: Training initiatives led by self-help groups, NGOs, and government agencies are crucial.

Coordination Between Stakeholders

Challenge: Ensuring seamless collaboration between ULBs, farmers, and waste-processing agencies.

Solution: Local partnerships, coupled with farmer incentives, are key to addressing this challenge.

International Best Practices

Several countries have adopted innovative waste management practices that can serve as models for India:

  • Germany’s focus on circular economy and organic waste conversion into high-quality compost and biogas.
  • Japan’s decentralized Takakura Composting Method widely adopted in urban areas.
  • Sweden’s bio-cycle farming model using urban organic waste to produce bio-fertiliser and biogas.
  • Singapore’s community-level composting hubs to manage organic waste sustainably.

Way Forward

Policy Recommendations

  • Integrate Urban Compost: Include urban wet waste composting into NMNF to meet the manure needs of farmers.
  • Decentralized Waste Management: Promote city-farmer partnerships for localized composting solutions.
  • Capacity Building: Enhance farmer training on composting and soil health management.
  • Financial Incentives: Provide subsidies for composting infrastructure and reduce operational costs for ULBs.

Conclusion

The National Mission for Natural Farming (NMNF) and the City-Farmer Partnership Model represent a transformative approach to addressing India’s agricultural and environmental challenges. Achieving NMNF’s target of supporting one crore farmers requires a collaborative effort across government agencies, ULBs, and local communities, ensuring a win-win outcome for agriculture, urban governance, and the environment.
