National Mission for Natural Farming and Urban Waste Management

Introduction

On November 25, 2024, the Government of India launched the National Mission for Natural Farming (NMNF). This mission aims to reduce dependency on chemical fertilizers and promote organic farming across the country. By focusing on cow dung-based manure and locally available non-chemical fertilizers, NMNF seeks to empower one crore farmers to transition to sustainable agricultural practices.

An important aspect of this mission is the integration with urban waste management systems, specifically under the Swachh Bharat Mission (SBM). This innovative approach addresses dual challenges of agricultural sustainability and waste management simultaneously.

Challenges in NMNF Implementation

Exclusion of Urban Organic Compost

India generates over 5.8 crore tonnes of solid waste annually, with the potential to produce 1 crore tonnes of organic compost. However, urban compost from segregated waste is not currently integrated into the NMNF framework. Incorporating this urban compost could significantly address manure needs for 15-20 lakh farmers annually.

Environmental Impact of Current Waste Management

Unprocessed municipal waste often ends up in landfills near rural areas, exacerbating environmental degradation and contributing to methane emissions. Urban Local Bodies (ULBs) process only 30-40% of waste, heavily relying on subsidies to keep waste-processing facilities functional.

Farmers' Transportation Costs

Organic manure requirements far exceed the amount of chemical fertilizers needed for crops. This disparity in requirements makes transportation costs a major barrier to organic farming. City-Farmer Partnership Model

Concept and Operational Framework

The City-Farmer Partnership model aims to bridge the gap between urban waste management and sustainable farming. By linking segregated urban wet waste with farmlands, this model allows direct composting on farms. It not only resolves waste management issues but also promotes sustainable agriculture practices.

Urban Local Bodies (ULBs) can deliver segregated wet waste directly to farms, bypassing waste processing plants. On farms, traditional pit composting methods can be employed to create organic compost within 2-3 months.

Impact Metrics

A city with a population of 1 lakh generates 10-15 tonnes of wet waste daily, which can produce enough compost for a single farmer's crop cycle. This process can significantly improve agricultural sustainability by reducing dependency on chemical fertilizers.

Key Benefits

  • For Farmers: Access to free organic manure, reduced transportation and input costs, and improved soil health.
  • For ULBs: Savings on operational subsidies, reduced landfill waste, and decreased methane emissions.
  • For the Environment: Reduced landfill waste, greenhouse gas emissions, and alignment with national missions like SBM and NMNF.

Case Study: Chikkaballapura, Karnataka

In Chikkaballapura, Karnataka, the City-Farmer Partnership for Solid Waste Management (SWM) provided over 200 farmers with 2,300 tonnes of segregated wet waste. This initiative led to the production of 600 tonnes of organic compost, reducing chemical fertilizer use by 50-60 tonnes. Soil health was significantly improved through rigorous testing before and after composting.

Key Enablers

  • Farmer training programs
  • Close monitoring of composting processes
  • Strong collaboration between ULBs, farmers, and local organizations

Challenges and Solutions

Waste Segregation

Challenge: Ensuring effective segregation of wet waste at the source. Solution: Public awareness campaigns and stricter enforcement of segregation norms.

Farmer Training

Challenge: Limited farmer capacity for on-farm composting. Solution: Implementing training programs through self-help groups (SHGs), NGOs, and government agencies.

Coordination Between Stakeholders

Challenge: Ensuring seamless collaboration between ULBs, farmers, and waste processing agencies. Solution: Establishing local partnerships and incentivizing farmer participation.

International Best Practices

Countries like Germany, Japan, Sweden, and Singapore have adopted advanced waste management practices that integrate organic waste for agricultural purposes. Germany focuses on a circular economy for composting, Japan uses decentralized composting techniques, Sweden has a bio-cycle farming model, and Singapore has community composting hubs to manage urban organic waste sustainably.

Way Forward

Policy Recommendations

  • Integrate urban wet waste composting into NMNF to meet the manure demands of farmers.
  • Promote city-farmer partnerships for decentralized composting solutions.
  • Strengthen farmer training programs and waste segregation practices at the source.
  • Provide financial incentives to enhance composting infrastructure on farms.

Conclusion

The National Mission for Natural Farming (NMNF) combined with the City-Farmer Partnership Model represents a progressive approach to solving India's agricultural and environmental issues. By aligning these initiatives with urban waste management, India can support sustainable farming practices while tackling urban waste effectively. However, the success of this mission will depend on collaborative efforts from all stakeholders involved, including government bodies, local organizations, and farmers.
