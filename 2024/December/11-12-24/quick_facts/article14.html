Saurashtra Fossils Say Early Humans Didn’t Stick To Coast

Overview

Recent findings in Saurashtra, India, challenge the theory of coastal migration of early humans, revealing evidence of inland migration instead.

Coastal Migration Theory

This theory suggests early human populations predominantly migrated along coastlines, utilizing maritime routes and coastal resources. Genetic studies support this theory, but archaeological evidence contradicts it.

Findings in Saurashtra

Middle Palaeolithic sites in Saurashtra, India, feature tools made from chert and jasper, dating back to 56,000–48,000 years ago. These tools indicate inland migration, not coastal habitation.

Relative Dating & Landmass Connections

Relative dating of the artefacts shows they belong to the Middle Palaeolithic period, and Saurashtra was part of a connected landmass, distant from coastal areas during this time.

Absence of Marine Evidence

No marine resources like fish or shellfish have been found in these settlements, further suggesting that early humans did not reside along the coast.

Challenges and Reevaluation

The lack of securely dated coastal sites, along with potential submergence of others, complicates proving coastal migration. This study encourages a broader perspective on early human dispersal patterns, emphasizing inland routes over coastal migration.
