Bhutan-India Strategic Partnership: A Geopolitical Overview

Introduction

The visit of Bhutan’s King and Queen to India in December 2023 underscores the enduring strategic and historical partnership between the two countries. This visit, amid growing Chinese pressure and Bhutan’s domestic challenges, highlights the deep-rooted relationship and its geopolitical significance in the region.

Key Takeaways from the Visit

Reaffirmation of India-Bhutan Relations

The meeting reaffirmed the unique and time-tested partnership between India and Bhutan, based on mutual trust and cooperation. India reiterated its commitment to Bhutan’s socio-economic development, including an increase in financial support from ₹5,000 crore to ₹10,000 crore for the 2024-2029 period. Bhutan’s Gelephu Mindfulness City Project, spearheaded by King Jigme Khesar, received Indian backing, further cementing India’s role in Bhutan’s developmental priorities.

Strategic Areas of Cooperation

Two major areas were highlighted: Clean Energy and Hydropower, and Infrastructure Development.

Clean Energy and Hydropower: Bhutan remains crucial to India’s renewable energy strategy, with hydropower playing a key role in Bhutan’s economy and energy exports to India. Strengthened cooperation in this area supports regional energy security.
Infrastructure Development: The visit focused on improving connectivity, underlining Bhutan’s geostrategic importance in the Himalayan region.

Geopolitical Context: China’s Growing Influence

China-Bhutan Border Issues

Bhutan and China have been negotiating their border disputes since 1984. In 2023, they signed a cooperation agreement aimed at expediting the boundary settlement and demarcation. China’s actions in this regard reflect a broader strategy to reduce India’s influence over Bhutan. Territorial disputes, particularly around the Siliguri Corridor, remain of immense strategic importance to New Delhi, as any territorial adjustment could undermine India’s access to its Northeastern states.

Construction of Villages in Disputed Areas

China has constructed villages in disputed areas along Bhutan’s borders. These settlements serve both civilian and military purposes, enabling China to solidify territorial claims by altering ground realities.

Economic Coercion

China has been offering economic incentives to Bhutan, including promoting it as a tourist destination and investing in Bhutan’s telecom sector. This carrot-and-stick approach aims to draw Bhutan closer to Beijing's influence.

India’s Role in Bhutan’s Security and Sovereignty

Strategic Dependence on India

Bhutan’s small military heavily relies on India for training, logistical support, and defense planning. The 2017 Doklam standoff illustrated India’s vital role in protecting Bhutan’s territorial integrity. Indian forces intervened to halt Chinese infrastructure construction in disputed areas.

The Friendship Treaty

The India-Bhutan Friendship Treaty underpins their bilateral relations, ensuring Bhutan’s sovereignty while allowing India to have a crucial role in Bhutan’s foreign and defense policies. This treaty plays a key role in mitigating Chinese pressures on Bhutan.

Challenges for Bhutan

Balancing India and China

Bhutan faces a delicate balancing act between maintaining strong ties with India and managing the growing assertiveness of China. While economic engagement with China offers short-term benefits, Bhutan’s sovereignty and historical ties with India make such engagement complicated.

Domestic Issues

Bhutan also faces domestic challenges, including significant youth migration to countries like Canada and Australia. This outmigration poses long-term challenges to Bhutan’s economy and labor market. Additionally, Bhutan’s economic reliance on hydropower and limited industrial development hinder its diversification.

The Strategic Importance of Bhutan to India

Geopolitical Buffer

Bhutan serves as a critical buffer state between India and China. The sovereignty of Bhutan is essential to India’s strategic stability, particularly in securing the Siliguri Corridor, a vital link to Northeast India.

Hydropower Partnership

Bhutan’s hydropower resources are vital to India’s renewable energy strategy. Continued collaboration in this sector ensures mutual benefits for both nations.

Conclusion

While Bhutan must navigate its complex relationship with China, its deep-rooted ties with India remain the cornerstone of its foreign policy. Bhutan’s sovereignty is essential not only for its own stability but for regional security. New Delhi must continue its proactive engagement with Bhutan, ensuring that Bhutan’s needs are met while countering Chinese influence in the region.
