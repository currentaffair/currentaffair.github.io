One Nation, One Election: Implications for Representative Democracy

Introduction

One Nation, One Election (ONOE) is a proposal aimed at aligning the election cycles of the Lok Sabha and State Assemblies to streamline administrative processes and reduce election fatigue. By fixing the tenure of these bodies and synchronizing their elections, this proposal seeks to make the electoral process more efficient. However, it raises critical questions about the representativeness and inclusivity of the democratic system.

Understanding the Proposal

Synchronization of Elections

The proposal aligns the election timelines of the Lok Sabha and State Assemblies, ensuring that elections are held simultaneously. This aims to reduce the frequency of elections and the associated administrative burden.

Fixed Tenure

Both the Lok Sabha and State Assemblies will have a fixed tenure. In case of early dissolution, mid-term elections will cover only the remainder of the tenure, maintaining consistency in the electoral cycle.

Amendments and Implementation

To implement ONOE, amendments will be made to Articles 83, 172, and 327 of the Indian Constitution. These changes will take effect after the 2029 general elections, marking the beginning of simultaneous elections in 2034.

Union Territories

The Union Territories Laws (Amendment) Bill, 2024, proposes aligning the tenure of Union Territories' legislative assemblies with the Lok Sabha and State Assemblies.

Administrative Efficiency and Election Fatigue

Efficiency Gains

The main aim of ONOE is to reduce the administrative burden of conducting multiple elections and alleviate election fatigue among voters and political stakeholders. The current system, with elections held at different intervals, strains resources and public attention.

Concerns Raised

ONOE raises concerns about the inclusivity and representativeness of the electoral process. By reducing the number of elections, there is a risk that some issues, particularly those affecting specific regions, may not be given adequate attention in national debates.

Representative Democracy: A Deeper Look

Defining Representative Democracy

Representative democracy is a system where citizens elect officials to make decisions on their behalf. The key principles include free and fair elections, political accountability, protection of individual rights, and balancing majority rule with minority interests.

Challenges Facing Representative Democracy

Despite its strengths, representative democracy is facing challenges. A 2024 Pew Research Center study revealed growing disillusionment with democracy across 24 nations, with many citizens feeling their voices are not adequately heard in political decision-making.

Jayaprakash Narayan’s Critique

In his 1959 work “A Plea for Reconstruction of Indian Polity,” Jayaprakash Narayan criticized parliamentary democracy for its defects, such as the risks posed by minority governments, centralization of power, and the financial costs of elections. He warned that these issues could lead to unrepresentative and unstable governments.

Pre-Legislative Consultation Process

Policy and Shortcomings

The Pre-Legislative Consultation Policy of 2014 mandates a minimum 30-day period for public feedback on proposed legislation. However, there are significant shortcomings, such as inadequate consultation periods (only 10 days in some cases) and a lack of explanatory material, leading to a perfunctory approach to public engagement.

Implications for Representative Democracy

Such practices could undermine the core values of representative democracy, especially inclusivity and participation. Curtailing consultation weakens the democratic process and risks sidelining marginalized voices in decision-making.

Implications for Centralisation and Electoral Accountability

Centralization vs. Federalism

ONOE could centralize power, overshadowing the issues and priorities of state governments. This poses a risk to India’s federal structure, as state-specific concerns may not receive the attention they deserve in the national arena.

Electoral Accountability

Simultaneous elections could dilute accountability. With national and state elections occurring together, the responsibility of holding representatives accountable may become more challenging, as local issues might be overshadowed by national politics.

Conclusion

The One Nation, One Election proposal represents a shift towards administrative efficiency but raises several questions regarding the health of representative democracy. A balanced approach is necessary, one that maintains the inclusivity, accountability, and federalism at the heart of India’s democratic system. To ensure the success of such a reform, the process must be carefully crafted to uphold the core principles of democracy while addressing the practical concerns of election fatigue and administrative burden.
