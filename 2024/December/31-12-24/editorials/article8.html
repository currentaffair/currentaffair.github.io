Addressing Market Concentration in India's UPI Ecosystem

Introduction

The Unified Payments Interface (UPI) has transformed digital payments in India, playing a crucial role in the nation’s financial inclusion strategy. However, as UPI adoption accelerates, concerns regarding market concentration have emerged. A duopoly, dominated by two foreign-owned Third Party App Providers (TPAPs), PhonePe and Google Pay, now controls over 85% of the market. This raises several issues regarding competition, innovation, data sovereignty, and systemic risk, which need to be addressed to ensure a healthy and competitive payment ecosystem moving forward.

Risks in the UPI Ecosystem

Systemic Vulnerability

The dominance of PhonePe and Google Pay has created significant concentration within the UPI ecosystem. With these two players handling the majority of transactions, any disruption in their services could have cascading effects across the entire network. A failure of either platform could compromise the resilience of UPI, leading to widespread service outages and undermining the public’s trust in digital payment systems.

Key Issue: UPI’s heavy reliance on PhonePe and Google Pay creates systemic risks, where any disruption could affect millions of transactions across the nation.

Decreased Competition and Innovation

The duopoly of PhonePe and Google Pay has created high entry barriers for smaller competitors, stifling innovation. These platforms primarily compete on user scale, leveraging their massive user bases to cross-sell financial products, rather than focusing on introducing innovative features or improving service offerings. This market consolidation reduces the incentive for smaller players to invest in new technologies, ultimately harming the diversity and innovation within the UPI ecosystem.

Foreign Dominance and Data Concerns

Both PhonePe (owned by Walmart) and Google Pay (owned by Google) are foreign-owned, which raises concerns about the control of critical national infrastructure by non-Indian entities. The dominance of foreign players can lead to potential risks related to data privacy, sensitive information access, and national security, particularly concerning the cross-border flow of data. While foreign participation is beneficial, it is crucial to create a more level playing field for domestic players to mitigate risks related to data sovereignty and protect the interests of Indian users.

Concern: The dominance of foreign-owned TPAPs in the UPI ecosystem raises significant questions about data privacy and control over sensitive financial information.

Regulatory Challenges and Delays

NPCI Directive on Market Share Cap

In 2020, the National Payments Corporation of India (NPCI) introduced a directive aimed at curbing the dominance of PhonePe and Google Pay by setting a market share cap of 30% per TPAP. This measure was intended to foster competition and prevent monopolistic control. However, the deadline for enforcing this cap has been repeatedly extended, allowing both PhonePe and Google Pay to continue their market consolidation. As of August 2024, PhonePe holds 48.36% and Google Pay 37.3%, well above the original cap.

Potential Increase in Market Share Cap

There have been discussions within the NPCI about increasing the market share cap from 30% to 40%. While this might address immediate concerns of market concentration, it could further entrench the dominance of these foreign-owned players. This move could also prevent smaller Indian developers from competing on equal terms, ultimately stalling the broader growth of the UPI ecosystem.

Recommendation: Strict enforcement of the existing market share cap is necessary to promote healthy competition and innovation within the UPI ecosystem.

Policy Recommendations

Enforce Market Share Caps

To reduce systemic risks and promote healthy competition, it is crucial that the NPCI enforces the market share cap without further delays. Strict adherence to this cap will create space for smaller players to grow and innovate, preventing the UPI ecosystem from becoming overly dependent on a few large players.

Encourage Indian-Owned TPAPs

India should focus on fostering domestic TPAPs, providing them with the necessary resources, financial support, and regulatory incentives to scale. By ensuring a more level playing field, Indian developers can better compete against the foreign giants and reduce the risks of foreign dominance in critical national infrastructure.

Safeguard Data Sovereignty

The government must prioritize robust data protection policies to ensure that users’ financial and personal information is kept secure. This includes enforcing stronger regulations around data storage, processing, and cross-border data transfers, particularly for foreign-owned platforms, to safeguard Indian citizens' rights.

Promote Innovation in UPI

To make UPI more inclusive, it is essential to prioritize innovations that cater to underserved segments such as rural populations and small businesses. Developing features like local language support, simplified user interfaces, and alternative payment solutions can help further drive UPI adoption across India, particularly in less accessible regions.

Conclusion

The rapid adoption of UPI has significantly transformed India’s payments landscape, but it has also introduced concerns related to market concentration, innovation, and data sovereignty. To ensure that the benefits of UPI continue to reach all sections of society, India must enforce market share caps, support Indian-owned TPAPs, safeguard data privacy, and promote innovation within the ecosystem. These measures will help maintain a competitive and resilient digital payments infrastructure, securing the future of UPI as a tool for financial inclusion and economic growth.
