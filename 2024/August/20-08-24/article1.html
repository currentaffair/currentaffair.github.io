Political Crisis in Bangladesh: Impact on Indian Investments

Introduction

The political upheaval in Bangladesh, marked by the resignation of former Prime Minister Sheikh Hasina, has generated significant uncertainty. This instability not only impacts the country's domestic affairs but also poses challenges to India, particularly in terms of its investments in Bangladesh. Indian companies, which have established a robust presence in Bangladesh, now face an uncertain future as the political climate shifts.

Impact on Indian Investments

Over the years, Indian firms have invested in multiple sectors within Bangladesh, including edible oils, power, infrastructure, automobiles, and pharmaceuticals. These investments were largely facilitated by the Hasina government, which created favorable conditions for foreign capital, including establishing special economic zones specifically for Indian investors.

Key Concern: The anti-India sentiment, which has been stirred by Hasina's opponents, led to the “India Out” campaign, directly targeting Indian businesses. With the potential for new leadership to adopt policies hostile to Indian interests, existing laws and regulations may change, endangering the future of Indian investments in the country.

Legal Protections for Indian Investors

In times of political instability, legal protections become critical. Jeswald Salacuse outlines three frameworks that govern foreign investments:

  • Domestic laws of the host country
  • Contracts between investors and host governments or companies
  • International law, including treaties and legal principles

Indian companies operating in Bangladesh can leverage the Bangladesh Foreign Private Investment (Promotion and Protection) Act for some protection, but domestic laws are often subject to change. International treaties, particularly Bilateral Investment Treaties (BITs), offer more robust safeguards.

The Role of the India-Bangladesh BIT

India and Bangladesh signed a BIT in 2009, which remains in force despite India’s termination of most of its BITs. This treaty offers significant protection to Indian investors, including protection from expropriation and guarantees of fair and equitable treatment (FET). The BIT also provides the right for investors to pursue dispute resolution through international tribunals, such as the Investor-State Dispute Settlement (ISDS) mechanism.

According to the United Nations Conference on Trade and Development (UNCTAD), 1,332 known ISDS claims had been filed by the end of 2023.

Challenges: Joint Interpretative Notes (JIN)

In 2017, India and Bangladesh adopted Joint Interpretative Notes (JIN) to clarify terms within the 2009 BIT. These notes, however, diluted the protective aspects of the BIT, especially regarding taxation and FET. The JIN was originally designed to shield India’s regulatory sovereignty, but in the context of India as a capital-exporting nation, it inadvertently favors Bangladesh.

Conclusion

The political turmoil in Bangladesh is a test case for India’s broader investment strategy. With outbound foreign direct investment (FDI) reaching $13.5 billion in 2023, India must reconsider its investment treaty practices. As Indian companies continue to expand globally, the need for robust legal frameworks that protect their interests becomes even more crucial. India must balance its roles as both a host and a home country in developing such treaties, ensuring adequate safeguards for its outbound investors.
